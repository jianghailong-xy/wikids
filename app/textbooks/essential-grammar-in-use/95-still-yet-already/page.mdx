Three tiny time-words live around the present and the present perfect: **still**, **yet**, and **already**. Each one tells a different story about a single moment.

- **still** = it was true before, and it's *still* true now.
- **yet** = it hasn't happened *until now* (but I expect it to).
- **already** = it happened *earlier than expected*.

Same scene, three angles — pick the right word and the sentence almost writes itself.

## #1 — still = the same as before

**still** points to a situation that **started in the past and is still going on now**. Nothing has changed.

It usually goes in the **middle** of the sentence: **before** an ordinary verb, **after** *am/is/are/was/were*, and **between** a helper and the main verb.

| Before | Now | With *still* |
|---|---|---|
| An hour ago it was raining. | It's raining. | It is **still** raining. |
| I was hungry. | I'm hungry. | I'm **still** hungry. |
| She lived in Barcelona. | She lives in Barcelona. | She **still** lives in Barcelona. |
| He was looking for a job. | He's looking for a job. | He's **still** looking for a job. |

<SayIt text="It is still raining." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I'm still hungry." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She still lives in Barcelona." />

More examples:

- I had a lot to eat, but I'm **still** hungry. *(I was hungry, and I'm hungry now)*
- 'Did you sell your car?' 'No, I've **still** got it.'
- 'Do you **still** live in Barcelona?' 'No, I live in Madrid now.'
- It happened a long time ago, but I can **still** remember it clearly.

<Callout type="info" title="Where does *still* sit?">
  • Before an ordinary verb — *I **still** live here.* <br />
  • After **am/is/are/was/were** — *She **is still** in bed.* <br />
  • Between two verbs — *I have **still** got it.* / *He has **still** not arrived.*
</Callout>

## #2 — yet = until now (only "no" or "?")

**yet** is the *waiting word*. It points to something that **hasn't happened so far**, but you expect it to happen — soon, maybe today, maybe later.

Two strict rules for **yet**:
1. Use it **only in negatives and questions** — never in plain positive statements.
2. **yet** usually goes at **the very end** of the sentence.

### Yet in negatives — *hasn't ... yet*

| Setup | Sentence with *yet* |
|---|---|
| Are Laura and Paul here? | No, they **haven't arrived yet**. |
| Where's Emma? | She **isn't here yet**. |
| What are you doing this evening? | I **don't know yet**. |
| Sam bought new shoes a week ago. | He **hasn't worn them yet**. |

<SayIt text="They haven't arrived yet." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She isn't here yet." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I don't know yet." />

### Yet in questions — *Have you ... yet?*

| Question | Possible reply |
|---|---|
| **Are** you ready to go **yet**? | Not yet. In a minute. |
| **Has** Ben come **yet**? | No, he's very late. |
| **Have** you finished with the newspaper **yet**? | No, I'm still reading it. |
| **Has** the bus left **yet**? | No, two more minutes. |

<SayIt text="Are you ready yet?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Has Ben come yet?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Have you finished with the newspaper yet?" />

<Callout type="warning" title="No *yet* in a positive statement">
  ✅ I **haven't** finished **yet**. &nbsp; ✅ Have you finished **yet**? <br />
  ❌ I have finished **yet**. &nbsp; *(if it's done, switch to **already**!)* <br />
  Yet only lives with **n't** or with a **question mark**.
</Callout>

## #3 — already = earlier than expected

**already** carries a small surprise: the action happened **sooner than you (or somebody else) thought**.

Like **still**, it usually sits in the **middle** of the sentence — between *have/has* and the past participle, after *am/is/are*, or before an ordinary verb.

| Setup | Reply with *already* |
|---|---|
| What time is Joe coming? | He's **already** here. *(earlier than expected)* |
| I'm going to tell you what happened. | That's not necessary. I **already** know. |
| Aren't you coming to the cinema? | No — I've **already** seen the film. |
| Don't forget to phone Sarah. | I've **already** phoned her. |

<SayIt text="He's already here." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I already know." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I've already phoned her." />

<Callout type="tip" title="The *already* face">
  When you say **already**, your eyebrows go up a little — there's a hint of surprise. *"You've **already** finished?"* You expected them to still be working.
</Callout>
