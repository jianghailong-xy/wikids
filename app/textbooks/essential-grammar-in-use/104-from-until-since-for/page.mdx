## Part A — *from … to* (or *from … until*)

Both ends marked. The default partner of *from* is **to**, but **until** also works.

- We lived in Japan **from 2003 to 2010**.
- I work **from Monday to Friday**.
- We lived in Japan **from 2003 until 2010**.

  </div>
  <p className="mt-2 text-center text-xs italic text-sky-700">from Monday to Friday</p>
</div>

## Part B — *until* on its own

When you only need to mark **the end**, use **until** (or its everyday twin **till**).

| until + word | sentence |
|---|---|
| Friday | They're going away tomorrow. They'll be away **until Friday**. |
| 3 o'clock | I read a book **until 3 o'clock**. |
| I come back | Wait here **until I come back**. |

You can also say **till**: *Wait here **till** I come back*. (Same meaning, more spoken.)

<Callout type="info" title="Compare *until* and *on*">
  **How long** will you be away? — **Until** Monday. *(stretch of time)* <br />
  **When** are you coming back? — **On** Monday. *(a single day)*
</Callout>

<SayIt text="They'll be away until Friday." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Wait here till I come back." />

## Part C — *since* + a starting point in the past

**since** points to a moment in the past — and the situation is still true now. The verb almost always sits in the **present perfect** (*have been / have done*).

- Joe is in hospital. He has been in hospital **since Monday**. *(= from Monday to now)*
- Sue and Dave have been married **since 1998**. *(= from 1998 to now)*
- It has been raining **since I arrived**.

    <span>Monday</span>
    <span className="flex-1 mx-3 h-2 rounded-full bg-emerald-400" />
    <span>now</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-2 text-center text-xs italic text-emerald-700">since Monday — and still going</p>
</div>

### Compare the trio

- We **lived** in Japan **from 2003 to 2010**. *(both ends — past simple)*
- We **lived** in Japan **until 2010**. *(only the end — past simple)*
- Now we live in Canada. We came to Canada in 2010. We **have lived** in Canada **since 2010**. *(starting point + still here — present perfect)*

## Part D — *for* + a length of time

**for** answers *how long?* with a pure stretch — three days, ten years, five minutes, a long time. No starting point, no ending — just the **length**.

- Gary stayed with us **for three days**.
- I'm going away **for a few weeks**.
- I'm going away **for the weekend**.
- They've been married **for ten years**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 flex items-center gap-3 rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5">
  <span className="text-sm font-semibold text-rose-900">Sunday</span>
  <span className="flex-1 h-2 rounded-full bg-rose-300" />
  <span className="text-sm font-semibold text-rose-900">Monday</span>
  <span className="flex-1 h-2 rounded-full bg-rose-300" />
  <span className="text-sm font-semibold text-rose-900">Tuesday</span>
</div>
<p className="-mt-3 mb-3 text-center text-xs italic text-rose-700">for three days</p>

<Callout type="warning" title="Never *since three days*">
  ❌ *Joe has been in hospital **since three days**.* <br />
  ✅ *Joe has been in hospital **for three days**.* <br />
  *since* takes a **moment** (Monday, 1998, I arrived). *for* takes a **length** (three days, ten years).
</Callout>

<SayIt text="They've been married for ten years." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I'm going away for a few weeks." />

## Now you try

Finish each sentence about your own life. Mind *since* (a moment) vs *for* (a length).

1. *I have lived in this city **since** ___ .*
2. *I have lived in this city **for** ___ .*
3. *Last weekend I was busy **from** ___ **to** ___ .*
4. *I'm going to study **until** ___ .*

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  **from … to / until** mark the **end** of a stretch. <br />
  **since** + a moment in the past + present perfect *(since 1998, since I arrived)*. <br />
  **for** + a length of time *(for three days, for ten years)* — never *since three days*.
</Callout>
