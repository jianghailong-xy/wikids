When a friend asks *"What should I do?"*, you reach for one tiny word: **should**. It's the friendly way to share an opinion — *that's a good idea*, *that's not a good idea* — without bossing anyone around. Today you'll learn the shape (**should** + infinitive), the negative (**shouldn't**), the polite opener (**I think ... should ...**), and how it differs from its bigger cousin **must**.

## The shape: should + infinitive

**Should** is a *modal verb* — it doesn't change for he/she/it, and the verb after it is the plain **infinitive** (no *to*, no *-s*, no *-ing*).

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-violet-50 to-purple-50 shadow-sm">
  <div className="bg-violet-100 px-5 py-3 text-center text-sm font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-800">
    subject + should / shouldn't + infinitive
  </div>
  <div className="grid grid-cols-3 gap-px bg-violet-200 text-center text-base">
    <div className="bg-white px-3 py-3 font-semibold text-slate-700">I / we / you / they<br />he / she / it</div>
    <div className="bg-white px-3 py-3 font-bold text-violet-900">should<br />shouldn't</div>
    <div className="bg-white px-3 py-3 font-semibold text-slate-700">do<br />stop<br />go<br />watch …</div>
  </div>
</div>

| ✅ Right | ❌ Wrong |
|---|---|
| He **should study** harder. | He should ~~studies~~ / ~~to study~~ harder. |
| You **should go** to bed. | You should ~~going~~ / ~~goes~~ to bed. |
| She **shouldn't watch** TV so much. | She shouldn't ~~watches~~ / ~~watching~~ TV so much. |

<SayIt text="He should study harder." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="You should go to bed." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She shouldn't watch TV so much." />

<Callout type="info" title="No -s, no to, no -ing">
  After **should**, the verb stays in its plainest form: **go**, **eat**, **buy**, **watch**. <br />
  ✅ He should **go**. &nbsp; ❌ He should **goes** / **to go** / **going**.
</Callout>

## should = it's a good idea

**You should do something** = *it is a good thing to do, it is the right thing to do*.

- Tom doesn't study enough. He **should study** harder.
- It's a good film. You **should go** and see it.
- When you play tennis, you **should** always **watch** the ball.

<SayIt text="He should study harder." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="You should go and see it." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="You should always watch the ball." />

## shouldn't = it's not a good idea

**You shouldn't do something** = *it is not a good thing to do*. **Shouldn't** = *should not*.

- Tom **shouldn't go** to bed so late.
- You watch TV all the time. You **shouldn't watch** TV so much.

<SayIt text="Tom shouldn't go to bed so late." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="You shouldn't watch TV so much." />

## I think ... should ... (the polite opener)

In real conversation, English speakers rarely shoot a bare *"You should …"* across the table. They cushion it with **I think** — it sounds like an opinion, not an order.

### I think ... should ...

- **I think** Lisa **should buy** some new clothes. *(= I think it's a good idea.)*
- It's late. **I think** I **should go** home now.
- A: Shall I buy this coat? &nbsp; B: Yes, **I think you should**.

<SayIt text="I think Lisa should buy some new clothes." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I think I should go home now." />

### I don't think ... should ...

To say the opposite, the **don't** jumps onto **think**, not onto *should*:

- **I don't think** you **should work** so hard. *(= I don't think it's a good idea.)*
- **I don't think** we **should go** yet. It's too early.

<SayIt text="I don't think you should work so hard." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I don't think we should go yet." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Where does 'not' go?">
  Move the negative **onto think**, not onto *should*: <br />
  ✅ I **don't think** you **should** work so hard. <br />
  ❌ I think you **shouldn't** work so hard. <em>(grammatical, but not what English speakers usually say)</em>
</Callout>

### Do you think ... should ... ?

Asking for advice? Flip **do you think** to the front and put **should** after the subject:

- **Do you think** I **should buy** this hat?
- What time **do you think** we **should go** home?

<SayIt text="Do you think I should buy this hat?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What time do you think we should go home?" />

## must vs should — strength dial

Both **must** and **should** give advice, but **must** is the loud cousin. **Should** says *good idea*; **must** says *you really need to*.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-yellow-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">should — gentle</p>
    <p className="text-center text-sm text-slate-700">a <strong>good</strong> film</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-center text-base font-semibold text-amber-900">You <strong>should</strong> go and see it.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-red-300 bg-gradient-to-br from-red-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-red-700">must — strong</p>
    <p className="text-center text-sm text-slate-700">a <strong>fantastic</strong> film</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-center text-base font-semibold text-red-900">You <strong>must</strong> go and see it!</p>
  </div>
</div>

<SayIt text="It's a good film. You should go and see it." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's a fantastic film. You must go and see it." />

<Callout type="tip" title="Pick by feeling">
  Is it just *a nice idea*? → **should**. Is it almost *an emergency, a once-in-a-lifetime, a rule*? → **must**.
</Callout>

## ought to — should's bookish twin

Another way to say **should** is **ought to** (notice the **to**). The meaning is the same; **ought to** just sounds a bit more formal or old-school.

- It's a good film. You **ought to go** and see it. *(= you should go)*
- I think Lisa **ought to buy** some new clothes. *(= Lisa should buy)*

<SayIt text="You ought to go and see it." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Lisa ought to buy some new clothes." />

<Callout type="info" title="Pronunciation hint">
  Most people say **ought to** quickly, almost like *"otta"*. *I ought to call her* sounds like *I otta call her*.
</Callout>
