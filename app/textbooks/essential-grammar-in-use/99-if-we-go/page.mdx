**if** is the *maybe* word. It opens a sentence about a **possible future** — something that might or might not happen.

> **If we go by bus**, it'll be cheaper. *(maybe yes, maybe no)*

Two big rules to learn today:

1. **No *will* in the *if*-half.** Just like *when*, the *if*-half stays in the **present** — even when the meaning is future. *If you **see** Ann tomorrow…* (not *if you will see*).
2. ***if* vs *when*** — *if* = maybe (I'm not sure); *when* = sure (it will happen).

Same word-order trick as Unit 98, with one extra twist.

## Part A — *if* at the start, or in the middle

Just like *when*, the *if*-half can sit at the **beginning** or in the **middle** of the sentence:

    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">It will be cheaper **if we go by bus**.</p>

In conversation, the *if*-half often stands **alone** as a short answer:

- A: 'Are you going to the concert?' &nbsp;B: 'Yes, **if I can get a ticket**.'
- A: 'Will you come tomorrow?' &nbsp;B: 'Maybe — **if I have time**.'

## Part B — after *if*, use the **present** (not *will*)

The biggest *if*-trap. Even when the meaning is future, the *if*-half stays in the **present**:

> **If you see Ann tomorrow**, can you ask her to call me? <br />
> *(not If you will see Ann tomorrow)*

More examples:

- **If I'm late this evening**, don't wait for me. *(not if I will be)*
- What shall we do **if it rains**? *(not if it will rain)*
- **If I don't feel** well tomorrow, I'll stay at home.
- I'll be very surprised **if Michael and Jane get married**. *(not if … will get)*

The future tense (*will / 'll*) goes in the **other** half:

| if-half *(present)* | + | main half *(future is fine)* |
|---|---|---|
| If you don't hurry, | | you'll miss the train. |
| If we go by bus, | | it'll be cheaper. |
| If I see Ann, | | I'll tell her. |

<Callout type="warning" title="The locked rule">
  After **if**, never use **will**. Use the **present** — even if the meaning is future.<br />
  ✅ *If it **rains** tomorrow…* &nbsp; ❌ *If it **will rain** tomorrow…*
</Callout>

## Part C — *if* vs *when*

Both glue words attach a time-half to a main half — but they say something **very different** about how sure you are.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">if = maybe</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900">A: Are you going out later? <br />B: Maybe. **If I go out**, I'll close the windows.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm italic text-slate-600">= it's possible I'll go out, but I'm not sure.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">when = sure</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">A: Are you going out later? <br />B: Yes, I am. **When I go out**, I'll close the windows.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm italic text-slate-600">= it's certain — I'm going out for sure.</p>
  </div>
</div>

Compare:

- **When I get home this evening**, I'm going to have a shower. *(I'm definitely getting home)*
- **If I'm late this evening**, don't wait for me. *(maybe I will be, maybe I won't)*
- We're going to play tennis **if it doesn't rain**. *(not when it doesn't rain)*

<Callout type="info" title="Quick test">
  Could you replace it with *every time* without changing the meaning? <br />
  Yes → **when**. &nbsp; No → **if**. <br />
  *When it rains, the road floods* (every time). &nbsp; *If it rains tomorrow, we'll stay home* (maybe).
</Callout>

## Now you try — six *if*-sentences

Say each one out loud about your own life.

1. *If I have time tomorrow, I'll ___ .*
2. *We can go to the beach tomorrow if ___ .*
3. *If I see ___ , I'll ___ .*
4. *I'll be surprised if ___ .*
5. *If it rains, ___ .*
6. *Is it OK if I ___ ?* — a polite question.

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — tomorrow's plan B">
  Pick three things you might do tomorrow. For each, say two halves: *If + present, … will/can/might + verb.* <br />
  *If the weather is good, I'll go for a run. If I have time, I'll call my mum. If it rains, we can stay in and watch a film.* <br />
  Three plan-Bs, all in present + future. Easy.
