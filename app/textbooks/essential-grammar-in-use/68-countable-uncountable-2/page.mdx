Lesson 67 split English nouns into two camps: **countable** (a car, an apple) and **uncountable** (water, money, music). Today we plug them into the article system: **a/an** for one countable thing, **some** for plurals and uncountables. Then we meet the *double-life* nouns (*a cake / some cake, a chicken / some chicken, a paper / some paper*) — same noun, two meanings — and the **always-uncountable trap**: *advice, bread, furniture, hair, information, news, weather, work*. These look easy but catch every learner: **never** *an advice, two informations, a bread*.

### Compare *a* and *some*

- Nicola bought **a hat, some shoes** and **some perfume**.
- I read **a newspaper**, made **some phone calls**, and listened to **some music**.

## Part B — Double-life nouns

Many nouns can be **both**. The countable form names *one whole thing*; the uncountable form names *the stuff inside*. Same word — different picture.

### Compare *a paper* and *some paper*

- I want something to read. I'm going to buy **a paper**. *(= a newspaper)*
- I want to make a shopping list. I need **some paper / a piece of paper**. *(not a paper)*

## Part C — Always-uncountable nouns (the trap list)

These nouns **look** countable to learners — but in English they aren't. **No *a/an*. No plural.** Burn them in:

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50">
  <div className="bg-rose-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-900">always uncountable — never <em>a/an</em>, never plural</div>
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-rose-200 sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-4">
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">advice</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">bread</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">furniture</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">hair</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">information</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">news</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">weather</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-rose-900">work</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- Can I talk to you? I need **some advice**. *(not an advice)*
- I'm going to buy **some bread**. *(not a bread)*
- They've got **some nice furniture**. *(not furnitures)*
- Silvia has very long **hair**. *(not hairs)*
- Where can I get **some information** about hotels? *(not informations)*
- Listen! I've just had **some good news**. *(not a good news)*
- It's nice **weather** today. *(not a nice weather)*
- 'Do you like your job?' 'Yes, but it's hard **work**.' *(not a hard work)*

### Trap inside the trap — *a job* (but not *a work*)

- I've got **a new job**. *(not a new work — but: it's a lot of **work**, hard **work**, etc.)*

<Callout type="warning" title="The newspaper test">
  Stuck? *News* doesn't look uncountable — but it is. So is *advice*. If you can put it in a phrase like ***some good ___***, it's uncountable: *some good news, some good advice*. Then never let *a* near it again.
</Callout>

## Now you try

Write **six** true sentences. Use *a/an* in two, *some + plural* in two, *some + uncountable* in two.

1. *I've got a/an ___ .*
2. *I'm going to buy a/an ___ tomorrow.*
3. *I have some ___ at home.* (plural countable)
4. *I bought some ___ at the supermarket.* (plural countable)
5. *Can I have some ___ , please?* (uncountable — pick one from the trap list!)
6. *I need some ___ about ___ .* (e.g., information / advice)

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — the trap list, in real sentences">
  Write **eight** true sentences, one for each trap-list noun: *advice, bread, furniture, hair, information, news, weather, work*. Each sentence must be **at least seven words long** and **never** put *a/an* in front of these nouns. Bonus: in each sentence, pair the noun with *some*, *much*, or an adjective like *good / hard / nice*. <br /><br /> Example: *I need some good advice about my new job.*
</Callout>
