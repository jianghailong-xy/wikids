In Lesson 3 you learned to talk about what's happening **right now** with **am/is/are + -ing**. Since then you've also met the **present simple** — *I do, he does* — for the things we do **all the time**. This lesson is the showdown: same verb, two different worlds. We'll learn how to pick the right one.

## Two tenses, two timelines

English has two ways to say "what someone does", and they live on different parts of the timeline.

    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">Present continuous</p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-2xl font-bold text-emerald-900">I'm doing</p>

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">Present simple</p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-2xl font-bold text-amber-900">I do</p>
    <div className="mt-4 flex items-center justify-between text-[10px] font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-slate-500">

    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-amber-900">= **in general**, all the time, or sometimes.</p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-sm italic text-amber-800">"I work from 9 to 5." "It rains a lot in winter."</p>

<Callout type="info" title="The decision rule">
  Before you speak, ask yourself one question: <br />
  **Is this happening at this very moment?** <br />
  • **Yes →** use **am/is/are + -ing** (present continuous). <br />
  • **No, it's just true in general →** use **I do / he does** (present simple).
</Callout>

## Same verb, two stories

Watch how the same verb changes its job depending on the timeline:

| In general (present simple) | Right now (present continuous) |
|---|---|
| Jack **plays** the guitar. | Jack **is not playing** the guitar. |
| Tom **has** a shower every morning. | Tom **is having** a shower at the moment. |
| It **rains** a lot in winter. | Look — it'**s raining**. |
| I **don't watch** TV very often. | You can turn off the TV. I'**m not watching** it. |
| What **do** you usually **do** at weekends? | Why are you under the table? What **are** you **doing**? |

## Saying "no" — two different helpers

The negative looks different in each tense, because they use **different helper words**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-4 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">Right now</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900"><strong>am/is/are</strong> + <strong>not</strong> + verb-ing</p>
    <ul className="mt-2 space-y-1 text-sm text-emerald-900">
      <li>• I'<strong>m not</strong> watch<strong>ing</strong> it.</li>
      <li>• She <strong>isn't</strong> work<strong>ing</strong> today.</li>
      <li>• They <strong>aren't</strong> listen<strong>ing</strong>.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">In general</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900"><strong>don't / doesn't</strong> + verb</p>
    <ul className="mt-2 space-y-1 text-sm text-amber-900">
      <li>• I <strong>don't watch</strong> TV very often.</li>
      <li>• She <strong>doesn't work</strong> on Sundays.</li>
      <li>• They <strong>don't listen</strong> to the radio.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't mix the helpers">
  ✅ I'**m not watching** it. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I don't watching it. <br />
  ✅ She **doesn't work** today. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ She isn't work today.
</Callout>

## Asking questions — two different helpers

Same idea: each tense has its own helper word at the front.

| Right now (continuous) | In general (simple) |
|---|---|
| **Is** he **playing** the guitar? | **Does** he **play** the guitar? |
| **Are** you **working** right now? | **Do** you **work** on Saturdays? |
| **What is** she **doing**? | **What does** she **do**? |
| **Why are** you **laughing**? | **Why do** you laugh at his jokes? |

Tap to listen:

<SayIt text="Is he playing the guitar?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Does he play the guitar?" />

<Callout type="tip" title="Mini-trick">
  Hear the **-ing** at the end? That's continuous. <br />
  Hear **do / does** at the front? That's simple.
</Callout>

## Special verbs that never take -ing

A small group of verbs are about **what's in your head or your heart** — feelings, opinions, knowledge. We don't use them in the present continuous, even when we mean "right now". They always stay in the simple form.

<div className="not-prose my-4 rounded-xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5">
  <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">No -ing for these:</p>
  <div className="mt-3 grid grid-cols-2 gap-x-4 gap-y-1 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900 sm:grid-cols-5">
    <span>like</span>
    <span>want</span>
    <span>know</span>
    <span>understand</span>
    <span>remember</span>
    <span>prefer</span>
    <span>need</span>
    <span>mean</span>
    <span>believe</span>
    <span>forget</span>
  </div>
</div>

So even when you're tired **right now**, you say:

- ✅ I'm tired. I **want** to go home. &nbsp; ❌ I'm wanting to go home.
- ✅ "Do you **know** that girl?" "Yes, but I **don't remember** her name."
- ✅ I **don't understand**. What **do** you **mean**?

<SayIt text="I want to go home." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I don't understand. What do you mean?" />

<Callout type="info" title="Why no -ing?">
  These verbs describe a **state**, not an action. You can't watch someone "knowing" or "wanting" — there's nothing to point a camera at. So English keeps them simple.
</Callout>
