In lesson 43 you saw that to say **no**, you find the helping verb and snap *n't* onto it. Today's recipe is the twin: to ask **a yes/no question**, you find the same little helper and **flip it to the front** — in front of the subject. *You are eating* swaps to *Are you eating?* Same words; new word order. And when there's no visible helper (present or past simple), **do / does / did** shows up to be one — exactly like in lesson 43.

## The big idea — flip the helper to the front

Find the helper running the sentence. To ask a yes/no question, just put it **before** the subject — like passing the ball to the front of the line.

## The trap — the subject sits **right after** the first verb

The most common mistake is letting the subject drift to the end of the sentence. Don't. The subject has to come **right behind** the helper.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">❌ subject at the end</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900 line-through">Where has gone David?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900 line-through">Are waiting those people for something?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900 line-through">When was invented the telephone?</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">✅ subject right after the helper</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Where <strong>has David</strong> gone?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900"><strong>Are those people</strong> waiting for something?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">When <strong>was the telephone</strong> invented?</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Three slots, one rule">
  Wh-word (optional) → **helper** → **subject** → rest of the sentence.<br />
  *Where* → *has* → *David* → *gone?*<br /><br />
  Don't let *David* slide to the back. The subject has to sit right next to its helper.
</Callout>

## "Hidden helpers" — present and past simple

Just like in lesson 43, *I work, she likes, we played* don't show their helper. The moment you ask a question, **do / does / did** has to step in. They sit at the front, and the main verb goes back to its **plain** shape.

### Watch what happens to the verb

Once *do/does/did* shows up, the main verb goes plain — drop the *-s*, drop the *-ed*. The helper carries the tense; the verb stays bare.

<Callout type="warning" title="The double-tense trap (again)">
  Once *does* or *did* shows up, the main verb goes <strong>plain</strong>. <br />
  ❌ Does Chris <span className="line-through">works</span> hard? &nbsp;✅ Does Chris **work** hard?<br />
  ❌ What did you <span className="line-through">had</span> for dinner? &nbsp;✅ What did you **have** for dinner?<br /><br />
  Same rule as in negatives — the helper already carries the tense. The verb stops trying.
</Callout>

## Bonus 1 — when *do* is the **main** verb

Sometimes English doubles up: **do you do? does he do? did you do?** The first one is the helper; the second is the action verb meaning *to do something*.

- What **do** you usually **do** at weekends? <SayIt text="What do you usually do at weekends?" iconOnly />
- A: What **does** your brother **do**? &nbsp; B: He works in a bank. <SayIt text="What does your brother do?" iconOnly />
- A: I broke my finger last week. &nbsp; B: How **did** you **do** that? <SayIt text="How did you do that?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Don't drop the second do">
  ❌ How did you that? &nbsp; ✅ How **did** you **do** that?<br /><br />
  Two *do*'s, two jobs: the first is the helper carrying the tense, the second is the action verb. Both have to be there.
</Callout>

## Bonus 2 — *Why isn't…? / Why don't…?* (Why + negative)

You can also flip a **negative** to ask why something *isn't* happening. The negative helper (with *n't* already attached) goes to the front of the subject.

- Where's John? **Why isn't he** here? <SayIt text="Why isn't he here?" iconOnly />
- **Why can't Paula** come to the meeting tomorrow? <SayIt text="Why can't Paula come to the meeting tomorrow?" iconOnly />
- **Why didn't you** phone me last night? <SayIt text="Why didn't you phone me last night?" iconOnly />

## Now you try: interview a friend

Pick a friend (or imagine one) and ask **eight** questions about their day. Try to use a **different helper** in each one. Aim to hit all of these:

1. **am / is / are** → *Are you …?*
2. **was / were** → *Were you …?*
3. **have / has** → *Have you …?*
4. **do / does** → *Do you …?* / *Does she …?*
5. **did** → *Did you …?*
6. **can / could** → *Can you …?*
7. **will** → *Will you …?*
8. **Why + negative** → *Why didn't you …?* / *Why aren't you …?*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — twelve questions, two minutes">
  Race the clock. In **two minutes**, write twelve real questions you'd actually ask a new friend on the first day. Use a **different helper** in each one. <br /><br />
  *Example:* "What's your name? Where are you from? How long have you lived here? Do you like spicy food? Did you grow up in a big city? Can you cook? What time do you usually get up? Are you working tomorrow? Will you be free this weekend? Have you ever been to Japan? Why didn't you come to the party last week? What does your sister do?"<br /><br />
  Twelve helpers, twelve flips — every English question shape, in your pocket.
</Callout>
