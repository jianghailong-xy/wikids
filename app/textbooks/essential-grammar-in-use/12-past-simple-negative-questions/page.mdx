## The big rule: **did** is the past-tense helper

You already know two helpers from the present: **do** and **does**. In the past, those two collapse into a **single** helper for everyone. One word. No exceptions.

Watch the same idea travel from **today** to **yesterday**:

| Now (present) | Then (past) |
|---|---|
| I **don't** watch TV very often. | I **didn't** watch TV yesterday. |
| **Does** she often go away? | **Did** she go away last week? |
| **Do** they live near here? | **Did** they live here last year? |
| He **doesn't** eat meat. | He **didn't** eat meat at the party. |

## The big trap: only **one** word carries the past

This is the rule that trips everyone up. Once **did** or **didn't** shows up, the main verb goes back to its **bare dictionary form** — no *-ed*, no *went*, no *saw*. The past has already been spoken.

> **did / didn't** + **plain verb** &nbsp;(not verb-**ed**, not the irregular past)

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <div className="grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
    <div>
      <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">Statement</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-xl font-semibold text-slate-800">
        They <span className="underline decoration-rose-400 decoration-2 underline-offset-4">went</span> to Paris.
      </p>
    </div>
    <div>
      <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">Question</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-xl font-semibold text-slate-800">
        <span className="rounded bg-amber-200 px-1.5">Did</span> they <span className="underline decoration-emerald-500 decoration-2 underline-offset-4">go</span> to Paris?
      </p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-4 text-center text-sm text-slate-600">
    The past tense jumped out of <strong>went</strong> and into <strong>did</strong>. Only one of them carries it — never both.
  </p>
</div>

A few before-and-afters to lock it in:

| Positive | Negative | Question |
|---|---|---|
| I **watched** | I **didn't watch** &nbsp;*(not didn't watched)* | **did** I **watch**? |
| they **went** | they **didn't go** | **did** they **go**? &nbsp;*(not did they went?)* |
| he **had** | he **didn't have** | **did** he **have**? |
| you **did** | you **didn't do** | **did** you **do**? |

<Callout type="warning" title="Pass the baton">
  The **past-ness** of the sentence has already moved into **did**. The verb behind it is just the bare form. <br />
  ✅ I **didn't watch** TV. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I didn't ~~watched~~ TV. <br />
  ✅ **Did** they **go**? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ ~~Did they went?~~ <br />
  Only **one** word at a time gets to be in the past — like a relay baton.
</Callout>

Listen and compare:

## Saying "no" — **didn't**

Same shrinking trick you've used since Lesson 1. Drop a letter, swap an apostrophe in. **Did not** becomes **didn't** — and there's only one form, for everyone.

<Contraction
  caption="did not → didn't"
  segments={[
    { keep: "did" },
    { drop: " " },
    { keep: "n" },
    { swap: "o", to: "'" },
    { keep: "t" },
  ]}
/>

<Callout type="info" title="One past 'no' for everyone">
  In the present you had to choose: **don't** or **doesn't**? In the past, that choice disappears. **I didn't, you didn't, he didn't, they didn't** — all the same. Easier!
</Callout>

- **'Did you do** the shopping?' — '**No, I didn't have** time.' <SayIt text="Did you do the shopping? No, I didn't have time." iconOnly />
- I **played** tennis yesterday, but I **didn't win**. <SayIt text="I played tennis yesterday, but I didn't win." iconOnly />
- We **went** to the cinema, but we **didn't enjoy** the film. <SayIt text="We went to the cinema, but we didn't enjoy the film." iconOnly />

## Asking questions: **did** goes to the front

There's no flipping in the past — same trick as **do/does** in the present. We **glue did in front** of the subject, and the verb stays in its plain form.

<WordFlip
  subject="You"
  verb="watched"
  rest="TV last night"
  caption="You watched TV last night. → Did you watch TV last night?"
/>

<WordFlip
  subject="They"
  verb="went"
  rest="to Paris"
  caption="They went to Paris. → Did they go to Paris?"
/>

<Callout type="info" title="The past question pattern">
  **Statement:** subject + **past verb** + ... &nbsp;&nbsp; → &nbsp;&nbsp; **Question:** **did** + subject + **plain verb** + ... ?
</Callout>

And just like before, the wh-words sit out in front:

| **wh-word** | **did** | **subject** | **plain verb** | **rest** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | **Did** | your sister | **call** | you? |
| **What** | **did** | you | **do** | last night? |
| **How** | **did** | the accident | **happen**? | |
| **Where** | **did** | your parents | **go** | for their holiday? |

## Short answers — bring the helper along

Just like with **be** and with **do/does**, a polite yes or no in English brings the helper word along. In the past, that helper is always **did** (or **didn't**).

| Question | Yes | No |
|---|---|---|
| **Did** you see Joe yesterday? | **Yes, I did.** | **No, I didn't.** |
| **Did** it rain on Sunday? | **Yes, it did.** | **No, it didn't.** |
| **Did** Helen come to the party? | **Yes, she did.** | **No, she didn't.** |
| **Did** your parents have a good holiday? | **Yes, they did.** | **No, they didn't.** |

<Callout type="tip" title="Mirror, mirror">
  The pronoun in your answer **mirrors the subject of your answer**, not the question. *"**Did you** see Joe?"* → *"**No, I didn't**."* (you turns into I!)
</Callout>

<SayIt text="Did you see Joe yesterday? No, I didn't." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Did it rain on Sunday? Yes, it did." />
