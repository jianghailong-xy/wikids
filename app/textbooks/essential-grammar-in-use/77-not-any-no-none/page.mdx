There are three ways to say *zero* in English. **not + any** with a negative verb (*There **aren't any** cars*). **no + noun** with a positive verb (*There are **no** cars*). And **none** on its own (the noun-less twin) — for answering *How much?* or *How many?* (*— **None**.*). Same idea, three shapes — and the trap is **never two negatives at once**.

## Part A — *not + any* and *no + noun*

    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">Tracey and Jack <strong>don't have any children</strong>.</p>

    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">no + noun — positive verb</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">There <strong>are no cars</strong> in the car park.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">We <strong>have no coffee</strong>.</p>

<Callout type="warning" title="Never both at once">
  ❌ *They don't have no children.* <br /> ✅ They <strong>don't have any</strong> children. *or* They <strong>have no</strong> children. <br /> One negative is enough — pick a side.
</Callout>

## Part B — *no* and *none*

- Use **no + noun**: *We have **no money**. There were **no problems**.*
- Use **none alone** (no noun): *'How much money do you have?' '**None**.' (= no money). 'Were there any problems?' 'No, **none**.'*

## Now you try

Write **six** zero-sentences. Use each shape at least once.

1. *Today there is/are no ___ .*
2. *I don't have any ___ .*
3. *How many ___ do you have? — None.*
4. *Who came to the party? — No-one.*
5. *There weren't any ___ in the shop.*
6. *We have no ___ at the moment.*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — one negative only">
  Take five English sentences you've written this week. Underline every negative word: *not, no, none, never, nobody, nothing*. Make sure each sentence has **only one** of these. *I don't know nothing* sounds wrong; *I don't know anything* / *I know nothing* are both right.
