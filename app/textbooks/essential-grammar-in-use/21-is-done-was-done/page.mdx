So far every sentence you've made has been **active** — somebody **does** something: *Lisa cleaned the office*. Today we learn the flip side — the **passive**: *the office was cleaned*. The thing that *gets done to* hops to the front, and the doer either rides along behind **by** … or quietly disappears. It's how English talks about products, places, and famous events when the doer is everyone, nobody, or beside the point.

## Mia tours a chocolate factory

Mia is on a school field trip to an old chocolate factory. She's texting Zoe between stops. Listen to how the guide and the signs talk — they don't say *who* does the work, only what **is done** and what **was done**. Tap each bubble to hear it.

## The big flip: active ↔ passive

Same event, two camera angles. **Active** points at the doer. **Passive** points at the thing it happens to. Watch the words swap places:

    <p className="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-900">
      <span className="rounded bg-amber-200/60 px-1.5">Somebody</span> <strong>cleans</strong> <span className="rounded bg-emerald-200/60 px-1.5">the office</span> every day.
    </p>
  </div>
  <div className="text-center text-2xl text-slate-400">↓ flip</div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-50 to-teal-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">passive — the thing is the star</p>
    <p className="text-lg font-semibold text-slate-900">
      <span className="rounded bg-emerald-200/60 px-1.5">The office</span> <strong>is cleaned</strong> every day.
    </p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-600">(<em>Somebody</em> has quietly walked off — we don't need to say who.)</p>
  </div>

The same trick in the past:

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-4 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-base text-slate-900">
      <strong>active:</strong> Somebody <strong>cleaned</strong> the office yesterday.

    <p className="text-base text-slate-900">
      <strong>passive:</strong> The office <strong>was cleaned</strong> yesterday.
    </p>
  </div>

<Callout type="info" title="Why bother flipping?">
  Use the **passive** when the doer is **unknown** (*my phone was stolen* — no idea who), **everyone** (*butter is made from milk* — that's just how it works), or **not the point** (*this house was built 100 years ago* — the builders are long gone, only the house matters).
</Callout>

## The recipe: be + past participle

Every passive sentence has the same two ingredients glued together:

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-center justify-center gap-3 text-2xl font-bold sm:text-3xl">
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-1 text-indigo-700">am / is / are</span>
    <span className="text-indigo-400 text-base">or</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-1 text-indigo-700">was / were</span>
    <span className="text-indigo-500">+</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-indigo-600 px-3 py-1 text-white">past participle</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-3 text-center text-sm text-slate-600">
    a form of <strong>be</strong> ↑ &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; ↑ the verb in its &ldquo;done&rdquo; form
  </p>
</div>

The form of **be** carries the tense and matches the subject:

| when? | subject | be | + past participle |
|---|---|---|---|
| now / always | I | **am** | invited / cleaned / made |
| now / always | he / she / it / *singular thing* | **is** | invited / cleaned / made |
| now / always | we / you / they / *plural things* | **are** | invited / cleaned / made |
| past | I / he / she / it / *singular thing* | **was** | invited / cleaned / made |
| past | we / you / they / *plural things* | **were** | invited / cleaned / made |

The past participle is the same *done-form* you met in Lesson 15:
- **regular** verbs just add **-ed**: *cleaned, damaged, invited, painted*
- **irregular** verbs you have to learn one by one: *made, built, taken, broken, stolen, written, woken, born*

## Present passive — *is done / are done*

Use **am / is / are + past participle** when the action is true **now**, **every day**, or **in general**.

| sentence | meaning |
|---|---|
| Butter **is made** from milk. | (that's just how butter works) |
| Oranges **are imported** into Britain. | (every day, by lots of people) |
| How often **are** these rooms **cleaned**? | (asking about a routine) |
| I **am** never **invited** to parties. | (poor me — it's a general fact) |
| Glass **is made** from sand. | |
| Many languages **are spoken** in Switzerland. | |

<SayIt text="Butter is made from milk." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Oranges are imported into Britain." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I am never invited to parties." />

## Past passive — *was done / were done*

Use **was / were + past participle** for a **finished moment** — exactly the time slots you know from Lesson 11 (*yesterday, last week, in 1908, two days ago*).

| sentence | meaning |
|---|---|
| This house **was built** 100 years ago. | (one house — singular → was) |
| These houses **were built** 100 years ago. | (more than one — plural → were) |
| When **was** the telephone **invented**? | (asking when it happened) |
| We **weren't invited** to the party last week. | (negative — *be* + n't) |
| '**Was** anybody **injured** in the accident?' '**Were** any cars **damaged**?' | (questions — flip *be* to the front) |
| My phone **was stolen** a few days ago. | (we don't know who took it) |

<SayIt text="This house was built 100 years ago." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="When was the telephone invented?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="My phone was stolen." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't drop the *be*">
  The passive needs **two pieces** — the *be* word **and** the past participle. <br />
  ❌ This house **built** 100 years ago. → ✅ This house **was built** 100 years ago. <br />
  ❌ Football **plays** in most countries. → ✅ Football **is played** in most countries. <br />
  Without *am / is / are / was / were*, it's not a passive — it's just broken.
</Callout>

## Negatives and questions

Same trick as **be** in Lessons 1, 2 and 10:

- **Negative** — add **n't** (or **not**) to the *be* word: *isn't, aren't, wasn't, weren't*.
- **Question** — flip the *be* word to the front of the subject.

      <li>Nothing <strong>was stolen</strong>.</li>

## A special passive — *was / were born*

In English, you don't *bear yourself* — you **are born**. So talking about **where** or **when** somebody started life is always passive: **was born** or **were born**. Saying *I am born* or *I born* is a classic learner trap.

- I **was born** in Berlin in 1993. *(not I am born / I born)* <SayIt text="I was born in Berlin in 1993." iconOnly />
- 'Where **were** you **born**?' 'In Cairo.' <SayIt text="Where were you born?" iconOnly />
- My parents **were born** in the same village.

<Callout type="info" title="Born = passive past — always">
  Even though it feels like a piece of who you are, English treats *being born* as something that happened **to** you, in the past. So always: **was born** (one person) / **were born** (more than one). Never *am born*, never *was borned*.
</Callout>

## Adding the doer back: *passive + by* …

If the doer **does** matter, glue them to the end with **by**. The thing still gets to be the star — the doer just trails behind.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-50 to-teal-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <p className="mb-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">passive + by + doer</p>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-baseline justify-center gap-2 text-xl font-semibold text-slate-900">
    <span className="rounded bg-emerald-200/60 px-1.5">The telephone</span>
    <span>was invented</span>
    <span className="rounded bg-amber-200/60 px-1.5">by Alexander Bell</span>
    <span>in 1876.</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-2 text-center text-sm text-slate-600">= Alexander Bell invented it.</p>
</div>

A few more, and notice how *by* simply names the doer:
- I was bitten **by a dog** a few days ago.
- Do you like these paintings? They were painted **by a friend of mine**.
- The window was broken **by the wind**, not by a person.

<SayIt text="The telephone was invented by Alexander Bell." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I was bitten by a dog." />

<Callout type="tip" title="Use *by* only when it adds something">
  Skip *by* when the doer is obvious or pointless: ❌ *the office is cleaned by somebody every day.* ✅ *the office is cleaned every day.* Use *by* when the doer is **interesting** or **specific** — a famous person, a particular dog, a surprising cause.
</Callout>

## Now you try: things around you

For each cue, say one true passive sentence out loud. Pick **is/are** for general truths and **was/were** for finished moments. Add **by …** only if the doer is interesting.

1. (this room / clean / every day — or not very often) → ?
2. (my phone / make / in ____ ) → ?
3. (I / born / in ____ in ____ ) → ?
4. (my favourite book / write / by ____ ) → ?
5. (something in my home / break / last week — or nothing) → ?
6. (English / speak / in many countries) → ?

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Pick a famous building, food, or invention and tell its mini-story in **4 sentences** — all passive. Use **was/were built/made/invented** at least once, **by …** at least once, and one **is/are made/used/known** for a present-day fact. <br />
  *Example:* &ldquo;The Eiffel Tower **was built** in 1889. It **was designed by** Gustave Eiffel. It **is made** of iron. Today it **is visited** by millions of people every year.&rdquo;
</Callout>
