Adverbs answer the question **how?** — *quickly, badly, suddenly, carefully, heavily*. The recipe is simple: take an adjective and stamp **-ly** on the end (*quick → quickly, bad → badly, careful → carefully*). Adverbs go with **action verbs** (*speaks quietly, listen carefully*); adjectives stay with **be / look / feel / smell / taste / sound* (*Sue **is** quiet, You **look** tired*). Plus four exception words — *hard, fast, late, early* — that wear the same shape in both jobs, and the special pair *good → well*.

  *quick → quickly, bad → badly, sudden → suddenly, careful → carefully, heavy → heavily*. Spelling: *easy → easily, heavy → heavily* (-y → -ily).

## Part A — make the adverb

The standard recipe is **adjective + -ly**:

| adjective | adverb |
|-----------|--------|
| quick | quick**ly** |
| bad | bad**ly** |
| sudden | sudden**ly** |
| careful | careful**ly** |
| heavy | heav**ily** *(-y → -ily)* |
| easy | eas**ily** *(-y → -ily)* |

## Part B — adverbs after action verbs

Adverbs tell us *how* something happens or *how* somebody does it:

- The train **stopped suddenly**.
- I **opened** the door **slowly**.
- Please **listen carefully**.
- I **understand** you **perfectly**.
- It's **raining heavily**.

## Part C — *hard, fast, late, early*

These four words are **adjectives and adverbs** — same shape in both jobs (no -ly):

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">as adjective</p>
    <p className="text-base text-amber-900">Sue's job is very <strong>hard</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">Ben is a <strong>fast</strong> runner.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">The bus was <strong>late / early</strong>.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">as adverb</p>
    <p className="text-base text-sky-900">Sue works very <strong>hard</strong>. <span className="text-xs italic text-slate-500">(not hardly)</span></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-sky-900">Ben can <strong>run fast</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-sky-900">I went to bed <strong>late / early</strong>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="hard ≠ hardly">
  *hardly* exists, but it doesn't mean *with effort* — it means *almost not* (*I hardly know him*). The adverb of *hard* is just **hard**: *Sue **works hard*** (not *works hardly*).
</Callout>

## Part D — *good* → *well*

The adjective *good* has a **special** adverb: **well**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">good — adjective</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">Your English is very <strong>good</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">It was a <strong>good</strong> game.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">well — adverb</p>
    <p className="text-base text-violet-900">You speak English very <strong>well</strong>. <span className="text-xs italic text-slate-500">(not very good)</span></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">Our team played <strong>well</strong>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

But **well** is also an **adjective** when it means *not ill, in good health*:

- 'How are you?' 'I**'m very well**, thank you. And you?'

## Now you try

Write **six** sentences using adverbs (or the adjective/adverb pair).

1. *I speak English ___ .* (well / badly / slowly …)
2. *It rained ___ yesterday.* (heavily / lightly …)
3. *I work ___ for my exams.* (hard)
4. *Please drive ___ .* (carefully / slowly …)
5. *My friend sings very ___ .*
6. *Sue is very ___ , so she always speaks ___ .* (adjective + adverb pair)

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — find the verb first">
  Before you choose, ask: 'What word is in front of the gap?' If it's *be / look / feel / smell / taste / sound* → **adjective** (*you look tired*). If it's any other action verb → **adverb** (*he ran quickly*). Two simple tests, no more guessing — and you'll never write *play bad* or *speak good* again.
</Callout>
