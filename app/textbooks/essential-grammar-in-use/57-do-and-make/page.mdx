*Do* and *make* mean almost the same thing in many languages — but in English they split the work. **Do** covers the **action** itself (the time you spend, the job, the chore). **Make** covers the **thing that didn't exist before** — coffee, a cake, a phone call, a noise. Once you tune your ear to that one little difference, the right word starts falling out on its own.

## Part A — *do* = a general word for actions

When you can swap in *something / nothing / anything / everything*, English wants **do**.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">do = a general action</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">What are you <strong>doing</strong> this evening? <span className="text-xs italic text-slate-500">(not making)</span></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">'Shall I open the window?' 'No, it's OK. I'll <strong>do</strong> it.'</p>

    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I <strong>did</strong> a lot of things yesterday.</p>

## Part B — *make* = produce / create

When the action **leaves a new thing behind** — coffee in a cup, a cake on the plate, a sound in the air — English wants **make**.

### Compare *do* and *make*

- I **did** a lot yesterday. I **cleaned** my room, **wrote** some letters, and **made** a cake.
- A: What do you **do** in your free time? Sport? Reading? Hobbies? <br /> B: I **make** clothes. I **make** dresses and jackets. I also **make** toys for my children.

<Callout type="info" title="Two-step test">
  Ask: *Is something new there afterwards?* If yes → **make** (a cake, a list, a noise). If it's just spending time on a job → **do** (homework, the washing-up, your job).
</Callout>

## Part C — Fixed expressions with *do*

Some pairings are just locked. Treat them as one chunk.

  <div className="grid gap-px bg-emerald-200 sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3">
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do an exam / a test</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do a course</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do homework</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do housework</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do somebody a favour</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-emerald-900">do an exercise</p></div>
  </div>

- I'm **doing my driving test** next week.
- John has just **done a training course**.
- Our children have to **do a lot of homework**.
- I hate **doing housework**, especially cleaning.
- Sue, could you **do me a favour**?
- I go for a run and **do exercises** every morning.

### Also: *do the washing / shopping / cooking …*

When the chore name **starts with *the***, it's almost always **do**:

  <div className="bg-amber-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-900">do the …</div>
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-amber-200 sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-5">
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">do the shopping</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">do the washing</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">do the washing-up</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">do the ironing</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">do the cooking</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- I **did the washing**, but I didn't **do the shopping**.

## Part D — Fixed expressions with *make*

These are the everyday classics. Each one produces something — a thing, a sound, an arrangement.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-pink-200 bg-pink-50">
  <div className="bg-pink-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-pink-900">make + …</div>
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-pink-200 sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3">
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make a mistake</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make an appointment</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make a phone call</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make a list</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make a noise</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-pink-900">make a bed</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- I'm sorry, I **made a mistake**.
- I need to **make an appointment** to see the doctor.
- Excuse me, I have to **make a phone call**.
- Have you **made a shopping list**?
- It's late. Don't **make a noise**.
- Sometimes I forget to **make my bed** in the morning.

### One trap: *make a film* but *take a photo*

We say *make a film* (you're producing something new) **but** *take a photo / a picture* (English just locked this one with *take*).

    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">When was this film <strong>made</strong>?</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">When was this photo <strong>taken</strong>? <span className="text-xs italic text-slate-500">(not made)</span></p>
  </div>
</div>

## Now you try: a *do/make* day

Write **six** true sentences about today, three with **do** and three with **make**.

1. *Today I have to **do** …*
2. *Yesterday I **did** …*
3. *I never **do** …*
4. *This morning I **made** …*
5. *I'm going to **make** …*
6. *I sometimes **make a mistake** with …*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — the new-thing test">
  Pick five everyday phrases that confuse you (e.g., *the bed, an exam, a noise, the cooking, an appointment*). For each, ask yourself: **Is something new there afterwards?** If yes → *make*. If it's just spending time on a job → *do*. Write the verb in front of each phrase. Then check: do **the** chores → make **a/an** thing.
</Callout>
