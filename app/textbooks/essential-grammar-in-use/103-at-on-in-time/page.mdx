Three little words for time — and a clear way to pick the right one.

> **at** 8 o'clock &nbsp; **on** Monday &nbsp; **in** April

Think of a sliding scale: **at** is a single point on the clock, **on** is a whole day or date, and **in** is a wider stretch — a month, a year, a season.

A few real sentences to hear the shapes:

- I start work **at 8 o'clock**. The shops close **at 5.30**.
- Bye! I'll see you **on Friday**. The concert is **on 22 November**.
- I'm going on holiday **in October**. Emma was born **in 1995**.

## Part A — *at* phrases that aren't a clock time

A small group of fixed phrases takes **at** even though they aren't a clock number:

| at the weekend | at night | at Christmas | at the end of … | at the moment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Are you going away **at the weekend**? | I can't sleep **at night**. | Where will you be **at Christmas**? | I'm going on holiday **at the end of October**. | Are you busy **at the moment**? |

<Callout type="info" title="Christmas vs Christmas Day">
  **at Christmas** = the whole festive period. <br />
  **on Christmas Day** = that specific day (25 December). <br />
  Same logic: *on New Year's Day* — but a New Year's *Eve* party can be *at New Year*.
</Callout>

## Part B — morning / afternoon / evening / night

The default is **in**:

- I always feel good **in the morning**.
- Do you often go out **in the evening**?

But add a **day name** in front, and the whole thing flips to **on**:

- I'm meeting Jackie **on Monday morning**.
- Are you doing anything **on Saturday night**?

And the lonely outlier — **at night** for sleeping hours.

## Part C — no preposition before *this / last / next / every*

A super common trap: drop the *at/on/in* before these four words.

| word | example |
|---|---|
| **this** | Are you going out **this evening**? *(not in this evening)* |
| **last** | **Last summer** we went to Canada. *(not in last summer)* |
| **next** | I'm leaving **next Monday**. *(not on next Monday)* |
| **every** | We go on holiday **every summer**. *(not in every summer)* |

<Callout type="warning" title="The silent slot">
  ❌ *I'll see you **on next** Monday.* &nbsp; → ✅ *I'll see you **next** Monday.* <br />
  ❌ *I went there **in last** week.* &nbsp; → ✅ *I went there **last** week.* <br />
  *this / last / next / every* already say "which one" — no preposition needed.
</Callout>

## Part D — *in* for time **from now**

**in five minutes / in a few days / in two years** = that much time **from now**.

- Hurry! The train leaves **in five minutes**. *(= five minutes from now)*
- Bye! I'll see you **in a few days**. *(= a few days from now)*
- The exam is **in three weeks**.

## Now you try

Finish each sentence about your own life.

1. *I usually get up **at** ___ .*
2. *I was born **in** ___ .*
3. *My next holiday is **in** ___ .*
4. *I'm meeting a friend **on** ___ .*

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  **at** for the clock and a few fixed phrases (*at night, at the weekend, at Christmas*). <br />
  **on** for a day or a date (*on Monday, on 22 November, on Friday morning*). <br />
  **in** for a longer stretch (*in April, in 1995, in spring*) — and *in five minutes* for "from now". <br />
  Drop *at/on/in* before **this / last / next / every**.
</Callout>
