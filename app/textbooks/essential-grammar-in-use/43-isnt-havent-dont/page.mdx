You've already met every English negative — *isn't, haven't, won't, can't, didn't…* — scattered across the last forty lessons. Today we line them all up and look at what they have in common: every single one is the same little move. **Find the helper, drop *not* (n't) right after it.** That's the whole machine. (And if there's no helper, *do/does/did* shows up to be one.)

## The big idea — drop *not* on the helper

Every English sentence that says **no** is built the same way. Find the helper that's already running the sentence (*am, is, are, was, were, have, has, will, can, could, must, should, would*) — then snap **not** (or its short form **n't**) onto its back.

## "Hidden helpers" — present and past simple

Here's the trick. *I work, she likes, we played, he got* — those have **no visible helper**. So when you want to say no, you call in **do / does / did** to be the helper, and *they* take the *n't*.

### Watch what happens to the verb

When *do/does/did* takes the n't, the main verb goes back to its **plain shape** — drop the *-s*, drop the *-ed*. The helper carries the tense; the main verb just sits there in its bare form.

<Callout type="warning" title="The double-tense trap">
  Once *doesn't* or *didn't* shows up, the main verb goes <strong>plain</strong>. <br />
  ❌ Lisa doesn't <span className="line-through">plays</span> the guitar. &nbsp;✅ Lisa doesn't **play** the guitar.<br />
  ❌ Emily didn't <span className="line-through">had</span> dinner. &nbsp;✅ Emily didn't **have** dinner.<br /><br />
  The helper already carries the tense. Putting it on the verb too is like wearing two coats.
</Callout>

## Bonus 1 — *Don't!* (telling someone NOT to)

Want to tell somebody not to do something? Just stick **Don't** in front of the verb. No subject, no helper hunting — done.

## Bonus 2 — when *do* is the **main** verb too

Sometimes English really does double up: **don't do**, **doesn't do**, **didn't do**. The first one is the helper; the second one is the verb that means *to do something*. Looks weird, sounds normal.

- Do something! → **Don't do** anything!
- Sue **does** a lot at weekends. → Sue **doesn't do** much at weekends.
- I **did** what you said. → I **didn't do** what you said.

<SayIt text="Don't do anything!" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Sue doesn't do much at weekends." />

<Callout type="info" title="Two do's, one job each">
  The first *do/does/did* is just the **helper** carrying *n't*. The second *do* is a real action verb meaning **perform / accomplish**. You'll meet this same double in *do you do?* and *did you do?* in the next lesson on questions.
</Callout>

## Now you try: a "what I did NOT do today" diary

Write **six** true negatives about your own day so far. Use a different helper in each one. Try to hit all of these:

1. **am / is / are** → *I'm not …*
2. **was / were** → *I wasn't …*
3. **have / has** → *I haven't …*
4. **did** → *I didn't … this morning.*
5. **don't / doesn't** → *I don't …*
6. **can / will / should** → *I can't / won't / shouldn't …*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — twelve nots">
  Race the clock: in **two minutes**, write twelve true negative sentences about yourself, each using a **different** helper from the table above. <br /><br />
  *Example:* "I'm not French. I wasn't born in Paris. I haven't visited Brazil. I don't drink coffee. My phone doesn't work today. I didn't sleep well. I can't ski. I couldn't find my keys this morning. I won't be home for dinner. I shouldn't eat so much chocolate. I wouldn't like to be famous. I mustn't forget to buy milk."<br /><br />
  Twelve helpers, twelve n'ts — you've just rehearsed every English negative there is.
