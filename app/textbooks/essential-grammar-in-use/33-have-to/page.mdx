Some things in life aren't a choice — they're just *necessary*. You **have to** wear a seatbelt. Your sister **has to** get up at six. Yesterday you **had to** go to the dentist. Today's lesson is the family of little phrases English uses for *I'm obliged to do this* — including how to say the opposite (*I don't have to*) and how it differs from its bossy cousin **must**.

## A — "I have to ..." (present)

**I have to do something** = it is necessary for me to do it; I am obliged to do it.

| subject | verb | + to + verb |
|---|---|---|
| I / we / you / they | **have** | to do, to work, to go, to wear … |
| he / she / it | **has** | to do, to work, to go, to wear … |

A few in real life — tap to listen:

- I'll be late for work tomorrow. I **have to go** to the dentist. <SayIt text="I have to go to the dentist." iconOnly />
- Jane starts work at 7 o'clock, so she **has to get** up at 6. <SayIt text="She has to get up at six." iconOnly />
- You **have to pass** a test before you can get a driving licence. <SayIt text="You have to pass a test." iconOnly />
- My eyes are not very good. I **have to wear** glasses. <SayIt text="I have to wear glasses." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the little -s">
  ✅ Jane **has to** get up at 6. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Jane **have to** get up at 6.<br />
  Just like *he/she/it* in the present simple, **have** turns into **has** for one person.
</Callout>

## B — "I had to ..." (past)

For *yesterday*, *last week*, *this morning*, etc., use **had to** — same shape for everyone, no -s gymnastics:

- I was late for work yesterday. I **had to go** to the dentist. <SayIt text="I had to go to the dentist." iconOnly />
- We **had to walk** home last night. There were no buses. <SayIt text="We had to walk home." iconOnly />
- Mum **had to catch** a 7 o'clock train. <SayIt text="Mum had to catch a seven o'clock train." iconOnly />

## C — Questions and negatives: do / does / did

Here's where **have to** behaves like a normal verb, not like *must*. To ask a question or make a negative, you need a helper: **do / does** in the present, **did** in the past.

### Past questions

  <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-indigo-700">did + ... + have to ... ?</p>

<Callout type="warning" title="The verb stays plain">
  After *do / does / did*, keep **have** in its plain shape — never *has* or *had* in the question.<br />
  ✅ **Does** she **have to** work? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Does she **has to** work?<br />
  ✅ **Did** they **have to** leave? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Did they **had to** leave?
</Callout>

### "don't have to" = it is **not** necessary

Listen carefully — this one trips people up. **I don't have to do something** doesn't mean *I'm not allowed to*. It means *I don't need to — but I can if I want.*

- I'm not working tomorrow, so I **don't have to get** up early. <SayIt text="I don't have to get up early." iconOnly />
- Ian **doesn't have to work** very hard. He's got an easy job. <SayIt text="Ian doesn't have to work very hard." iconOnly />
- We **didn't have to wait** very long for the bus — it came in a few minutes. <SayIt text="We didn't have to wait." iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="don't have to ≠ mustn't">
  These two look similar but mean opposite things:<br />
  → **don't have to** = *it isn't necessary* (you can choose)<br />
  → **mustn't** = *it's forbidden* (you can't choose)<br />
  *You **don't have to** tell anyone.* — your secret is safe; tell people if you like. <br />
  *You **mustn't** tell anyone.* — keep it shut, no exceptions.
</Callout>

## D — must and have to

In English you have two ways to say something is necessary: **must** (the bossy modal) and **have to** (the everyday verb). They overlap — but only sometimes.

### When you give *your* opinion → either is fine

If **you personally** think something is necessary, both work.

- It's a fantastic film. You **must see** it. *or* You **have to see** it. <SayIt text="You must see it." iconOnly /> <SayIt text="You have to see it." iconOnly />

### When it's a *fact*, not your opinion → use **have to** (not must)

If you're just reporting a rule or a fact about someone else's life, **must** sounds wrong. Use **have to**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">a fact about her day</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Jane won't be at work this afternoon. She <strong>has to go</strong> to the doctor.</p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-xs text-emerald-700">(this is not my opinion — it's just a fact)</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">my personal opinion</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">Jane isn't well. She doesn't want to go to the doctor, but I told her she <strong>must go</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-1 text-xs text-violet-700">(this is what *I* think she should do)</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="tip" title="One quick test">
  Ask yourself: *Am I sharing a rule/fact, or pushing my own view?* <br />
  → **fact / rule** (timetables, jobs, laws) → **have to** is safest<br />
  → **my own urging** ("you really should!") → **must** is at home
</Callout>

## Now you try: a tiny obligation portrait

Say one true sentence about your life for each opener. Use **have to**, **has to**, **had to**, or **don't have to** — whichever fits.

1. **Every morning I …** *(have to / don't have to)*
2. **My best friend …** *(has to / doesn't have to)*
3. **Yesterday I …** *(had to / didn't have to)*
4. **Tomorrow I …** *(have to / don't have to)*
5. **At my school we …**
6. **One thing I really don't have to do is …**

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Tell a 6-sentence story about a *day from hell* — real or imagined. Use **had to** at least three times (*I had to ..., then I had to ..., and after that I had to ...*) and **didn't have to** at least once for the silver lining. Try it out loud first, then write it down. <br />
  *Example starter:* "Last Tuesday was crazy. I **had to get** up at 5. The car broke down, so I **had to take** the bus. At work I **had to finish** a huge report by lunch. I **didn't have to stay** late, though — my boss let me go at 4. Then I **had to walk** home in the rain. I **had to laugh** about it later — what else could I do?"
</Callout>
