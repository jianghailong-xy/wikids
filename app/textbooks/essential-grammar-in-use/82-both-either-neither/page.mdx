Three little words for **two things or two people**: **both** = the two of them (*both cities*), **either** = one or the other (*either way*), and **neither** = not one and not the other (*neither job*). *Both* takes a **plural noun and verb**; *either* and *neither* take a **singular**. And like *most/some/all*, they slip an **of** in front of *the / these / my* — but never in front of a plain noun.

<Callout type="tip" title="Two = both / either / neither">
  *both* = **A and B** (plural). *either* = **A or B** (singular). *neither* = **not A and not B** (singular). For three or more things, use *all / some / none* instead.
</Callout>

### Compare *either* and *neither*

- 'Would you like **tea or coffee**?'
  - '**Either**. I don't mind.' *(= tea or coffee)*
  - 'I **don't want either**.' *(not I don't want neither)*
  - '**Neither**.' *(= not tea and not coffee)*

<Callout type="warning" title="Never two negatives">
  ❌ *I don't want neither.* &nbsp; ✅ I **don't want either** (with a negative verb), or simply **Neither** on its own (with no verb).
</Callout>

## Part B — *both / either / neither* + noun

| word | + noun |
|------|--------|
| **both** | + **plural** noun (windows, books, children) |
| **either / neither** | + **singular** noun (window, book, child) |

- Last year I went to Paris and Rome. I liked **both cities** very much.
- First I worked in an office, then in a shop. **Neither job** was very interesting.
- There are two ways from here to the station. You can go **either way**.

## Part C — *both of / either of / neither of*

In front of **the / these / those / my / your / Paul's** add **of**:

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50">
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-sky-200 sm:grid-cols-2">
    <div className="bg-white p-3"><p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">both (of)</p><p className="mt-1 text-sm text-sky-900">the / these / my … (with or without of)</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3"><p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">either of / neither of</p><p className="mt-1 text-sm text-sky-900">the / these / my … (of is needed)</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- **Neither of my parents** is British.
- I haven't read **either of these books**.
- I like **both of those pictures**. *(or I like **both** those pictures.)*
- **Both of Paul's sisters** are married. *(or **Both** Paul's sisters are married.)*
- **Neither of Paul's sisters** is married. *(not Neither Paul's sisters)*

<Callout type="tip" title="The of rule">
  After *both*, **of is optional** before *the / these / my* (*both of those pictures* = *both those pictures*). After *either / neither*, **of is needed** (*either of these books*, not *either these books*).
</Callout>

## Part D — *both of them / neither of us*

In front of pronouns (**them, us, you**), *of* is **always** there:

- Paul has two sisters. **Both of them** are married.
- Sue and I didn't eat anything. **Neither of us** was hungry.
- Who are those two people? I **don't know either of them**.

## Now you try

Write **six** sentences about your life. Use *both, either,* or *neither* in each.

1. *Both of my parents ___ .*
2. *Neither of my friends ___ .*
3. *I have two ___ . Both ___ .*
4. *I have two ___ . Neither ___ .*
5. *Tea or coffee? — ___ , please.*
6. *Either ___ or ___ is fine.*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — singular vs plural">
  Pick a verb and try all three: *be married* → *Both my parents **are** married.* / *Either parent **is** married.* / *Neither parent **is** married.* The shape of *be* tells you whether the word is plural (*both*) or singular (*either / neither*). Do the same with *have a car, like coffee, speak French*.
</Callout>
