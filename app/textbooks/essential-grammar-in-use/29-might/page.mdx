You already know how to say what you **will** do — *I'll go, it'll rain.* But sometimes you're not sure. Maybe yes, maybe no. For that, English has a tiny word that does all the heavy lifting: **might**. *I **might** go to New York. It **might** rain.* It means **maybe will** — and that's almost the whole lesson.

## At the travel agent's

Tom is browsing brochures. His friend Anna asks where he's going for his holidays — but Tom hasn't decided yet. Listen for **might** in his answers.

## The big idea: might = maybe will

Picture a fork in the road. **Will** says *"I'm taking the left path — I'm sure."* **Might** shrugs and says *"Maybe left, maybe right — I haven't decided."*

<Callout type="info" title="One sentence to remember">
  **I might go** = *it is possible that I will go.* <br />
  Anywhere you could say *"maybe I will…"*, you can say *"I might…"* instead.
</Callout>

## The shape: might + plain verb

Here's the whole grammar in one table. **Might** never changes — it's the same for **I**, **she**, **they**, everybody. And the verb after it is always the **plain infinitive** (no **-s**, no **to**).

| subject | + might (not) + | plain verb |
|---|---|---|
| I / we / you / they | might | **be** lucky. |
| he / she / it | might (not) | **go** to New York. |
|  |  | **play** tennis tomorrow. |
|  |  | **rain** later. |
|  |  | **come** to the party. |

<SayIt text="I might go to the cinema." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She might phone this afternoon." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It might rain." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Three traps to dodge">
  ❌ She might<strong>s</strong> go. &nbsp; ✅ She **might go**. &nbsp; *(no **-s** on might or on the verb)* <br />
  ❌ I might **to** go. &nbsp; ✅ I **might go**. &nbsp; *(no **to** after might)* <br />
  ❌ I might **going**. &nbsp; ✅ I **might go**. &nbsp; *(no **-ing** — use the plain verb)*
</Callout>

## might in real life

Each of these everyday situations calls for **might**, because the speaker isn't sure.

- I **might go** to the cinema this evening, but I'm not sure. <SayIt text="I might go to the cinema this evening." iconOnly />
- A: When is Rebecca going to phone you? &nbsp; B: I don't know. She **might phone** this afternoon. <SayIt text="She might phone this afternoon." iconOnly />
- Take an umbrella with you. It **might rain**. <SayIt text="It might rain." iconOnly />
- Buy a lottery ticket. You **might be** lucky. <SayIt text="You might be lucky." iconOnly />
- A: Are you going out tonight? &nbsp; B: **I might.** *(= I might go out — you can drop the verb if it's obvious!)* <SayIt text="I might." iconOnly />

<Callout type="tip" title="Sure vs possible — listen for the difference">
  • **I'm playing tennis tomorrow.** → it's a plan; I'm sure. <br />
  • **I might play tennis tomorrow.** → maybe yes, maybe no. <br /> <br />
  • **Rebecca is going to phone later.** → she said so; it's set. <br />
  • **Rebecca might phone later.** → who knows? Possibly.
</Callout>

## Saying "maybe not" — might not

To say something is **possibly not** going to happen, just slip **not** in: **might not + plain verb**.

> **I might not go** = *it is possible that I will not go.*

| Setup | Sentence with **might not** |
|---|---|
| I'm tired and the traffic is bad. | I **might not go** to work tomorrow. |
| Sue's a bit ill. | She **might not come** to the party. |
| The clouds look heavy, but… | …it **might not rain** after all. |
| I'll try to finish, but… | …I **might not have** time. |

<SayIt text="I might not go to work tomorrow." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Sue might not come to the party." />

<Callout type="info" title="No short form for 'might not'">
  Unlike *isn't* or *won't*, **might not** stays as two words in writing. (You'll sometimes see *mightn't* in books, but it's rare — stick with **might not**.)
</Callout>

## may = might (a polite cousin)

There's a second word that does the same job: **may**. **I may go** means exactly the same as **I might go**. You can use either one.

| might | may (same meaning) |
|---|---|
| I **might go** to the cinema this evening. | I **may go** to the cinema this evening. |
| Sue **might not come** to the party. | Sue **may not come** to the party. |
| It **might rain** later. | It **may rain** later. |

<SayIt text="I may go to the cinema this evening." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Sue may not come to the party." />

### One special use — "May I ...?"

**May I ...?** has its own little job: it's a polite way of asking *"Is it OK if I ...?"* or *"Can I ...?"*

- **May I** ask a question? &nbsp; *(= is it OK to ask?)* <SayIt text="May I ask a question?" iconOnly />
- **May I** sit here? &nbsp; — Yes, of course. <SayIt text="May I sit here?" iconOnly />
- **May I** borrow your umbrella? <SayIt text="May I borrow your umbrella?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="tip" title="Two faces of may">
  • **It may rain.** → *may* = **might** (possibility). <br />
  • **May I sit here?** → *may* = **may I have permission?** (polite request). <br />
  Same word, two jobs — the sentence shape tells you which one.
</Callout>

## Now you try: three maybes for tomorrow

Tomorrow is a blank page. Say **three** true sentences out loud, each starting with **I might…** — three things that are *possible*, not certain.

1. (something you might do in the morning) → *I might…*
2. (something you might eat or drink) → *I might…*
3. (somewhere you might go, or someone you might see) → *I might…*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Picture tomorrow and tell a 4-sentence story out loud: <br />
  • **one thing you're sure about** — *I'm …ing tomorrow.* <br />
  • **two things that are possible** — *I might …* <br />
  • **one thing that's possibly NOT going to happen** — *I might not …* <br />
  *Example:* "I'm seeing my friend Lisa tomorrow. We might go to the park, or we might watch a film at her place. I might not get home before midnight!"
</Callout>
