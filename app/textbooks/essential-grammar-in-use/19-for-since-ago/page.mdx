So far we've talked about **right now** and about **the past**. Today we open the door between them. **For**, **since**, and **ago** are three tiny words that all answer one big question: *how long?* Pick the wrong one and a sentence breaks; pick the right one and it sings.

## A quick heads-up: 'has been', 'have been'

You'll see lots of **has been** and **have been** in this lesson. That's a special form for things that **started before now and are still true now** — your full guided tour comes in later lessons. For today, just read it as *the long-stay verb*. **For** and **since** are its closest friends.

- David **has been** at the airport for two hours. *(he arrived earlier — and he's still there)*
- I **haven't** eaten since breakfast. *(I ate at breakfast — and I'm still hungry now)*

## The big idea: three little time words

Each word answers a slightly different *how long?* question.

<Callout type="info" title="One question, three angles">
  All three measure time, but they look at it differently. **For** measures the *length* of a stretch. **Since** points at where the stretch *started*. **Ago** counts *back* from now to a point in the past.
</Callout>

## "for" — a length of time

Use **for** when you can answer with a number + a time word: *three days, two hours, six months, a long time*. The time stretches; **for** measures how much of it.

Things that fit naturally after **for**:

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| three days | ten minutes | a week |
| an hour | two hours | four weeks |
| a month | six months | five years |
| a long time | ages | the whole afternoon |

- Richard has been in Canada **for six months**. <SayIt text="Richard has been in Canada for six months." iconOnly />
- We've been waiting **for two hours**. <SayIt text="We've been waiting for two hours." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't say 'since six months'">
  ✅ Richard has been in Canada **for six months**. <br />
  ❌ Richard has been in Canada **since six months**. <br /><br />
  *Six months* is a **length**, not a starting point. Lengths get **for**.
</Callout>

## "since" — a point in time

Use **since** when you can answer with a **moment** the period started: *Monday, 9 o'clock, January, 2019, I was a child*. The clock or calendar tells you exactly *when* the stretch began.

      <div className="col-span-3 bg-emerald-200 px-2 py-2 font-semibold text-emerald-900">since Monday &nbsp; →</div>
    </div>
    <div className="mt-2 flex justify-between text-xs font-medium text-slate-500">
      <span>past</span>
      <span>now</span>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

Things that fit naturally after **since**:

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | Wednesday | yesterday |
| 9 o'clock | 12.30 | last night |
| 24 July | Christmas | the weekend |
| January | I was ten years old | we arrived |
| 1985 | this morning | breakfast |

A few in real sentences:

- Richard has been in Canada **since January**. *(= from January to now)* <SayIt text="Richard has been in Canada since January." iconOnly />
- We've been waiting **since 9 o'clock**. *(= from 9 o'clock to now)* <SayIt text="We've been waiting since 9 o'clock." iconOnly />
- I've lived in London **since I was ten years old**. <SayIt text="I've lived in London since I was ten years old." iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Notice the bonus form">
  After **since**, you can also drop in a **whole little sentence** about when the period began: *since I was ten*, *since we arrived*, *since the rain stopped*. **For** never does that — it always wants a length.
</Callout>

## "ago" — counting back from NOW

**Ago** means *before now*. Take a length of time, glue **ago** to the end, and you've pointed at a moment in the past.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5">
  <p className="mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">three weeks ago</p>
  <div className="mx-auto max-w-md">
    <div className="relative h-10 rounded-lg border-2 border-rose-300 bg-white">
      <div className="absolute inset-y-0 left-0 flex w-1/3 items-center justify-center rounded-l-md border-r-2 border-rose-500 bg-rose-100 text-sm font-semibold text-rose-900">
        🕒 then
      </div>
      <div className="absolute inset-y-0 right-0 flex w-1/3 items-center justify-center rounded-r-md border-l-2 border-rose-500 bg-rose-100 text-sm font-semibold text-rose-900">
        ⏰ now
      </div>
      <div className="absolute inset-y-0 left-1/3 right-1/3 flex items-center justify-center text-xs font-medium text-rose-700">
        ← three weeks ago
      </div>
    </div>
    <div className="mt-2 flex justify-between text-xs font-medium text-slate-500">
      <span>past</span>
      <span>now</span>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

Some real-life examples — notice the verb. **Ago** travels with the **past tense** (*started, did, had, was, went*…):

- Susan **started** her new job **three weeks ago**. *(= three weeks before now)* <SayIt text="Susan started her new job three weeks ago." iconOnly />
- "When did Tom go out?" "**Ten minutes ago**." *(= ten minutes before now)* <SayIt text="Ten minutes ago." iconOnly />
- I **had** dinner **an hour ago**. <SayIt text="I had dinner an hour ago." iconOnly />
- Life **was** very different **a hundred years ago**. <SayIt text="Life was very different a hundred years ago." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="'ago' lives at the end">
  In English, **ago** comes **after** the length, not before it.<br />
  ✅ I had dinner **an hour ago**. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I had dinner **ago an hour**.<br />
  ✅ Tom went out **ten minutes ago**. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Tom went out **ago ten minutes**.
</Callout>

## "for" vs "ago" — same length, different job

Both **for** and **ago** sit next to a length of time, but they ask different questions:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">how long? → for</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700"><strong>How long has Helen been in Ireland?</strong></p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">She has been in Ireland <strong>for three days</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-xs text-amber-800">⏱️ a stretch from start until now</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">when? → ago</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700"><strong>When did Helen arrive in Ireland?</strong></p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">She arrived in Ireland <strong>three days ago</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-xs text-rose-800">📍 a point three days back from now</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="tip" title="Spot the verb tense">
  **For** loves *has been / have been* (long-stay). **Ago** loves *did / was / went / had* (past, finished moments). If your sentence is in the past simple, you almost always want **ago**.
</Callout>

## Use ago: when was the last time…?

Read each question. Imagine a real answer in your head. Say it out loud using **ago** + a past-tense verb.

1. **When was your last meal?** → *e.g.* "I had lunch **two hours ago**."
2. When was the last time you were ill?
3. When did you last go to the cinema?
4. When was the last time you were in a car?
5. When was the last time you went on holiday?
6. When did you last call a friend on the phone?

<Callout type="tip" title="Listen to your verb">
  Did you say *was, had, went, called*? Good — those are past-tense verbs, and **ago** loves them. If you start to say *I have been*, switch to **for** or **since** instead.
</Callout>
