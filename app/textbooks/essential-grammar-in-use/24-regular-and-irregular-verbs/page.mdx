By now you've used loads of past verbs — *cleaned, went, lived, broke, studied, made*. Time to step back and look at the **two families** they belong to. Every English verb has two important past forms: a **past simple** (*I cleaned*) and a **past participle** (*I have cleaned*). For the **regular** family, both forms are the same boring **-ed**. For the **irregular** family, things get more interesting — sometimes the two forms match, sometimes they don't.

## Lisa shows Emma round the new flat

Emma comes over for tea. Lisa walks her around the flat — there's been a lot going on. Listen for the past verbs. Sometimes they tell you what Lisa **did** (past simple). Sometimes they tell you what's **been done** (have/has + past participle), or what **is done** to things in general (be + past participle). Tap each bubble to hear it.

## Every verb has two past forms

Stop thinking of past tense as one word. Every verb actually carries **two** past shapes in its pocket — and you've already met them both.

<Callout type="info" title="The participle never travels alone">
  You'll never see a past participle on its own. It always has a helper word in front of it — **have / has** for the present perfect, or **am / is / are / was / were / been** for the passive. If there's no helper, you want the **past simple**, not the participle.
</Callout>

## Family 1 — regular verbs: one shape, two jobs

For most English verbs, the past simple **and** the past participle are **exactly the same word** — just **-ed**. One ending does both jobs. Lazy and convenient.

The **same word** does both jobs:

- I **cleaned** my room yesterday. *(past simple — alone)*
- I **have cleaned** my room. *(past participle — after **have**)*
- These rooms **are cleaned** every day. *(past participle — after **are**)*

<SayIt text="I cleaned my room yesterday." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I have cleaned my room." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="These rooms are cleaned every day." />

<Callout type="tip" title="Spelling reminder (Lesson 11)">
  Three quick tweaks for **-ed**: words ending in **-e** add only **-d** (*live → lived*); a **consonant + y** flips to **-ied** (*study → studied*); short verbs with one vowel + one consonant **double the consonant** (*stop → stopped*).
</Callout>

## Family 2 — irregular verbs: no -ed, three patterns

Irregular verbs **never** take **-ed**. Instead they change shape. There are three patterns to spot — and once you know them, you can read any verb table.

### Pattern A — same shape for both jobs (like the regulars, just no -ed)

About half of all irregular verbs are nice and friendly: **past simple = past participle**, just like the **-ed** family, only with a different shape.

Three jobs, three sentences with **break**:

- Somebody **broke** this window last night. *(past simple)*
- Somebody **has broken** this window. *(past participle — present perfect)*
- This window **was broken** last night. *(past participle — passive)*

<Callout type="warning" title="The 'broke / broken' trap">
  After **have / has** or **is / was**, you must use the **participle** — never the past simple. <br />
  ✅ Somebody **has broken** the window. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Somebody has **broke** the window. <br />
  ✅ The window **was broken**. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ The window was **broke**.
</Callout>

## Where do past participles live?

A past participle is the verb in its **"done"** form. It only ever shows up after a helper. The two helpers you'll meet most often:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-50 to-teal-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">have / has + participle</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">= an action linked to <em>now</em>. (Lessons 15–18)</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Tina <strong>has lived</strong> in London for ten years.</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Somebody <strong>has broken</strong> the window.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-violet-50 to-fuchsia-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">be + participle (passive)</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">= the action <em>happens to</em> the subject.</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-violet-900">Butter <strong>is made</strong> from milk.</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-violet-900">My car <strong>has been repaired</strong>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="Just a peek at the passive">
  You'll meet the **passive** properly in a later lesson — for now, just notice the **shape**: a form of *be* (*is, are, was, were, been*) plus a **past participle**. The participle form is the same one you already use after *have / has*.
</Callout>

## Now you try: three sentences, three jobs

Pick any verb you like — *clean, paint, break, make, eat, write, repair*. Say **three** sentences out loud, one for each job:

1. **Past simple** — *Yesterday I …*
2. **Present perfect** — *I have …* (or *I've already …*)
3. **Passive** — *It is …* / *It was …* (e.g., *It was made by …*, *It is cleaned every day*)

Try it out loud. <br />
