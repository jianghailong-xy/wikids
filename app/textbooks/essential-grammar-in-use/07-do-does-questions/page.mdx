You already know how to **tell** someone what you do every day: *I work in a bank. She lives in London. They speak English.* That's the **present simple** — your "facts and habits" tense. Today we learn the other half: how to **ask** about someone else's facts and habits. The trick is one tiny word: **do** (or **does**).

## The big trick: add **do** to the front

In the last lessons, **am / is / are** *flipped* with the subject to make a question. Present simple is different — there's no flipping. Instead, we **glue a tiny helper word in front**: **do** (or **does** for he/she/it). The rest of the sentence stays where it is.

## Do or does? Let the subject decide

Just like *is* and *are*, the helper word changes shape based on **who** we're asking about. There are only two forms — and **does** is just for one person or thing:

| Subject | Helper |
|---|---|
| **I / we / you / they** | **do** |
| **He / she / it** | **does** |

- **Do** *I* know you?
- **Do** *we* need eggs?
- **Do** *you* speak English?
- **Do** *they* live near here?
- **Does** *he* like music?
- **Does** *she* drive?
- **Does** *it* rain a lot here?

<Callout type="tip" title="Same rule as -s">
  Easy memory hook: if the subject takes the **-s** verb (*he work**s***, *she like**s***), then it takes the **-s** helper too — **doe**s. Same family.
</Callout>

## One word of warning: the verb loses its **-s**

When **does** shows up, it takes the **-s** with it. The main verb goes back to its **base form** — the bare dictionary version with nothing on the end.

## Asking for details: Wh-questions

Yes/no isn't always enough — sometimes we need *where*, *what*, *how*, *why*. Same trick: put the wh-word **before** the helper.

<Callout type="info" title="The pattern">
  **Wh-word** + **do/does** + **subject** + **verb** + ... ?
</Callout>

- **Where do** you live? <SayIt text="Where do you live?" iconOnly />
- **What does** this word **mean**? <SayIt text="What does this word mean?" iconOnly />
- **How often do** you **watch** TV? <SayIt text="How often do you watch TV?" iconOnly />
- **What do** you **want** for dinner? <SayIt text="What do you want for dinner?" iconOnly />
- **How much does** it **cost** to fly to Rome? <SayIt text="How much does it cost to fly to Rome?" iconOnly />
- **Where does** your sister **work**? <SayIt text="Where does your sister work?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="tip" title="A useful idiom: 'What do you do?'">
  *"What do you do?"* doesn't mean *what are you doing right now?* — it means **what's your job?** <br />
  *"What do you do?"* &nbsp; — &nbsp; *"I work in a bank."*
</Callout>

## Where do **always** and **usually** go?

Words like **always**, **usually**, **often**, **sometimes** describe how often something happens. In a question, they slot **between the subject and the verb** — *not* at the front, *not* at the end.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| ~~Always does Chris work on Sundays?~~ | **Does Chris always work on Sundays?** |
| ~~Do you get up early always?~~ | **Do you always get up early?** |
| ~~Usually what do you do at weekends?~~ | **What do you usually do at weekends?** |

<SayIt text="Does Chris always work on Sundays?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What do you usually do at weekends?" />

## Saying yes and no the short way

Just like with *be*, polite English answers a yes/no question with the matching helper word — not just a bare *yes* or *no*.

| Question | Yes | No |
|---|---|---|
| **Do** *you* watch TV? | **Yes, I do.** | **No, I don't.** |
| **Do** *they* speak English? | **Yes, they do.** | **No, they don't.** |
| **Does** *he* work hard? | **Yes, he does.** | **No, he doesn't.** |
| **Does** *she* live in London? | **Yes, she does.** | **No, she doesn't.** |
| **Does** *it* rain a lot? | **Yes, it does.** | **No, it doesn't.** |

<Callout type="tip" title="Mirror trick">
  The helper in your answer **mirrors the subject of your answer**, not the question. *"**Do you** like coffee?"* → *"Yes, **I do**."* (you turns into I.)
</Callout>

<SayIt text="Do you play the piano? No, I don't." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Does your sister live in London? No, she doesn't." />

## Now you try: interview a new friend

Imagine someone has just joined your class. Write **six** present-simple questions you could ask them — and try to use a different starter word for each:

1. **Where** ... ?
2. **What** ... ?
3. **How often** ... ?
4. **Do** you ... ?
5. **Does** your family ... ?
6. **What time** ... ?

Say each one out loud. Listen for the **do** or **does** at the start, and check that the verb after the subject is in its **base form** (no -s).

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Pick a famous person — a singer, an athlete, a fictional character. Write **five questions** you'd love to ask them about their daily life. At least one should start with **does**, at least one should be a **wh-question**, and at least one should use **always** or **usually**. Bonus: answer them in their voice with **Yes, I do.** / **No, I don't.**
