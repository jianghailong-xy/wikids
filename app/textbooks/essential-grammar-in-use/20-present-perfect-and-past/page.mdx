You've already met two ways to talk about earlier events: **I have done** (present perfect — *the past reaching into now*) and **I did** (past simple — *a finished moment back there*). They look similar, but English is picky about which one to use. The trick is to listen for **time clues** — words like **yesterday**, **last week**, **ever**, **just**, **for six years**. They quietly tell you which tense to pick.

## Lisa calls Emma

Lisa hasn't seen her sister Emma in ages. They jump on a video call to catch up. Listen carefully — sometimes Lisa says **have you done?** (asking about *life until now*), sometimes **did you do?** (asking about a *finished moment*). Tap each bubble to hear it.

## Rule 1 — finished time → past simple

If the sentence names a **finished time** (*yesterday, last week, in 2002, two minutes ago*), use the **past**. The present perfect doesn't fit here, because that time isn't *now anymore*.

| ✅ past simple (with finished time) | ❌ present perfect |
|---|---|
| I **saw** Paula yesterday. | I have seen Paula yesterday. |
| Where **were** you on Sunday afternoon? | Where have you been on Sunday? |
| We **didn't have** a holiday last year. | We haven't had a holiday last year. |
| 'What **did** you **do** last night?' | What have you done last night? |
| Shakespeare **lived** from 1564 to 1616. He **was** a writer. | Shakespeare has lived from 1564 to 1616. |

<Callout type="warning" title="The yesterday trap">
  As soon as you hear yourself attach a **finished time word** (*yesterday, last week, on Friday, in 2002*), make the verb past — even if the action feels recent. <br />
  ✅ I **finished** my work at 2 o'clock. &nbsp; ❌ I've finished my work at 2 o'clock.
</Callout>

## Rule 2 — When ... ? and What time ... ? → past simple

Asking *exactly when* something happened? Use the past — because **when** is asking for a finished point on the timeline.

- **When did** you buy your computer? *(not When have you bought)* <SayIt text="When did you buy your computer?" iconOnly />
- **What time did** Andy go out? *(not What time has Andy gone)* <SayIt text="What time did Andy go out?" iconOnly />

But when the question is **Have you ever...?** or **Have you...yet?** — that's life-until-now territory, so present perfect is right at home.

- **Have** you **ever been** to Spain? <SayIt text="Have you ever been to Spain?" iconOnly />
- **Has** Sam **phoned** you yet? <SayIt text="Has Sam phoned you yet?" iconOnly />

## Rule 3 — is the situation still alive?

This is the deepest rule, and it explains why the tenses *feel* so different. Ask yourself: **is the action / situation still connected to now?**

<Callout type="info" title="One short test">
  Before you choose, ask: **is the line still touching now?** <br />
  → **Yes** (the result, the place, the person, the chance is still here) → **present perfect** <br />
  → **No** (it ended, with a time word, or the person is gone) → **past simple**
</Callout>

## Side-by-side: same words, different tense

The same vocabulary — *see, go, live, phone* — flips between the two tenses depending on the time clue. Tap to hear the pair:

| present perfect (line to now) | past simple (finished moment) |
|---|---|
| **Have** you **seen** Kate? *(where is she now?)* | **Did** you **see** Kate on Saturday? |
| **Have** you **ever been** to Spain? | **Did** you **go** to Spain last year? |
| Sam **hasn't phoned** me yet. | Sam **didn't phone** me yesterday. |
| We **'ve lived** in Singapore for six years. *(we still do)* | We **lived** in Glasgow for six years. *(but not now)* |
