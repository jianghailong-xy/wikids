In the last lesson you met **the echo** — keep the helper, drop the rest. Today the echo learns two new tricks. **Flip it into a question** — *Are you? Have you? Don't you?* — and you've shown the other person you're listening, surprised, or curious. **Tape it onto the end of a sentence** — *…isn't it? …have you?* — and you've asked them to nod along. Same eight little helpers from yesterday; two brand-new jobs.

## Trick 1 — the reply question (show you're listening)

When someone tells you something, you don't have to say *"Oh, that's interesting!"* every time. English has a tinier, friendlier move: bounce the **helper back as a question**.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-rose-50 to-pink-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">the long way</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">"I'm writing a book."</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-500">"Oh, that's really interesting! Tell me more about it!"</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-50 to-teal-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">the short way</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">"I'm writing a book."</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">"<strong>Are you?</strong> What about?"</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="Same helper, just flipped">
  The opener is *I'**m** writing a book.* &nbsp;→&nbsp; the helper is *am*.<br />
  Flip it for a question (*am → are* with *you*) and you've got *Are you?* The rest of the sentence (*writing a book*) is already understood — drop it.
</Callout>

### Negative opener → negative reply question

If the opener is **negative** (*I'm not, Sue isn't, Bill can't*), keep it negative when you bounce it back. The surprise lands better that way.

### When the helper is invisible — *do / does / did*

Present simple and past simple don't show their helper in the opener. The moment you reply, **do / does / did** has to step in. (Same rule as lesson 40 — the echo just wears a question mark this time.)

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">present simple → do / does</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">'I speak four languages.' <br/>'<strong>Do you?</strong> Which ones?'</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">'Ben doesn't eat meat.' <br/>'<strong>Doesn't he?</strong> Does he eat fish?'</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-sky-50 to-cyan-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">past simple → did</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">'Nicola got married last week.' <br/>'<strong>Did she?</strong> Really?'</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">'I didn't sleep well last night.' <br/>'<strong>Didn't you?</strong> Was the bed uncomfortable?'</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't repeat the verb">
  ✅ 'I speak four languages.' &nbsp; '**Do you?** Which ones?'<br />
  ❌ 'I speak four languages.' &nbsp; '**Do you speak?** Which ones?'<br /><br />
  Once *do/does/did* shows up, the original verb is already understood. Just the helper, then a follow-up question.
</Callout>

## Trick 2 — the question tag (invite them to nod)

The same little helpers can also live at the **end** of a sentence, as tiny tag-along questions. They mean *right? · don't you agree? · am I correct?* — and English speakers stitch them onto sentences all day long.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
  <p className="text-lg font-semibold text-indigo-900">It's a beautiful day, <span className="rounded-md bg-white px-2 py-0.5 shadow-sm">isn't it?</span></p>
  <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-600">Yes, it's perfect.</p>
</div>

### The flip rule

Here's the move: a **positive** sentence takes a **negative** tag. A **negative** sentence takes a **positive** tag. The helper itself stays the same — only the polarity flips.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">positive → negative tag</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">It's a beautiful day, <strong>isn't it?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Kate lives in London, <strong>doesn't she?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">You closed the window, <strong>didn't you?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Those shoes are nice, <strong>aren't they?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Tom will be here soon, <strong>won't he?</strong></p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">negative → positive tag</p>
    <p className="text-base text-violet-900">That isn't your car, <strong>is it?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">You haven't met my mother, <strong>have you?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">Helen doesn't go out much, <strong>does she?</strong></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">You won't be late, <strong>will you?</strong></p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="Three things the tag has to match">
  1. **Same helper family** — *is → isn't*, *will → won't*, *can → can't*. If the sentence had no visible helper (present/past simple), reach for *do/does/did*.<br />
  2. **Opposite polarity** — positive opener gets a negative tag, and vice versa.<br />
  3. **Pronoun matches the subject** — *Kate → she*, *Tom → he*, *those shoes → they*, *the dog → it*.
</Callout>

## Reply question or question tag — what's the difference?

Both use the same little helpers — but they sit in different places and do different jobs.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">reply question</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">A whole little sentence on its own.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">"I've bought a new car." &nbsp; "<strong>Have you?</strong>"</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-500">Job: show interest or surprise.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">question tag</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">A tail glued to the end of someone's own sentence.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">"You've bought a new car, <strong>haven't you?</strong>"</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-500">Job: invite a yes (or check you're right).</p>
  </div>
</div>
