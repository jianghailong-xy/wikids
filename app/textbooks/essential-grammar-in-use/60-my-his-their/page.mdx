In lesson 59 you saw the *I/me, he/him* pair — pronouns that **stand alone**. Today's set is different: **my, our, your, his, her, their, its**. They never stand alone — they always **travel with a noun** (*my hands, his car, their parents*). Each one is a tiny tag that says *whose?*. Get the right tag and the rest of the sentence builds itself.

## A rainy day full of umbrellas

A sudden shower, six umbrellas, six different owners. Listen for the tiny word in front of each *umbrella*.

### One pattern, seven shapes

- **I** like **my** house.
- **We** like **our** house.
- **You** like **your** house.
- **He** likes **his** house.
- **She** likes **her** house.
- **They** like **their** house.
- Oxford (= **it**) is famous for **its** university.

## Part B — *his / her / their* close-up

These three are the ones learners mix up most. Match the **owner** with the right tag.

    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-sky-900"><strong>his</strong> sister</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-sky-900"><strong>his</strong> parents</p>

    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-amber-900"><strong>their</strong> son</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-amber-900"><strong>their</strong> daughter</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-amber-900"><strong>their</strong> children</p>
  </div>

<Callout type="info" title="The owner picks the tag — not the thing">
  *his sister* is fine — *sister* is a girl, but *his* says **the owner** is a boy. The thing being owned doesn't change the tag.
</Callout>

## Part C — *its* vs *it's* — the classic trap

These two look almost identical but mean **completely** different things.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">its = belongs to it (no apostrophe)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">Oxford is famous for <strong>its</strong> university.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">This is a beautiful tree. <strong>Its</strong> leaves are a beautiful colour.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">it's = it is (with apostrophe)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900">I like Oxford. <strong>It's</strong> a nice place. <span className="text-xs italic text-slate-500">(= It is a nice place.)</span></p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="The apostrophe test">
  Stuck? Try saying **it is** in the gap. If it fits → write **it's**. If it doesn't → write **its**. Easy and 100% reliable.
</Callout>

## Now you try

Write **six** true sentences about the people and things in your life — using a different possessive in each one.

1. *My …*
2. *Our …*
3. *His …* (about a man / boy you know)
4. *Her …* (about a woman / girl you know)
5. *Their …* (about two or more people you know)
6. *Its …* (about a place, animal, or thing — not *it's*!)

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — never *I/he/she* in front of a noun">
  Read your sentences out loud. If you hear *I house*, *he car*, *she dog* — stop! In English, possessives **must** travel with a noun. Convert the subject pronoun to its possessive (I → **my**, he → **his**, she → **her**) and the sentence snaps into place.
</Callout>
