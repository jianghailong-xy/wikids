So far almost everything you've said has lived in the **past** or the **now**. Today we open a window onto the **future**. The first phrase to learn is small but powerful: **am / is / are + going to + verb**. With it, you can announce a plan you've already made (*I'm going to buy a new phone*) or a thing you can already see coming (*Look at the sky — it's going to rain!*).

## The shape: am / is / are + going to + verb

If you can do **am / is / are** (Lesson 1) and you can do the **present continuous** (Lesson 3), you can already do this. The recipe is:

> **am / is / are** + **going to** + the plain verb

The first word changes with the subject — exactly like Lessons 1 and 3 — and **going to** never changes.

<Conjugation ending=" going to watch TV." />

| Subject | + be | + going to | + verb |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | **am** | going to | watch |
| **He / She / It** | **is** | going to | watch |
| **We / You / They** | **are** | going to | watch |

Tap to hear the three forms:

<SayIt text="I am going to watch TV this evening." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She is going to buy a new car." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="They are going to play tennis tomorrow."/>

<Callout type="warning" title="Three pieces, always">
  The three pieces — **be** + **going to** + a plain verb — must travel together. Drop one and the sentence breaks. <br />
  ✅ I **am going to wash** the car. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I going to wash the car. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I am going to washing. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I am going wash.
</Callout>

## Saying "no" — slip in *not*

Just like with the present continuous, the negative is one tiny word slotted between **be** and **going to**:

> **am / is / are** + **not** + **going to** + verb

| Subject | + be + not | + going to | + verb |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | **'m not** | going to | have |
| **He / She / It** | **isn't** | going to | have |
| **We / You / They** | **aren't** | going to | have |

- I **'m not going to have** breakfast — I'm not hungry. <SayIt text="I'm not going to have breakfast." iconOnly />
- It **isn't going to rain** today. The sky is clear. <SayIt text="It isn't going to rain today." iconOnly />
- We **aren't going to invite** Mark. He's away anyway. <SayIt text="We aren't going to invite Mark." iconOnly />

## What does it mean? Two close cousins.

**Going to** does two jobs. Both of them point forward in time — but the *reason you're sure* is slightly different.

## A quick cousin: present continuous for arrangements

You already met **I am doing** for *right now* in Lesson 3. It also has a future job: it's the natural choice for **fixed arrangements** — things on the calendar, agreed with someone else.

- I **am playing** tennis with Julia tomorrow. *(we agreed yesterday — it's on the calendar)* <SayIt text="I am playing tennis with Julia tomorrow." iconOnly />
- We **are having** dinner with Sam on Friday. *(booked)* <SayIt text="We are having dinner with Sam on Friday." iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Going to vs am doing — a tiny nudge">
  For most everyday plans, both are fine and a native speaker would barely notice the difference. A gentle nudge: <br />
  • **am doing** leans on a **fixed arrangement** with another person or place (a booking, an appointment). <br />
  • **am going to** leans on the **decision in your head** — even if nothing's booked yet. <br />
  Don't agonise — pick either, and you'll be understood.
</Callout>

## Now you try: three things tomorrow

Think about a real day coming up — tomorrow, or this weekend. Picture three things you've already decided. Then say each one out loud as a full sentence beginning with **I'm going to ...** or, for a negative, **I'm not going to ...**.

1. Something you're going to **do in the morning** → ?
2. Something you're going to **eat** → ?
3. Someone you're going to **see** or **call** → ?
4. Something you're **not going to do** because you don't want to → ?
5. Something you can already see is **going to happen** (the weather, a deadline, a film coming out) → ?

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Tell a tiny **6-sentence story** about your **next weekend**. Use **I'm going to...** at least twice (your plans), **I'm not going to...** at least once (something you've decided to skip), and one **It's going to...** prediction about something you can already see coming. Try it out loud. <br />
  *Example starter:* "**This weekend I'm going to be busy.** On Saturday morning I'm going to clean the kitchen — it's a mess. After that I'm going to meet my sister for lunch. I'm not going to wash the car, because it's going to rain all weekend. On Sunday evening I'm going to watch a film at home and go to bed early. Monday is going to be a long day!"
</Callout>
