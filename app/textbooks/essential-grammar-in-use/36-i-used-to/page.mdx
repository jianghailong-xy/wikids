People change. Hair gets shorter, hobbies fade, neighbourhoods empty out. English has a special little phrase for the things that **were true for a stretch of your life — but aren't anymore**: **used to**. It's not quite past simple, not quite present. It's the verb of "*back then, I was a different person*."

Dave **used to work** in a factory. Now he works in a supermarket. Two true sentences about the same person — different chapters of his life. Tap each line to hear it.

## The shape: subject + used to + verb

The verb after **used to** is always the **plain base form** — the dictionary form. No -s, no -ed, no -ing. Just the bare verb.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <div className="grid grid-cols-[auto_auto_1fr] items-center gap-x-4 gap-y-2 text-center">
    <div className="rounded-lg bg-white/80 px-3 py-2 text-sm font-semibold text-indigo-900">
      I / you / we / they<br />he / she / it
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-lg bg-indigo-200 px-4 py-2 text-base font-bold text-indigo-900">
      used to
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-lg bg-white/80 px-3 py-2 text-sm font-semibold text-indigo-900">
      be · work · have · play · live · like…
    </div>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-3 text-center text-xs italic text-indigo-700">One shape for everyone — <strong>used to</strong> never changes.</p>
</div>

The verb that follows can be almost anything: **be, have, work, like, play, live, see, eat…** The phrase says *this was a habit / a state for a long time, but not now*.

- When I was a child, I **used to like** chocolate. <SayIt text="When I was a child, I used to like chocolate." iconOnly />
- I **used to read** a lot of books, but I don't read much these days. <SayIt text="I used to read a lot of books, but I don't read much these days." iconOnly />
- Lisa has got short hair now, but it **used to be** very long. <SayIt text="Lisa has got short hair now, but it used to be very long." iconOnly />
- They **used to live** in the same street as us, so we **used to see** them a lot. But we don't see them much these days. <SayIt text="They used to live in the same street as us, so we used to see them a lot." iconOnly />
- Helen **used to have** a piano, but she sold it a few years ago. <SayIt text="Helen used to have a piano, but she sold it a few years ago." iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Two flavours: habit or state">
  **Used to** covers both:
  - **Habits** — things you did again and again: *I used to play tennis. We used to see them a lot.*
  - **States** — things that were true for a long time: *Her hair used to be long. He used to have a dog.*
  In both cases, the rule is the same: it was true *back then*, and it isn't *now*.
</Callout>

## Negative and question: "use to" loses its -d

Here's the only sneaky part. In the negative and the question, **did / didn't** is already doing the past-tense work — so **used** drops its -d and becomes plain **use**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-3">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">positive</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">I <strong>used to</strong> like tomatoes.</p>
    <p className="text-xs text-slate-600">subject + <strong>used to</strong> + verb</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">negative</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">I <strong>didn't use to</strong> like tomatoes.</p>
    <p className="text-xs text-slate-600">subject + <strong>didn't use to</strong> + verb</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">question</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-sky-900">Where <strong>did</strong> you <strong>use to</strong> live?</p>
    <p className="text-xs text-slate-600">(question word) + <strong>did</strong> + subject + <strong>use to</strong> + verb</p>
  </div>
</div>

- When I was a child, I **didn't use to like** tomatoes. <SayIt text="When I was a child, I didn't use to like tomatoes." iconOnly />
- Where **did** you **use to live** before you came here? <SayIt text="Where did you use to live before you came here?" iconOnly />
- **Did** you **use to play** football at school? <SayIt text="Did you use to play football at school?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="The missing -d">
  ✅ I **didn't use to** like tomatoes. &nbsp; ❌ I didn't **used to** like tomatoes.<br />
  ✅ Did you **use to** live here? &nbsp; ❌ Did you **used to** live here?<br /><br />
  Why? Because **did / didn't** is already past tense. Putting -d on *used* would be doubling up — like saying *didn't liked* instead of *didn't like*.
</Callout>

## "used to" is for the PAST only

There is no present version. You **cannot** say *I use to play golf* to mean *I play golf these days*. For habits that are true **now**, just use the **present simple**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">past habit (no longer true)</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-amber-900">I <strong>used to play</strong> tennis.</p>
    <p className="text-xs text-slate-600">= I played tennis a lot back then. I don't now.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">present habit (true now)</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">These days I <strong>play</strong> golf.</p>
    <p className="text-xs text-slate-600">= present simple. Not "I use to play golf."</p>
  </div>
</div>

- I **used to play** tennis. These days I **play** golf. *(not "I use to play golf")* <SayIt text="I used to play tennis. These days I play golf." iconOnly />
- We usually **get** up early. *(not "We use to get up early")* <SayIt text="We usually get up early." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't translate 'usually' as 'use to'">
  If the sentence is about *what you do now* — even if it's a regular habit — the right tool is the **present simple** (often with *usually, normally, every day, these days*). **Used to** only opens the door to the past.
</Callout>

## Side-by-side: "used to" vs "now"

The cleanest way to use **used to** is to pair it with a "now" sentence. The contrast does all the work.

| back then (used to + verb) | now (present simple) |
|---|---|
| Dave **used to work** in a factory. | He **works** in a supermarket. |
| Lisa's hair **used to be** very long. | Now it **is** short. |
| Helen **used to have** a piano. | She **doesn't have** one anymore. |
| We **used to live** near the airport. | We **live** in the city centre now. |
| I **used to read** a lot of books. | I **don't read** much these days. |

## A tiny "before / after" portrait

Look at each prompt. Say one true sentence with **used to** about your past self, then a "now" sentence in the present simple. The contrast is the whole point.

1. **Food** — "When I was younger, I **used to** ___ , but now I ___ ."
2. **A hobby you stopped** — "I **used to** ___ , but I don't anymore."
3. **A place you lived** — "We **used to** ___ , but we ___ now."
4. **Something you didn't like as a child** — "I **didn't use to** ___ , but I do now."
5. **Hair / glasses / a pet** — "I **used to** ___ , but ___ ."
6. **A school subject** — "I **used to** ___ at school, but I ___ ."

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Tell a 5-sentence "**then and now**" story about yourself. Use **used to** at least three times — once for a habit, once for a state (*be / have*), and once in the negative (*didn't use to*). End with one sentence in the **present simple** about how things are today. <br />
  *Example:* "When I was little, I **used to be** really shy. I **used to spend** every recess reading by myself. I **didn't use to like** big groups at all. My family **used to live** in a tiny town where I knew everybody. Now I **live** in a city, I **have** lots of friends, and I love being around people."
</Callout>
