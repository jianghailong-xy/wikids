So far, almost every sentence you've made has had a **subject** — *I, you, she, we...*. This lesson is different. When you tell someone **to do** (or **not to do**) something — or invite them to do it **with** you — English drops the subject entirely and just uses the **plain verb**: **Come in. Don't fall. Let's go.**

  Read it again and notice: **Come here**, **wait for me**, **don't forget**, **let's go**, **let's not walk**, **have a chocolate** — none of them say *you* or *we*. The verb just stands there alone, and that's what makes it an order, a warning, or an invitation.

## A — Do this! (the imperative)

When you tell somebody **to do** something, just use the **plain verb** — no *I*, no *you*, no *-s*.

> **verb** + (the rest)

- **Come** here and **look** at this.
- I don't want to talk to you. **Go** away!
- I'm not ready yet. Please **wait** for me.
- Please **be** quiet. I'm working.

<SayIt text="Come here and look at this." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Please wait for me." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Please be quiet." />

<Callout type="info" title="Why no subject?">
  When you speak straight to someone, *you* is already obvious — you're looking right at them. English saves the breath and drops it.
</Callout>

### Friendly **Have ...** uses

The same plain-verb shape powers two warm everyday phrases:

| What you say | What you mean |
|---|---|
| Bye! **Have** a good holiday! / **Have** a nice time! / **Have** a good flight! / **Have** fun! | I hope you have a good holiday etc. |
| '**Have** a chocolate.' &nbsp; 'Oh, thanks.' | Would you like a chocolate? |

<SayIt text="Have a good holiday!" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Have a nice time!" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Have a chocolate." />

## B — Don't do that! (the negative imperative)

To tell someone **not to do** something, put **don't** in front of the plain verb. That's it — no *do*, no *does*, no *am/is/are*.

> **don't** + **verb**

- Be careful! **Don't fall.**
- Please **don't go.** Stay here with me.
- Be here on time. **Don't be late.**

<SayIt text="Be careful! Don't fall." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Please don't go." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Don't be late." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Tiny trap: don't be">
  **Be** is still a verb, so it follows the same rule. ✅ **Don't be late.** &nbsp; ❌ Don't late. &nbsp; ❌ Aren't late.
</Callout>

## C — Let's do this! (inviting people to join you)

When you want **other people to do something with you**, start the sentence with **Let's** (= *let us*). It turns an order into an invitation.

> **Let's** + **verb** &nbsp;= *you and I do this together*

- It's a nice day. **Let's go** out. *(= you and I can go out)*
- Come on! **Let's dance.** *(= you and I can dance)*
- Are you ready? **Let's go.**
- **Let's have** fish for dinner tonight.
- A: Shall we go out tonight? &nbsp; B: No, I'm tired. **Let's stay** at home.

<SayIt text="It's a nice day. Let's go out." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Come on! Let's dance." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Let's stay at home." />

### The negative — Let's not ...

To say *let's NOT do it together*, use **Let's not** + plain verb.

- It's cold. **Let's not go** out. Let's stay at home.
- **Let's not have** fish for dinner tonight. Let's have chicken.

<SayIt text="It's cold. Let's not go out." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Let's not have fish tonight. Let's have chicken." />

## Reply with **No, don't ...** or **No, let's not ...**

When someone offers to do something *for you*, say **No, don't ...** (it's just one person — them). When someone suggests doing it *together*, say **No, let's not ...** (it's both of you).

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Imagine you're packing for a weekend trip with a friend. Say **six short lines** out loud — at least **two imperatives** (*Pack your toothbrush. Be ready by eight.*), **two negatives** (*Don't forget your charger. Don't be late.*), and **two Let's ...** (*Let's leave at nine. Let's not take the big suitcase.*). Try saying them in a row, like one side of a real conversation. <br />
  *Example starter:* "**Be ready by eight.** Don't forget your passport. Pack a jumper — it's cold there. Let's leave early. Let's not take a taxi — let's get the train. And have fun!"
