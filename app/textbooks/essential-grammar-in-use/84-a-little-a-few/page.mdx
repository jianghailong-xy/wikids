Two pairs of small-amount words — and the tiny word **a** changes the meaning. **a little** and **a few** = *some, but not much / not many* (a positive feeling: there's a bit). **little** and **few** (no *a*) = *nearly nothing, nearly no one* (a negative feeling: there's hardly any). Same words, opposite mood — all controlled by one little article.

## Part C — *little* / *few* (without *a*) = nearly nothing / nearly no one

Drop the **a** and the meaning flips. The amount is now **almost zero**:

- There was **little food** in the fridge. It was **nearly empty**.
- There were **few people** in the theatre. It was **nearly empty**.
- Dan is very thin because he eats **very little**. *(= nearly nothing)*
- Your English is very good. You make **very few mistakes**.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use very for emphasis">
  In real speech, *little* and *few* (without *a*) almost always come with **very** in front: *very little money, very few people*. Adding *very* makes the 'nearly nothing' feel even stronger.
</Callout>

## Part D — *a little* vs *little*, *a few* vs *few*

The same nouns, opposite feeling:
