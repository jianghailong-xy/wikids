Meet Sarah. She goes to work every day, always there from **8.30 until 4.30**. So at 11 o'clock now, **Sarah is at work**. At 11 o'clock yesterday, Sarah **was** at work. And at 11 o'clock tomorrow? Easy bet — **Sarah will be at work**. That tiny word **will** is how English steps into the future.

## The shape: will + infinitive

**will** lives next to the bare verb (no *to*, no *-s*, no *-ing*). It looks the same for everyone — *I*, *you*, *we*, *they*, *he*, *she*, *it* all share one **will**.

### The two tiny short forms

In real speech, almost nobody says *I will* or *will not* in full. They squash:

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">'ll = will</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">I'll · you'll · we'll · they'll · he'll · she'll · it'll</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600 italic">"I'll be at home." · "She'll come."</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">won't = will not</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">I won't · you won't · she won't · they won't</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600 italic">"I won't be here tomorrow." · "It won't rain."</p>
  </div>
</div>

<SayIt text="I'll be at home this evening." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It won't rain tomorrow." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Will you be at home?" />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't add an -s to he/she/it">
  **will** never changes shape. Same for everyone. <br />
  ✅ She **will be** late. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ She **wills be** late. <br />
  ✅ He'**ll come**. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ He'**ll comes**.
</Callout>

## When we reach for 'will' — for the future

Use **will** when you talk about **the future** — tomorrow, next week, this evening, in five minutes. Especially for things you **expect**, **predict**, or **decide right now**.

- Sue travels a lot. Today she is in Madrid. Tomorrow she'**ll be** in Rome. Next week she'**ll be** in Tokyo.
- You can call me this evening. I'**ll be** at home.
- Leave the old bread in the garden. The birds **will eat** it.
- We'**ll** probably **go** out this evening.
- **Will** you **be** at home this evening?
- I **won't be** here tomorrow. *(= I will not be here)*
- Don't drink coffee before bed. You **won't sleep**.

### The 'I think ... will' move

A huge slice of everyday English is *opinions about the future*. We wrap them with **I think** + something + **will**:

- **I think** Kelly **will pass** the exam.
- **Do you think** the exam **will be** difficult?

For the negative, English does a tiny dance. We move the *not* to **think**, not to **will**:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">say it like this</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">I <strong>don't think</strong> it <strong>will rain</strong> this afternoon.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">not like this</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900 line-through">I think it won't rain this afternoon.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="tip" title="One-line rule for opinions">
  **I think + will** for what you expect. **I don't think + will** for what you don't expect. The *not* always rides on **think**. <br />
  ✅ I don't think she **will come**. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I think she **won't come**.
</Callout>

## When NOT to use 'will'

Here's the catch: if a plan is **already arranged** (you've decided, booked, written it on the calendar), English uses **going to ...** or the **present continuous** — not *will*.

| ✅ already arranged | ❌ not 'will' |
|---|---|
| We'**re going** to the cinema on Saturday. *(tickets bought)* | We will go to the cinema on Saturday. |
| I'**m not working** tomorrow. *(day off booked)* | I won't work tomorrow. |
| **Are** you **going to do** the exam? *(it's on your schedule)* | Will you do the exam? |

<Callout type="info" title="Quick rule of thumb">
  → If the plan **lives in your calendar already**, say **I'm doing …** or **I'm going to …**. <br />
  → If you're **predicting, expecting, or deciding on the spot**, say **I'll …**.
</Callout>

## A quieter cousin: 'shall'

There's one more word that does almost the same job as **will**: **shall**. But it has a strict rule — it only goes with **I** and **we**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">okay — I / we</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I <strong>shall be</strong> late tomorrow.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">= I will (I'll) be late tomorrow.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I think we <strong>shall win</strong>.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">= I think we will (we'll) win.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">never — you / they / he / she / it</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900 line-through">Tom shall be late.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">→ Tom <strong>will be</strong> late. ✅</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900 line-through">They shall come.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-600">→ They <strong>will come</strong>. ✅</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="tip" title="When in doubt, say 'will'">
  In modern English, **will** is the safe default for everybody. **Shall** still sounds natural with **I** and **we** (especially in offers — *Shall we go?*), but you'll never be wrong if you simply say *I will / we will*. Just don't put it next to **you**, **he**, **she**, **it**, or **they**.
</Callout>
