A whole family of position-prepositions for **placing one thing relative to another** — perfect for describing rooms, photos, and where everybody's standing.

## Part A — *next to / beside / between / in front of / behind*

Picture five people standing for a photo:

```
        E
A   B   C
        D
```

- A is **next to** B. *or* &nbsp; A is **beside** B.
- B is **between** A and C.
- D is **in front of** B.
- E is **behind** B.

Three more spatial words for the same scene:

- A is **on the left**.
- C is **on the right**.
- B is **in the middle** (of the group).

<Callout type="tip" title="next to vs beside">
  *next to* and *beside* mean the same thing — pick whichever feels right. *Beside* sounds slightly more bookish; *next to* is the everyday choice.
</Callout>

## Part B — *opposite* vs *in front of*

Easy to mix up. **opposite** = facing each other (across a table, across a train aisle). **in front of** = the same direction, one ahead of the other.

## Part C — *by* (= *next to* / *beside*)

A short, friendly word for "right next to". Common in a cosy fixed phrases:

- Our house is **by the sea**. *(= beside the sea)*
- Who is that man standing **by the window**?
- If you feel cold, why don't you sit **by the fire**?

<SayIt text="Our house is by the sea." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Sit by the fire." />

## Part D — *under*

**under** = below, lower than (and often inside or covered):

- The cat is **under the table**.
- The girl is standing **under a tree**.
- I'm wearing a jacket **under my coat**.

## Part E — *above* and *below*

**above** = higher than. **below** = lower than. Think of a horizontal line — A sits over it, B sits beneath.

- A is **above the line**. *(= higher than the line)*
- B is **below the line**. *(= lower than the line)*
- The pictures are **above the shelves**.
- The shelves are **below the pictures**.

<Callout type="info" title="above / below vs over / under">
  **above / below** — usually with no contact (higher / lower than): *the lamp above the table*. <br />
  **over / under** — often suggests contact or covering: *a coat over a chair*, *a cat under the table*. <br />
  In many sentences either pair works. Don't sweat it — the textbook usage is your safe bet.
</Callout>

## Now you try

Look around you and finish four sentences:

1. *I am sitting **next to** ___ .*
2. *There is something **behind** me — ___ .*
3. *On my left is ___ , on my right is ___ .*
4. *The light is **above** ___ .*

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  **next to / beside / by** = right alongside. **between** A **and** B. **in front of** = ahead, same direction. **opposite** = face-to-face. **behind** = at the back. **on the left / right**, **in the middle**. **under** for under-and-covered, **above / below** for higher / lower without contact.
</Callout>
