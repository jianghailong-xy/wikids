Where the sun rises. How a cat behaves at 3pm. For all of that, English uses a different tool: the **present simple**.

## The big rule: he / she / it adds -s

Every present simple verb has only **two** shapes. Pick the subject, then either keep the verb plain or add **-s**:

| Subject | Verb |
|---|---|
| **I / we / you / they** | read &nbsp; like &nbsp; work &nbsp; live &nbsp; watch |
| **He / she / it** | read**s** &nbsp; like**s** &nbsp; work**s** &nbsp; live**s** &nbsp; watch**es** |

That's it. One subject, one ending. It feels small, but **forgetting the -s** is one of the most common slips for English learners — and one native speakers catch instantly.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't drop the -s">
  ✅ My brother **works** in a bank. <br />
  ❌ My brother **work** in a bank. <br />
  ✅ Lucy **lives** in London. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Lucy **live** in London.
</Callout>

## Spelling: how to add the -s the right way

Most verbs just take a plain **-s** on the end (*reads*, *likes*, *works*, *lives*). But three little tricks are worth knowing.

### Rule 1 — verb ends in **-s**, **-sh**, or **-ch**? Add **-es**.

<Contraction
  caption="watch → watches"
  segments={[
    { keep: "wat" },
    { swap: "ch", to: "ches" },
  ]}
/>

Same trick for **pass → passes**, **finish → finishes**, **wash → washes**, and **teach → teaches**. The extra **e** gives your tongue an easy place to land.

### Rule 2 — verb ends in **consonant + y**? Swap the **y** for **ies**.

<Contraction
  caption="study → studies"
  segments={[
    { keep: "stud" },
    { swap: "y", to: "ies" },
  ]}
/>

Same with **try → tries**, **fly → flies**, **cry → cries**.

<Callout type="warning" title="Vowel + y stays put">
  Only swap the **y** when there's a **consonant** before it. After a **vowel**, just add a normal **-s**: ✅ *play → plays*, ✅ *enjoy → enjoys*, ✅ *say → says*.
</Callout>

### Rule 3 — three little troublemakers

A handful of common verbs don't behave. The good news: there are only three you really need to know now.

## When to use it: things that are true *in general*

The present simple is your "anytime, all the time, that's just how it is" tense. We use it for:

- **Habits and routines** — *I get up at 7. He plays football on Sundays.*
- **Things that are always true** — *The sun rises in the east. Water boils at 100°C.*
- **Jobs and where people live** — *She works in a hospital. They live in Tokyo.*
- **Likes and dislikes** — *I like big cities. We don't like cold weather.*

## "Always", "never", and friends

When we want to say *how often*, we drop a tiny adverb in front of the verb: **always · usually · often · sometimes · never**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-gradient-to-r from-violet-50 to-fuchsia-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <p className="mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">
    How often? — from 100% to 0%
  </p>
  <div className="flex flex-wrap items-center justify-center gap-1 text-center sm:gap-2">
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-violet-200 px-3 py-1 text-sm font-bold text-violet-900">always</span>
    <span className="text-violet-400">›</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-violet-100 px-3 py-1 text-sm font-bold text-violet-800">usually</span>
    <span className="text-violet-400">›</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-violet-100 px-3 py-1 text-sm font-bold text-violet-800">often</span>
    <span className="text-violet-400">›</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-violet-100 px-3 py-1 text-sm font-bold text-violet-800">sometimes</span>
    <span className="text-violet-400">›</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-violet-200 px-3 py-1 text-sm font-bold text-violet-900">never</span>
  </div>
</div>

The word order is small but firm: **subject → adverb → verb**.

<Callout type="info" title="The adverb sits BEFORE the verb">
  ✅ Sue **always gets** to work early. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Sue gets always to work early. <br />
  ✅ I **never eat** breakfast. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I eat never breakfast. <br />
  ✅ Mark **usually plays** football on Sundays. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Mark plays usually football on Sundays.
</Callout>

Listen — notice how the adverb hugs the verb:

<SayIt text="Sue always gets to work early." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I never eat breakfast." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="We often go away at weekends." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I sometimes walk to work, but not very often." />
