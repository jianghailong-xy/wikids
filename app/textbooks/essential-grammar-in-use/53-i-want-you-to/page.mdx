In lesson 52 you saw *I want to do something*. Today the verb tells **somebody else** to do it: *I want **you** to do something. I told **Sam** to wait. She asked **me** to lend her money.* The shape is locked: **verb + person + to + verb**. We'll also meet two oddballs that break the rule — **make** and **let** — which take a person but **no** *to*. And the friendly *Let's …* shape that invites someone to do something **with** you.

## Part A — *I want you to do something*

The shape is: **verb + person + to + verb**. The person sits **between** the two verbs. The to + verb is what you want them to do.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50">
  <div className="bg-amber-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-900">verb + person + to + verb</div>
  <div className="p-5 text-center">
    <p className="text-lg font-semibold text-amber-900">I want &nbsp;<span className="rounded bg-rose-100 px-2">you / Sarah / her</span>&nbsp; to &nbsp;<span className="rounded bg-emerald-100 px-2">be</span>&nbsp; happy.</p>
  </div>
</div>

- I **want you to be** happy. *(not I want that you are happy)*
- They didn't **want anybody to know** their secret.
- Do you **want me to lend** you some money?

*Would like* works the exact same way:

- **Would you like me to lend** you some money?

<Callout type="warning" title="The Romance-language trap">
  ❌ I want <span className="line-through">that you are</span> happy.<br />
  ✅ I **want you to be** happy.<br /><br />
  English doesn't use *that you are* / *que tu sois* / *che tu sia* after *want*. The shape is locked: **want + person + to + verb**.
</Callout>

## Part C — *I told you to* / *I told you not to*

To tell someone **not** to do something, just tuck *not* between the person and *to*: **person + not + to + verb**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">positive — to + verb</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">Jane: 'Wait for me.'</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-500">→ Jane <strong>told me to wait</strong> for her.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">negative — not + to + verb</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900">Paul: 'Don't wait for me.'</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-500">→ Paul <strong>told Sue not to wait</strong> for him.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## Part D — *make* and *let* (no *to*!)

Two verbs break the rule. After **make** and **let**, you use the person + **plain verb** — no *to*.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">❌ with to</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900 line-through">He makes me to laugh.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900 line-through">Sue let me to use her phone.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">✅ no to</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">He's very funny. He <strong>makes me laugh</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">At school our teacher <strong>made us work</strong> very hard.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Sue <strong>let me use</strong> her phone.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="Easy memory trick">
  *Make* (force someone) and *let* (allow someone) — these two are old-school muscle words. They don't need a polite *to* in the middle. Plain verb, straight from the source.
</Callout>

## Part E — *Let's …* (do something together)

**Let's …** = *Let us …* — a gentle invitation to do something **with** the speaker. Plain verb, no *to*.

- Come on! **Let's dance**.
- 'Do you want to go out tonight?' 'No, I'm tired. **Let's stay** at home.'
- **Let's not** argue. *(negative)*

## Now you try: about people in your life

Write **six** true sentences about people you know, using a different starter each time.

1. *I want my friend …* (to + verb)
2. *I told my brother / sister …* (to + verb)
3. *My teacher made me …* (no to)
4. *My parents let me …* (no to)
5. *Someone taught me …* (to + verb)
6. *My friend asked me …* (to + verb)

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — eight little instructions">
  Imagine you're directing the perfect day. Write **eight** sentences ordering, allowing, or forbidding people to do things. Use each of these starter verbs once: **want, ask, tell, advise, expect, persuade, make, let**. Watch the *to / no-to* split — *make* and *let* go without *to*. <br /><br />
  *Example:*<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;1. I'll **ask** my flatmate **to leave** the kitchen tidy.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;2. I'll **tell** the delivery person **to ring** the bell.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;3. I'll **advise** my sister **to take** her umbrella.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;4. I **expect** the bus **to arrive** at 8.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;5. I'll **persuade** my friend **to join** us for dinner.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;6. I **want** the day **to feel** unhurried.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;7. I'll **make** my brother **wash** the dishes.<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;8. I'll **let** my dog **stay** on the sofa for once.<br /><br />
  Eight commands. Six with *to*, two without. The whole lesson, in one orchestrated day.
</Callout>
