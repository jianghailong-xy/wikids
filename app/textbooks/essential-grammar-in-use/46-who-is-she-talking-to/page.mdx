In lessons 44 and 45 you built every question shape English has — flips, no-flips, *who/what* as subject and object. Today's lesson adds two finishing touches that catch a lot of learners out. **One:** in *who/what/where/which* questions, the little prepositions (*to, from, with, about, of, in*…) almost always float to **the end**, far away from the wh-word that introduced them. **Two:** if you want someone to **describe** something — its size, mood, weather, vibe — English has its own special question: **What's it like?** And the *like* in there isn't the verb you know.

## Trick 1 — prepositions float to the end

In statements, a preposition stays glued to its noun: *I'm talking <strong>to my cousin</strong>.* But the moment you turn that noun into *who* or *what* and yank it to the front, the preposition is left behind — floating at the **end** of the question.

### How to find the right preposition

You don't really pick the preposition — the **verb** does. Each verb already comes with its preposition partner. When you ask the question, just keep that partner where the verb left it.

## Trick 2 — *What's it like?* (asking for a description)

When you want someone to **describe** something — tell you about its size, quality, mood, weather — English has a fixed pattern: **What is/are/was/were … like?** Notice that *like* here is **not** the verb *like* (as in *I like coffee*). It's a preposition stranded at the end, just like the ones above.

  <div className="bg-amber-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-900">the description question</div>
  <div className="p-5 text-center">
    <p className="text-2xl font-bold text-amber-900">What &nbsp; is/are/was/were &nbsp; <span className="text-amber-700">[the thing]</span> &nbsp; like?</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-600">= "Tell me something about it — is it good or bad, big or small, old or new, friendly or unfriendly?"</p>

</div>

## Two *like*'s — don't mix them up

This is the trap that catches everyone at least once. *What's it **like**?* and *Do you **like** it?* sound similar but mean **completely different things**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">like = preposition (similar to)</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-amber-900">What's your new house <strong>like</strong>?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600">→ Describe it. <em>"It's big and bright."</em></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-xs italic text-slate-500">Asking for a <strong>description</strong>.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">like = verb (enjoy)</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">Do you <strong>like</strong> your new house?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600">→ Yes / No. <em>"Yes, I love it."</em></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-xs italic text-slate-500">Asking for an <strong>opinion</strong>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="The shape gives it away">
  Verb *like* sits **right after the subject** — *Do you **like** it?*<br />
  Preposition *like* sits **at the very end** — *What's it **like**?*<br /><br />
  If you can put a real **noun** in place of *like* (*…similar to what?*), it's the preposition. If you can swap it for *enjoy*, it's the verb.
</Callout>

## Now you try: the friend who just got back

Your friend has just come back from a long trip. Use **What … like?** to ask for a description of each thing — and one **Do you like …?** for an opinion. Read your questions out loud first.

1. The hotel → *…?*
2. The food → *…?*
3. The weather → *…?*
4. The people → *…?*
5. The cities → *…?*
6. The flight → *…?*
7. (opinion) Their favourite city on the trip → *Did you like …?*

Now flip it: ask **eight** questions about your friend's day, each one ending with a different preposition. Try to use these verbs:

- **talk to / about**
- **listen to**
- **wait for**
- **look at / for**
- **go to / with**

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — first day in a new town">
  You've just moved to a new town. Write **six** questions a curious neighbour might ask you — every one of them ending with a preposition or *like*. <br /><br />
  *Example:*<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;1. Where are you **from**?<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;2. Who did you move **with**?<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;3. What's your old town **like**?<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;4. What are the neighbours **like**?<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;5. Which school do your kids go **to**?<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;6. What are you most excited **about**?<br /><br />
  Six questions, six little tails. You've just upgraded every wh-question you'll ever ask.
