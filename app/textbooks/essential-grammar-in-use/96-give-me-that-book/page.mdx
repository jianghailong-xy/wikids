A small group of verbs — **give, lend, pass, send, show** (plus **buy** and **get**) — always involve **two** things: a *thing* and a *person*. English lets you put them in **either order**, but the little glue word changes:

- **give something *to* somebody** — *I gave the keys **to** Sarah.*
- **give somebody something** — *I gave **Sarah** the keys.* *(no preposition!)*

Same meaning, two shapes. Today's lesson is about picking the right one — and dodging the four little traps that break the pattern.

After each of these, English offers two structures.

## Part B — *give something to somebody*

The **thing** comes first, then **to + person**.

> subject + verb + **something** + **to** + **somebody**

| Setup | Sentence |
|---|---|
| That's my book. | **Give it to me.** |
| These are Sue's keys. | Can you **give them to her**? |
| | Can you **give these flowers to your mother**? |
| | I **lent my car to a friend** of mine. |
| | Did you **send the money to Kate**? |
| We've seen these photos. | You **showed them to us**. |

## Part C — *give somebody something*

The **person** comes first, then **the thing**. **No preposition!**

> subject + verb + **somebody** + **something**

| Setup | Sentence |
|---|---|
| | **Give me that book.** It's mine. |
| | Tom **gave his mother some flowers**. |
| | I **lent Joe some money**. |
| How much money did you | **lend him**? |
| | I **sent you an email**. Did you get it? |
| | Nicola **showed us her holiday photos**. |
| | Can you **pass me the salt**, please? |

### *buy / get + somebody + something*

The same two-passenger trick works for **buy** and **get** — but the *to*-version uses **for**, not *to* (because nobody is *handing* it over; you're doing it **on their behalf**):

- I **bought my mother some flowers**. = I bought some flowers **for** my mother.
- I'm going to the shop. Can I **get you** anything? = Can I get anything **for** you?

<Callout type="info" title="*to* or *for*?">
  • **to** when the thing physically goes from you to the person: *give / lend / pass / send / show + to.* <br />
  • **for** when you do it *on their behalf*: *buy / get + for.* <br />
  Wrong glue word = wrong sentence.
</Callout>

## Part D — four little traps

Both structures work — almost always. Here are the **four rules** that keep you on the right side of the line.

### Trap 1 — never *to + person + thing*

You can switch the order, but you can't **mix** the two shapes:

| ✅ Correct | ❌ Wrong |
|---|---|
| I gave the keys **to Sarah**. | I gave **to Sarah** the keys. |
| I gave **Sarah** the keys. | |
| Can you give the book **to me**? | Can you give **to me** the book? |
| Can you give **me** the book? | |

<Callout type="warning" title="The order is locked">
  Once you put the **person first**, drop the **to**. <br />
  Once you say **to (somebody)**, the **thing must come before it**.
</Callout>

### Trap 2 — *it* and *them* prefer the first structure

When the thing is **it** or **them** (a tiny pronoun), English much prefers the **thing-first, to + person** shape:

- ✅ I gave **it to her**. &nbsp; ❌ I gave **her it**.
- ✅ Here are the keys. Give **them to your father**. &nbsp; ❌ Give **your father them**.
- ✅ Show **them to us**. &nbsp; ❌ Show **us them**. *(awkward)*

<Callout type="tip" title="The *it/them* rule">
  When the thing is **it** or **them**, lock in **verb + it/them + to + person**.<br />
  *Give it to her. Show them to us. Send it to me.*
</Callout>

### Trap 3 — *buy* and *get* take *for*, not *to*

A classic slip. *Buy* and *get* follow the same two-shape pattern, but the *to*-version becomes **for**:

- ✅ I bought **my mother some flowers**. = I bought some flowers **for my mother**.
- ❌ I bought some flowers **to my mother**.
- ✅ I want to **buy you a present**. = I want to buy a present **for you**.
- ❌ I want to **buy for you a present**. *(mixing the two shapes — see Trap 1!)*

### Trap 4 — keep verb + thing + to + person in that order

Once you commit to the *to*-version, the order is **verb + thing + to + person** — never *verb + to + person + thing*:

- ✅ I'll lend you some money. &nbsp; ✅ I'll lend some money to you.
- ❌ I'll lend to you some money.
- ✅ Did you send me the bill? &nbsp; ✅ Did you send the bill to me?
- ❌ Did you send the bill me? *(no glue word at all)*

## Now you try — six tiny sentences

Picture your last day. Say each sentence out loud.

1. *I gave ___ ___ .* — tell what you handed to someone (person-first).
2. *I sent ___ to ___ .* — what did you send and to whom (thing-first)?
3. *Can you lend me ___ , please?* — ask politely for something.
4. *I bought ___ for ___ .* — a recent gift (with *for*).
5. *Pass me ___ , please.* — at a meal table.
6. Show a friend a photo. Say it twice: once with *it* and once with the noun (the photo).

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — the *it/them* drill">
  Pick five things on your desk right now (a pen, your phone, a glass…). For each, say a sentence where the thing is **it** or **them** and a person comes second. <br />
  *Give it to me. Pass them to her. Send it to him. Lend it to me. Show them to us.* <br />
  Doing this once locks in the rule: with *it/them*, the **to-shape** wins.
</Callout>
