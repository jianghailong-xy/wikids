You can already step back in time with **was / were** (Lesson 10). Now we'll meet the past tense's two main characters together: **I did** (past simple) for things that happened and finished, and **I was doing** (past continuous) for things that were already in the middle of happening. Most past stories need *both*.

Three little events **happened and finished** (the phone *rang*, he *stopped*, he *answered*) — but underneath all of them, one longer thing was already going on (he *was reading*). That's the whole picture.

<Callout type="tip" title="The two questions">
  • **What happened?** → past simple. *His phone rang.* <br />
  • **What was going on at that moment?** → past continuous. *He was reading.*
</Callout>

## Two tenses, two shapes

| Tense | Shape | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **past simple** | I **did**, she **rang**, they **played** | finished action — start + end inside the past |
| **past continuous** | I **was doing**, she **was ringing**, they **were playing** | action **in progress** at a point in the past |

The past continuous is built like the present continuous, but you swap **am/is/are** for their past partners — **was / were** — and keep the **-ing** on the verb:

- **am / is** + -ing &nbsp; → &nbsp; **was** + -ing &nbsp; *(I, he, she, it)*
- **are** + -ing &nbsp; → &nbsp; **were** + -ing &nbsp; *(we, you, they)*

<Callout type="info" title="The decision rule">
  Before you speak, ask yourself one question: <br />
  **Was the action finished, or was it in the middle?** <br />
  • **Finished →** past simple (*I played*, *the phone rang*). <br />
  • **In the middle →** past continuous (*I was playing*, *he was reading*).
</Callout>

## When two pasts meet: *when* and *while*

Most real stories have a **long thing going on** and a **short thing that happens** inside it. English has a beautiful little pattern for that:

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <p className="text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-indigo-700">the classic past pattern</p>
  <p className="mt-3 text-center text-base text-indigo-900">
    <strong className="text-emerald-700">past continuous</strong>
    &nbsp;<em>(the long, ongoing thing)</em>&nbsp;
    <span className="font-bold text-indigo-500">+ when +</span>
    &nbsp;<strong className="text-amber-700">past simple</strong>
    &nbsp;<em>(the short thing that happens)</em>
  </p>
  <p className="mt-4 text-center text-base text-indigo-900">
    Jack <strong className="text-emerald-700">was reading</strong> a book <strong className="text-indigo-500">when</strong> his phone <strong className="text-amber-700">rang</strong>.
  </p>
</div>

You can flip the order — the meaning stays the same:

- *Jack* **was reading** *a book* **when** *his phone* **rang**.
- **When** *his phone* **rang**, *Jack* **was reading** *a book*.

<Callout type="tip" title="Two helpers, one job">
  Use **when** with the *short* finished event. Use **while** with the *long* ongoing one. Both push you toward the same picture: <br />
  • *I broke my arm* **when** *I* **was painting** *my room.* <br />
  • *I broke my arm* **while** *I* **was painting** *my room.* <br />
  Both are fine — pick the one that sounds nicer in the sentence.
</Callout>

And watch what happens when **both** verbs are past simple — the meaning changes:

| Sentence | Picture |
|---|---|
| When the phone rang, Jack **was reading**. | He had already started reading *before* the phone rang. |
| When the phone rang, Jack **stopped reading** and **answered** it. | First the phone rang, *then* he stopped, *then* he answered — three things in a row. |

<SayIt text="Jack was reading a book when his phone rang." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="When his phone rang, Jack stopped reading and answered it." />

## Same scene, two angles

Each row tells the same little story twice — once as a finished action, once as an ongoing one.

| Past simple (finished) | Past continuous (in the middle) |
|---|---|
| Jack **read** a book yesterday. *(beginning to end)* | Jack **was reading** a book when his phone rang. |
| **Did** you **watch** the game on TV last night? | **Were** you **watching** TV when I phoned you? |
| It **didn't rain** while we were on holiday. | It **wasn't raining** when I got up. |
| I **started** work at 9 and **finished** at 4.30. | At 2.30 I **was working**. |
| I **saw** Lucy and Steve this morning. | They **were waiting** at the bus stop. |
| Kelly **fell** asleep while she was reading. | She **was reading** when she fell asleep. |

<SayIt text="Did you watch the game on TV last night?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Were you watching TV when I phoned you?" />

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch out for stative verbs">
  The same little group of "in your head / heart" verbs from Lesson 8 — **know, want, like, remember, understand, believe, mean** — still don't take **-ing**, even in the past. <br />
  ✅ I **knew** the answer. &nbsp; ❌ I was knowing the answer. <br />
  ✅ She **wanted** to go home. &nbsp; ❌ She was wanting to go home.
</Callout>
