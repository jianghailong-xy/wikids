Many adjectives in English don't travel alone — they come with a **fixed preposition partner**. *afraid* always pulls **of**; *good* always pulls **at**; *interested* always pulls **in**. The locked partner is part of the word — learn them as a pair.

> 🐕 He's **afraid of** dogs. <br />
> 🧮 I'm not very **good at** maths. <br />
> 💼 I'm **fed up with** my job.

## Part A — adjective + preposition

The everyday list. The bold preposition is the locked partner.

| adjective + prep | example |
|---|---|
| **afraid of … / scared of …** | Are you **afraid of dogs**? |
| **angry with** somebody | Why are you **angry with me**? What have I done? |
| **angry about** something | Are you **angry about last night**? |
| **different from / to** … | Lisa is very **different from her sister**. |
| **fed up with** … *(= had enough of)* | I'm **fed up with my job**. I want to do something different. |
| **full of** … | The room was **full of people**. |
| **good at** … | Are you **good at maths**? |
| **interested in** … | I'm not **interested in sport**. |
| **married to** … | Sue is **married to a dentist**. *(= her husband is a dentist)* |
| **nice / kind of** somebody **to** … | It was **kind of you to help us**. Thank you. |
| **be nice / kind to** somebody | David is friendly. He's always very **nice to me**. |
| **sorry about** a situation | I'm afraid I can't help you. I'm **sorry about that**. |
| **sorry for / about** doing sth | I'm **sorry for not phoning** you yesterday. |
| **be / feel sorry for** somebody | I feel **sorry for them**. They're in a difficult situation. |

<Callout type="info" title="Two faces of *angry* and *sorry*">
  **angry with** a person, **angry about** a thing/event. <br />
  **sorry about** a situation, **sorry for** a person, **sorry for / about** an action you did. <br />
  Pick the right partner for what comes after.
</Callout>

## Part B — preposition + **-ing**

A second rule that runs through every preposition you've ever seen: **after a preposition, the verb takes -ing**.

| chunk | full sentence |
|---|---|
| good **at** telling | I'm not very good **at telling stories**. |
| fed up **with** doing | Are you fed up **with doing the same thing every day**? |
| sorry **for** not phoning | I'm sorry **for not phoning you yesterday**. |
| thank you **for** helping | Thank you **for helping me**. |
| thinking **of** buying | Mark is thinking **of buying a new car**. |
| left **without** saying | Tom left **without saying goodbye**. *(= he didn't say goodbye)* |
| **after** doing | **After doing the shopping**, they went home. |

<Callout type="warning" title="Never *to + verb* after a preposition">
  ❌ *good at **to tell*** &nbsp; ❌ *thank you for **to help*** <br />
  ✅ *good at **telling*** &nbsp; ✅ *thank you for **helping*** <br />
  After **of / at / for / with / without / about / in / on / before / after** — verb ends in **-ing**.
</Callout>

The *not* trick: a negative -ing form puts *not* in front of the *-ing*:

- I'm sorry **for not phoning** you. *(= sorry I didn't phone)*
- Tom left **without saying** goodbye. *(= he didn't say goodbye)*

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  Adjectives lock to a fixed preposition: *afraid **of**, good **at**, interested **in**, married **to**, fed up **with**, sorry **about / for**, kind **of / to**, angry **with / about**.* <br />
  After **any** preposition, the verb ends in **-ing** — *good at telling, sorry for not phoning, thank you for helping, without saying*.
</Callout>
