Most of the time, the doer of the verb and the receiver are different people: *I see her*, *they invited us*. But sometimes they're **the same person**: *I cut myself, she enjoyed herself*. For that twist, English has a special set of pronouns that end in **‑self / ‑selves** — the **reflexives**. Today we meet all eight, plus their easy-to-mix-up cousin: **each other** (= the *other* person, not yourself).

## Mirror, buffet, beach

  When *the doer* and *the thing being acted on* are the same person, swap *him/her/them* for *himself/herself/themselves*. *He's looking at **himself*** = he is looking at the same person who is doing the looking — i.e., him.

- I looked at **myself** in the mirror.
- He cut **himself** with a knife.
- She fell off her bike, but she didn't hurt **herself**.
- Please help **yourself**. *(one person)*
- Please help **yourselves**. *(two or more people)*
- We had a good holiday. We enjoyed **ourselves**.
- They had a nice time. They enjoyed **themselves**.

<Callout type="warning" title="Singular vs plural you">
  *yourself* is for **one** person. *yourselves* is for **two or more**. English doesn't show "plural you" anywhere else — but here it does!
</Callout>

## Part C — *by myself / by yourself …* = alone

When you do something **on your own** (with no one else), use **by + myself / yourself / himself …**.

- I went on holiday **by myself**. *(= I went alone)*
- 'Was she with friends?' 'No, she was **by herself**.'
- We weren't working with a team — we did it **by ourselves**.

## Part D — *each other* (different people doing the same thing)

When **two (or more) people** do the same thing **to one another**, use *each other* — not a *‑self*.

## Now you try

Write **five** true sentences about you and the people around you.

1. *On holiday I really enjoyed ___ .*
2. *Sometimes I talk to ___ when I'm alone.*
3. *Yesterday I went to ___ by myself.*
4. *My friend and I see ___ every week.*
5. *Take care of ___ ! (Said to two friends.)*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — each other vs themselves">
  Two friends are at a costume party. Write **two** sentences about them: one with *each other* (they're looking at one another) and one with *themselves* (they're each looking in a mirror). Same scene, two reflexives, two completely different pictures. That's the whole lesson in one example.
</Callout>
