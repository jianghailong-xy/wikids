## The pattern: don't / doesn't + verb

In Lesson 1, we said "no" by adding **not** after **am / is / are**. But ordinary verbs (**work**, **like**, **drink**, **go**...) can't carry **not** on their own. They get a tiny helper: **do** or **does**.

> **subject** + **don't / doesn't** + **verb**

The helper depends on the subject — exactly the same split you already know from positives:

| Subject | positive | negative |
|---|---|---|
| **I / we / you / they** | work, like, do, have | **don't** work, like, do, have |
| **He / she / it** | works, likes, does, has | **doesn't** work, like, do, have |

Tap to hear all three:

<SayIt text="I don't drink coffee." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She doesn't like her job." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="They don't watch TV." />

<Callout type="info" title="Why two words for 'no'?">
  English needs a helper to attach **not** to a normal verb. **Do** is that helper for **I / we / you / they**, and **does** is the version that already carries the **-s** for **he / she / it**.
</Callout>

  **I do not drink coffee** and **I don't drink coffee** mean exactly the same thing. In speaking and most writing, the short form wins.
</Callout>

## The big trap: doesn't kills the -s

Here's the mistake every beginner makes — and the one rule that will save you. Once **doesn't** does the job of "no" + "third person", the verb behind it goes back to its **plain form** (no **-s**).

> **doesn't** + **plain verb** &nbsp;(not verb-**s**)

- ✅ Sarah **doesn't speak** Italian. &nbsp; ❌ Sarah doesn't speak**s** Italian.
- ✅ My car **doesn't use** much petrol. &nbsp; ❌ My car don't use much petrol.
- ✅ Paula **doesn't have** breakfast. &nbsp; ❌ Paula doesn't **has** breakfast.
- ✅ David **doesn't do** his job well. &nbsp; ❌ David doesn't **his job** well.

<Callout type="warning" title="Pass the baton">
  The **-s** has already moved into **does**. The verb behind it is just the bare form. Only **one** word at a time gets to carry the -s — like a relay baton.
</Callout>

Listen and compare:

<SayIt text="Sarah speaks Spanish, but she doesn't speak Italian." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Mark eats fish, but he doesn't eat meat." />
