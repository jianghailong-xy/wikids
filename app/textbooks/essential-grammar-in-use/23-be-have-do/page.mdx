You've now met every tense in the first half of this book — present, past, continuous, perfect. They all look different, but underneath, they're all built from just **three little engine words**: **be**, **have**, and **do**. Pick the right engine and the right shape of the main verb, and the whole sentence clicks together.

## Three engines, one job

Each engine pulls a different *shape* of the main verb behind it. Look at the shape of the second word — it tells you which engine you need.

</Callout>

## A — be + -ing  (continuous)

You already know this one well. **be** + **-ing** says the action is *in progress*: happening **now** (present continuous) or *was already going on* at a moment in the past (past continuous).

| present continuous | past continuous |
|---|---|
| Please be quiet. I**'m working**. | I **was working** when she arrived. |
| It **isn't raining** at the moment. | It **wasn't raining**, so we didn't need an umbrella. |
| What **are** you **doing** this evening? | What **were** you **doing** at 3 o'clock? |

<SayIt text="I'm working." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It isn't raining." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What were you doing at 3 o'clock?" />

→ See again: lessons **3–4** (present continuous) and **13** (past continuous).

## B — be + past participle  (passive)

Here's a new trick **be** can do. Pair **be** with a **past participle** (*cleaned, made, built, broken, born*) and the meaning flips: the subject **receives** the action instead of doing it. We call this the **passive**.

| passive present (am/is/are + p.p.) | passive past (was/were + p.p.) |
|---|---|
| I**'m** never **invited** to parties. | The office **was cleaned** yesterday. |
| Butter **is made** from milk. | These houses **were built** 100 years ago. |
| These offices **aren't cleaned** every day. | How **was** the window **broken**? |
| | Where **were** you **born**? |

<SayIt text="Butter is made from milk." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="These houses were built one hundred years ago." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Where were you born?" />

<Callout type="info" title="be + -ing  vs  be + past participle">
  Both start with **be**, but the *second* word tells you what's going on: <br />
  • **am working** → I'm doing it (continuous, **-ing**) <br />
  • **am invited** → it's done **to** me (passive, **past participle**)
</Callout>

## C — have + past participle  (present perfect)

**have / has** + a **past participle** ties a past action to the present — a result you can still see, or life-until-now. You met it in lessons 15–18.

- I**'ve cleaned** my room. *(it's clean now)* <SayIt text="I've cleaned my room." iconOnly />
- Tom **has lost** his passport. *(he can't find it now)* <SayIt text="Tom has lost his passport." iconOnly />
- Kate **hasn't been** to Canada. *(in her life so far)* <SayIt text="Kate hasn't been to Canada." iconOnly />
- Where **have** Paul and Emma **gone**? <SayIt text="Where have Paul and Emma gone?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="Two helpers love past participles">
  Both **be** and **have** can pull a past participle — but they mean very different things: <br />
  • **The window <strong>was broken</strong>.** &nbsp;→&nbsp; *somebody broke it (passive)* <br />
  • **I <strong>have broken</strong> the window.** &nbsp;→&nbsp; *I'm the one who broke it, and the result is now (perfect)*
</Callout>

## D — do / does / did + infinitive  (negatives & questions)

The simple tenses (*I work*, *she lives*, *we went*) don't bring their own helper — so when you want to say **no** or ask a **question**, English borrows **do / does / did**. The main verb stays in its **plain shape** (no -s, no -ed).

| present simple — negatives & questions | past simple — negatives & questions |
|---|---|
| I like coffee, but I **don't like** tea. | I **didn't watch** TV yesterday. |
| Chris **doesn't go** out very often. | It **didn't rain** last week. |
| What **do** you usually **do** at weekends? | What time **did** Paul and Emma **go** out? |
| **Does** Sam **live** alone? | |

<SayIt text="I don't like tea." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Does Sam live alone?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What time did they go out?" />

<Callout type="warning" title="No double endings with do / did">
  The job of the **-s** or **-ed** moves to **does** / **did**. The main verb goes back to plain. <br />
  ✅ She **doesn't go**. &nbsp; ❌ She doesn't goes. <br />
  ✅ It **didn't rain**. &nbsp; ❌ It didn't rained.
</Callout>

## Now you try: tell a tiny story

Use **all three engines** in five sentences about your day. Try to slip in:

1. one **be + -ing** (*I'm sitting…*, *I was reading…*)
2. one **have/has + past participle** (*I've already…*, *I haven't… yet*)
3. one **do/does/did** negative or question (*I didn't…*, *Do you…?*)
4. *(bonus)* one **be + past participle** passive (*I was born in…*, *My phone was made in…*)

<Callout type="info" title="Star challenge">
  Pick any three sentences from the practice exercises and **flip them**: turn a positive into a negative, a statement into a question, a present into a past. Each flip is a chance to ask: *which engine do I need now?* <br />
  *Example:* "Tom has lost his passport." → "**Has** Tom **lost** his passport?" → "Tom **hasn't lost** his passport." → "Tom **didn't lose** his passport." (different tense, different engine!)
</Callout>
