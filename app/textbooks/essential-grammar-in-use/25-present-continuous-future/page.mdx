In Lesson 3 you met **am/is/are + -ing** as the *live commentary* tense — *I'm working, she's reading* — for things happening **right now**. Today, the same little shape gets a second job. When you've **already arranged** something (a ticket is bought, a date is in your diary, a friend is expecting you), English uses the very same **am/is/are + -ing** to say what's coming up. *I'm playing tennis tomorrow.* No new grammar — just a new use.

## The big idea: it's already in the diary

Pictures help here. **Now** Andrew is sitting on his sofa with his diary open — he's not on the tennis court. But tomorrow at 2:50, he **is playing tennis**, because the appointment is **already arranged**. The plan exists *now*; the action arrives *later*.

## Same shape, two jobs

You already know how to build this — nothing new to memorise. Just stick a future time word (*tomorrow*, *tonight*, *next week*, *on Friday*) on the end and the meaning slides into the future.

| Subject | + be | + verb-ing | + future time word |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | **'m** | playing | tennis tomorrow. |
| **He / She** | **'s** | going | to the dentist on Friday. |
| **We** | **'re** | having | a party next weekend. |
| **You / They** | **'re** | meeting | their friends tonight. |

<SayIt text="I'm playing tennis tomorrow." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She's going to the dentist on Friday." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="We're having a party next weekend." />

<Callout type="info" title="Two clues do the trick">
  <strong>am/is/are + -ing</strong> by itself could be *now* or *later* — the listener can't tell yet. <br />
  Add a <strong>future time word</strong> (<em>tomorrow, tonight, next week, on Friday</em>) and it instantly means: <em>this is already arranged</em>.
</Callout>

## When does this future shape fit?

Use **am/is/are + -ing** for the future when **you** (or someone else) **have already arranged** it — there's an appointment, a ticket, a friend who's expecting you. Not for things you're just *thinking* about.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50/60 p-5 shadow-sm">

    <ul className="mt-3 space-y-1.5 text-sm text-emerald-900">
      <li>• Sophie <strong>is going</strong> to the dentist on Friday. <em>(she has an appointment)</em></li>
      <li>• We <strong>'re having</strong> a party next weekend. <em>(invitations are out)</em></li>
      <li>• I <strong>'m meeting</strong> Tom at 8 o'clock. <em>(we agreed on 8)</em></li>
      <li>• I <strong>'m not going</strong> out tonight. I <strong>'m staying</strong> at home. <em>(decided)</em></li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50/60 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">❌ just an idea, not arranged</p>
    <ul className="mt-3 space-y-1.5 text-sm text-rose-900">
      <li>• <s>I'm winning the lottery next year.</s> <em>(no plan can arrange that)</em></li>
      <li>• <s>It's raining tomorrow.</s> <em>(weather isn't an appointment)</em></li>
      <li>• <s>I'm being a doctor one day.</s> <em>(a wish, not a plan)</em></li>

## Asking and saying no — same flip, same n't

You already know these moves from Lesson 3. Nothing changes — just add a future time word.

- **Are you meeting** your friends tonight? <SayIt text="Are you meeting your friends tonight?" iconOnly />
- **What are** you **doing** tomorrow evening? <SayIt text="What are you doing tomorrow evening?" iconOnly />
- **What time is** Lisa **arriving**? <SayIt text="What time is Lisa arriving?" iconOnly />
- I**'m not working** next week. <SayIt text="I'm not working next week." iconOnly />
- Lisa **isn't coming** to the party next week. <SayIt text="Lisa isn't coming to the party next week." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="The 'do you' trap">
  In English, **arranged plans** take the present continuous — *not* the present simple. <br />
  ✅ <strong>Are you going</strong> out tonight? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Do you go out tonight? <br />
  ✅ I<strong>'m staying</strong> at home this evening. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I stay at home this evening. <br />
  ✅ Lisa <strong>isn't coming</strong> to the party. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Lisa doesn't come to the party.
</Callout>

## One exception: timetables take the present *simple*

There's one futureish situation where English flips back to the **plain simple**: trains, planes, films, concerts, programmes — anything on a **fixed timetable** that nobody personally arranged.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-indigo-700">present continuous — usually for people</p>
    <ul className="mt-3 space-y-1.5 text-sm text-indigo-900">
      <li>• I <strong>'m going</strong> to a concert tomorrow.</li>
      <li>• What time <strong>are</strong> you <strong>leaving</strong>?</li>
      <li>• We <strong>'re having</strong> dinner at 8.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">present simple — for timetables</p>
    <ul className="mt-3 space-y-1.5 text-sm text-amber-900">
      <li>• The concert <strong>starts</strong> at 7:30.</li>
      <li>• What time <strong>does</strong> your train <strong>leave</strong>?</li>
      <li>• The film <strong>finishes</strong> at 10.</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<SayIt text="I'm going to a concert tomorrow. The concert starts at seven thirty." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What time does your train leave?" />

<Callout type="tip" title="Quick rule of thumb">
  Ask: <strong>did a person arrange this, or is it printed on a timetable?</strong> <br />
  → A person arranged it (a meeting, a party, a dentist appointment) → <strong>am/is/are + -ing</strong>. <br />
  → It's a fixed schedule (trains, films, concerts, classes) → <strong>present simple</strong>.
</Callout>
