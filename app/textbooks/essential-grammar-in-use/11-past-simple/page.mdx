In Lesson 10 you learned **was** and **were** — the past of *be*. But what about all the **other** verbs? *I work, I go, I eat* — how do you say those things about **yesterday**? That's today's job. Most verbs gain a tidy little **-ed** tail. A handful of stubborn ones change shape completely. Let's meet both kinds.

## The big rule: regular verbs add -ed

For most verbs, the past simple is dead simple — glue **-ed** onto the end and the same word works for **everyone**: I, you, he, she, it, we, they.

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <p className="mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">
    one ending, every subject
  </p>
  <div className="flex items-center justify-center gap-3 text-2xl font-bold sm:text-3xl">
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-1 text-amber-700">work</span>
    <span className="text-amber-500">+ ed →</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-amber-600 px-3 py-1 text-white">worked</span>
  </div>
  <p className="mt-3 text-center text-sm text-slate-600">
    I worked · you worked · he worked · she worked · we worked · they worked
  </p>
</div>

Watch a sentence travel from **today** into **yesterday**:

| Now (present simple) | Then (past simple) |
|---|---|
| They **watch** TV every evening. | They **watched** TV yesterday evening. |
| I **clean** my teeth every morning. | This morning I **cleaned** my teeth. |
| Terry **works** in a bank. | Terry **worked** in a bank from 2005 to 2011. |
| It often **rains** here. | Yesterday it **rained** all morning. |
| We **enjoy** parties. | We **enjoyed** the party last night. |

<SayIt text="They watched TV yesterday evening." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="We enjoyed the party last night." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Yesterday it rained all morning." />

<Callout type="info" title="One shape fits all seven pronouns">
  No **-s** tricks, no **was/were** split. The past simple of a regular verb is **the same word** no matter who did it. <br />
  ✅ I **worked**. &nbsp; ✅ He **worked**. &nbsp; ✅ They **worked**.
</Callout>

## Spelling: three little tweaks

Most verbs really are just *verb + ed*. But three small spelling rules save you from common mistakes.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-3">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-4 text-center">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">already ends in -e</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-2xl font-bold text-rose-900">+ d</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">danc<strong>e</strong> → danc<strong>ed</strong></p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">liv<strong>e</strong> → liv<strong>ed</strong></p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-4 text-center">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">consonant + y</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-2xl font-bold text-sky-900">y → ied</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">tr<strong>y</strong> → tr<strong>ied</strong></p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">stud<strong>y</strong> → stud<strong>ied</strong></p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">cop<strong>y</strong> → cop<strong>ied</strong></p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-4 text-center">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">short word, one vowel + one consonant</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-2xl font-bold text-emerald-900">double it</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">sto<strong>p</strong> → sto<strong>pp</strong>ed</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">pla<strong>n</strong> → pla<strong>nn</strong>ed</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the y rule">
  The **y → ied** swap only happens after a **consonant**. If a vowel comes before the **y**, just add **-ed** as usual. <br />
  ✅ tr**y** → tr**ied** (consonant + y) &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ sta**y** → sta**yed** (vowel + y) <br />
  ✅ enjo**y** → enjo**yed** &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ ~~enjoied~~
</Callout>

## How -ed sounds (a tiny secret)

The spelling never changes — it's always **-ed** — but your mouth makes **three different sounds** depending on the verb. Tap each one:

| Sound | When it happens | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **/t/** (sounds like "t") | after sharp endings: k, p, s, ch, sh, f | wor**ked**, sto**pped**, finis**hed**, watc**hed** |
| **/d/** (sounds like "d") | after soft endings and vowels | clea**ned**, pla**yed**, ope**ned**, rai**ned** |
| **/ɪd/** (a whole extra syllable!) | after **t** or **d** | wan**ted**, star**ted**, nee**ded**, en**ded** |

<SayIt text="I worked, I stopped, I watched." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I cleaned, I played, I rained." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I wanted, I started, I needed." />

<Callout type="tip" title="The two-syllable trap">
  Only verbs ending in **t** or **d** add a syllable for **-ed**. **wan-ted** is two syllables; **wal-ked** is still one. Don't say "walk-ed" — it's just "walkt".
</Callout>

## The shape-shifters: irregular verbs

Now the bad news / good news. Some of the most common verbs in English are **irregular** — they don't take **-ed**. They change shape completely. The good news: there are **only about fifty** that you really need, and you'll meet them so often they'll stick.
