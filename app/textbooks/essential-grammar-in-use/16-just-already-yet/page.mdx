By now you've met the **present perfect** — *I **have** arrived. She **has** gone.* Today we're adding three tiny words that love to ride along with it: **just**, **already**, and **yet**. They're small, but each one tells a completely different story about *when* (or *whether*) the action happened.

<Callout type="tip" title="Three new words, one familiar tense">
  Every sentence above uses **'ve / 's + past participle** — the present perfect you already know — but each one carries one of three little time words: **just**, **already**, or **yet**. That's all today's lesson is about.
</Callout>

## #1 — just = a moment ago

**Just** points to something that happened **only minutes ago** — so recent it almost still feels like *now*.

It sits **between have/has and the past participle**:

> subject + **have / has** + **just** + past participle

| Without "just" | With "just" (= a moment ago) |
|---|---|
| Laura and Paul have arrived. | They've **just** arrived. |
| I've had dinner. | I've **just** had dinner. |
| He has gone. | He's **just** gone. |
| The film has started. | The film has **just** started. |

<SayIt text="They've just arrived." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I've just had dinner." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="He's just gone." />

<Callout type="info" title="How recent is 'just'?">
  Imagine the action finished **a few seconds, a minute, or a few minutes ago** — fresh enough that you can almost still see it happening. If it was an hour ago, **just** starts to feel a bit too big.
</Callout>

## #2 — already = sooner than expected

**Already** carries a small surprise. The action happened **earlier than you (or somebody else) expected**.

It sits in the **same spot** as **just** — between have/has and the past participle:

> subject + **have / has** + **already** + past participle

| Question / setup | Answer with "already" |
|---|---|
| What time are Laura and Paul coming? | They've **already** arrived. *(= you didn't expect them yet)* |
| It's only 9 o'clock... | …and Anna has **already** gone to bed. *(= earlier than I expected)* |
| Jon, this is Emma. | Yes, I know. We've **already** met. *(= no need to introduce!)* |

<SayIt text="They've already arrived." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Anna has already gone to bed." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="We've already met." />

<Callout type="tip" title="The 'already' face">
  When you say **already**, your eyebrows go up a little — there's a hint of surprise. *"You've **already** finished?"* You expected them to still be working.
</Callout>

## #3 — yet = until now (only for "no" and "?")

**Yet** is the opposite — it points at something that **hasn't happened so far**, but you expect it to happen **soon**.

Two big rules for **yet**:
1. Use it **only in negatives and questions** — never in plain positive statements.
2. **Yet usually goes at the very end** of the sentence.

### Yet in negatives — "haven't ... yet"

| Setup | Sentence with "yet" |
|---|---|
| Are Laura and Paul here? | No, they **haven't arrived yet**. *(but soon)* |
| Does James know? | I **haven't told him yet**. *(but I will)* |
| Silvia bought a new dress, but… | …she **hasn't worn it yet**. |

<SayIt text="They haven't arrived yet." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I haven't told him yet." />

### Yet in questions — "Have you ... yet?"

| Question | Possible reply |
|---|---|
| **Have** Laura and Paul **arrived yet**? | No, not yet. We're still waiting. |
| **Has** Nicola **started** her new job **yet**? | No, she starts next week. |
| **Have** you **worn** that dress **yet**? | No, but I will tonight. |

<SayIt text="Have they arrived yet?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Has she started her new job yet?" />

<Callout type="warning" title="No 'yet' in a positive statement">
  ✅ I **haven't** finished **yet**. &nbsp; ✅ Have you finished **yet**? <br />
  ❌ I have finished **yet**. &nbsp; *(if you've finished, use **already**!)* <br />
  Switch the verb to **negative**, make a **question**, or pick a different word.
</Callout>

  • **just / already** → between **have/has** and the past participle. <br />
  • **yet** → at the **end**, and only with **n't** or in a question.
</Callout>
