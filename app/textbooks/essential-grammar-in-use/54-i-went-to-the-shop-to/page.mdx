Most lessons so far asked **what** people are doing. Today we ask **why**. English has a tiny shape that answers it in three words: **to + verb**. *Why are you going out?* — *To buy some food.* That's the whole machine. Today we install three flavours of it: **to + verb** for purpose, the **to vs for** decision (verb takes *to*, noun takes *for*), and the *wait* family — three different *waits* that all live with little prepositions and *to*-verbs.

## Part A — *to + verb* tells us **why**

When you want to say **why** someone does something, just stick **to** + plain verb on the end of the sentence. That's the whole shape.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50">
  <div className="bg-amber-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-900">action + to + verb = the reason</div>
  <div className="p-5 text-center">
    <p className="text-lg font-semibold text-amber-900">Paula went to the shop &nbsp;<span className="rounded bg-rose-100 px-2">to get</span>&nbsp; a newspaper.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600">Why? <strong>To get a newspaper.</strong></p>
  </div>
</div>

- 'Why are you going out?' '**To buy** some food.' <SayIt text="Why are you going out? To buy some food." iconOnly />
- Catherine went to the station **to meet** her friend.
- Sue turned on the television **to watch** the news.
- I'd like to go to Spain **to learn** Spanish.

### *Money / time + to + verb*

You can also clip *to + verb* onto a **noun** like *money*, *time*, *space*, *room* — to say what the money or time is **for**.

- We need some **money to buy** food.
- I haven't got **time to watch** television.

## Part B — *to* + verb &nbsp; vs &nbsp; *for* + noun

Here's the trap. *To* and *for* both answer the why question — but they hate sharing. Pick the right partner: *for* needs a **noun**, *to* needs a **verb**.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">

    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">I went to the shop <strong>to get</strong> a newspaper.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">They're going to Brazil <strong>to see</strong> their friends.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">We need some money <strong>to buy</strong> food.</p>

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">for + noun</p>
    <p className="text-base text-violet-900">I went to the shop <strong>for a newspaper</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">They're going to Brazil <strong>for a holiday</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-violet-900">We need some money <strong>for food</strong>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="The classic mix-up">
  ❌ I went to the shop <span className="line-through">for get</span> a newspaper.<br />
  ❌ They're going to Brazil <span className="line-through">to a holiday</span>.<br /><br />
  Look at the next word. If it's a **verb** → use *to*. If it's a **noun** → use *for*. Don't swap them.
</Callout>

## Part C — the *wait* family

*Wait* is one of those verbs that loves prepositions and *to*-verbs. Three shapes, three meanings.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50">
  <div className="bg-sky-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-900">three flavours of wait</div>
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-sky-200 sm:grid-cols-3">
    <div className="bg-white p-4 text-center">
      <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">wait for + noun</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">Wait <strong>for me</strong>.</p>
      <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-700">Are you waiting <strong>for the bus</strong>?</p>
    </div>
    <div className="bg-white p-4 text-center">
      <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">wait to + verb</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">I'm waiting <strong>to go</strong>.</p>
      <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-700">Are you waiting <strong>to see</strong> the doctor?</p>
    </div>
    <div className="bg-white p-4 text-center">
      <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">wait for ___ to + verb</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">Wait <strong>for them to change</strong>.</p>
      <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-700">Waiting <strong>for the doctor to come</strong>.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

### When to use which

- **wait for + noun** — you're just waiting for the thing/person to arrive. *Wait for the bus. Wait for me.*
- **wait to + verb** — *you* are waiting so that *you* can do the verb. *I'm waiting to go.* (= I'll go when I can.)
- **wait for + somebody/something + to + verb** — you're waiting for **someone else** to do the verb. *I'm waiting for the lights to change.* (= the lights will change, then I'll go.)

## Now you try: a why-day

Write **six** true sentences about today using **to + verb** to say *why* you did each thing. Try to use a different verb in each one.

1. I got up at … *to …*
2. I went to … *to …*
3. I called / messaged … *to …*
4. I opened my computer / phone *to …*
5. I (will) go to … *to …*
6. I need some money / time *to …*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — to vs for vs wait">
  Write **eight** sentences about a single afternoon, mixing all three shapes from this lesson:<br /><br />
  • two with **to + verb** (purpose)<br />
  • two with **for + noun** (purpose)<br />
  • two with **money/time + to + verb**<br />
  • two with the **wait** family (wait for / wait to / wait for ___ to)<br /><br />
