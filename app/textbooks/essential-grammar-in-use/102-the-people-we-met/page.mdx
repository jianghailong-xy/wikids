In Unit 101 the joining word **who/that/which** was the **subject** of the second half (*the man **who** phoned* — *who* did the phoning). Today's relative clause is different: the joining word is the **object** — somebody else does the action.

> The man is carrying a bag. **It** is very heavy.<br />
> → **The bag (that) he is carrying** is very heavy.

The man is carrying *the bag*. The bag is the **thing being carried**, not the doer. When *who/that/which* is the **object**, English lets you **drop it altogether**.

<Callout type="tip" title="The drop rule">
  Look at the second half. Is *who/that/which* the **doer** (subject)? **Keep it.**<br />
  Is somebody else the doer, and *who/that/which* is the **receiver** (object)? You can **keep it or drop it** — both are fine.
</Callout>

## Part A — keep it or drop it

Spot the subject and the object in each pair.

| subject | verb | object | relative clause |
|---|---|---|---|
| The man | was carrying | a bag | → **the bag (that) the man was carrying** |
| Kate | won | some money | → **the money (that) Kate won** |
| You | wanted | some books | → **the books (that) you wanted** |
| We | met | some people | → **the people (who) we met** |

You can say either way — both sound natural:

- Did you find **the books you wanted**? &nbsp; *or* &nbsp; … the books **that** you wanted?
- **The people we met** were very friendly. &nbsp; *or* &nbsp; The people **who** we met were very friendly.
- **Everything I said** was true. &nbsp; *or* &nbsp; Everything **that** I said was true.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't say it twice">
  ❌ *The film we saw **it** was very good.* <br />
  ✅ *The film we saw was very good.* <br />
  English never repeats the object — *it* and the relative clause already point to the same thing.
</Callout>

<SayIt text="The bag he is carrying is very heavy." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="The people we met were very friendly." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Did you find the books you wanted?" />

## Part B — prepositions hang on the end

Sometimes the verb in the second half wants a little tail word: *to / in / at / about / for*. In English, that tail **stays at the end**.

| Two sentences | One sentence |
|---|---|
| Eve **is talking to** a man. → | Do you know **the man Eve is talking to**? |
| We **stayed at** a hotel. → | **The hotel we stayed at** was near the station. |
| I **told** you **about** some books. → | These are **the books I told you about**. |

<Callout type="warning" title="One *to/at/about*, not two">
  ❌ *the books I told you **about them*** <br />
  ✅ *the books I told you **about*** <br />
  *About* already points to the books — don't add *them* on top.
</Callout>

### Place + *where*

For places you can use **where** instead of dropping a *to/in/at*:

- **The hotel where** we stayed was near the station. &nbsp; *(= the hotel we stayed at)*
- The town **where** I grew up has changed a lot.
- I went back to **the restaurant where** we had dinner.

<SayIt text="The hotel where we stayed was near the station." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Do you know the man Eve is talking to?" />

## Part C — quick rewind: subject vs object

The subject relative clause from Unit 101 — the joining word is the **doer**, so you **must** keep it:

- I met a woman **who can speak** six languages. *(who is the doer)*
- Jack was wearing a hat **that was** too big for him. *(that is the doer)*

Today's object relative clause — somebody else is the doer, so the joining word is **optional**:

- The hat **(that) Jack bought** was too big.
- The woman **(who) I met** can speak six languages.

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  When *who/that/which* is the **object**, English lets you drop it: *the bag (that) he is carrying*, *the people (who) we met*. Prepositions stay at the end (*the hotel we stayed **at***), and *where* swaps in for places (*the hotel **where** we stayed*).
</Callout>
