Three quantity words for **'a big amount'**: **much** for *uncountable* nouns (*much money, much food*), **many** for *plural* nouns (*many books, many people*), and **a lot of** as the all-rounder that works with both. The catch: *much* sounds odd in **positive** sentences — there we usually swap to *a lot of* (*I drink a lot of coffee*, not *I drink much coffee*).

### *a lot of* — both kinds of noun

- We bought **a lot of food**. *(uncountable)*
- We bought **a lot of books**. *(plural)*
- Paula doesn't have **a lot of free time**.
- Did they ask **a lot of questions**?

### Singular or plural verb?

- There **is** a lot of **food/money/water** … *(uncountable → singular verb)*
- There **are** a lot of **trees/shops/people** … *(plural → plural verb)*
- **A lot of people speak** English. *(not speaks)*

## Part B — *much* in positives is unusual

We use **much** mostly in **questions** and **negatives**:

- Do you drink **much coffee**? ✅
- I don't drink **much coffee**. ✅

But in **positive** sentences, *much* sounds wrong — use **a lot of** instead:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">❌ unnatural</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900">I drink <s>much coffee</s>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">'Do you drink much coffee?' '<s>Yes, much.</s>'</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">✅ natural</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">I drink <strong>a lot of</strong> coffee.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">'Do you drink much coffee?' 'Yes, <strong>a lot</strong>.'</p>
  </div>
</div>

**many** and **a lot of** are happy in all sentence types — positive, negative, question:

- We **have many friends / a lot of friends**. ✅
- We **don't have many friends / a lot of friends**. ✅
- **Do you have many friends / a lot of friends**? ✅

## Part C — *much* and *a lot* without a noun

You can drop the noun and just say *much* or *a lot* on their own — usually after the verb:

- Amy spoke to me, but she **didn't say much**.
- 'Do you watch TV **much**?' 'No, **not much**.' *(= not often)*
- We like films, so we go to the cinema **a lot**. *(not go to the cinema much)*
- I **don't like him very much**.

## Now you try

Write **six** true sentences about your life.

1. *I drink a lot of ___ .*
2. *I don't have much ___ .*
3. *I don't know many ___ .*
4. *I have a lot of ___ .*
5. *I don't ___ much.* (= not often)
6. *How much ___ do you ___ every day?*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — the positive trap">
  Take five sentences you said today in positive form (not a question, not a negative). Did you use *much* anywhere? If yes, swap it for *a lot of* and read it aloud. *I have much homework* → *I have a lot of homework*. The new sentence sounds like a real English speaker; the old one doesn't.
</Callout>
