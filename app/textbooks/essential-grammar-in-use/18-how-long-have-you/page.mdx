Helen flew to Ireland on **Monday**. Today is **Thursday**, and she's still there — riding her bike past green hills and stopping strangers for directions. A cyclist asks her *"How long have you been in Ireland?"* and she answers *"Since Monday."* That tiny exchange hides one of the most useful patterns in English. Today we'll learn it — and the trap most learners fall into when they try to say it.

## Helen on the road

## The big idea: from "now" to "how long"

Look at Helen's situation. There are two true things you could say:

The first sentence uses **is** — a single moment, *now*. The second uses **has been** — a line connecting **then** (Monday) to **now** (Thursday).

<Callout type="info" title="The 'still true now' shape">
  Whenever a situation **started in the past** and **is still going on**, English picks **present perfect** — not present simple, not past simple. That's the shape: *one foot in the past, one foot in the present.*
</Callout>

## How long ...? — the question

To ask **how long** something has lasted, English uses **present perfect**:

  Helen arrived on Monday and it's now Thursday: <br />
  • She has been in Ireland **for three days**. (the size of the gap) <br />
  • She has been in Ireland **since Monday**. (where the gap starts)
</Callout>

## The trap: don't use "is" or "are" with **how long**

This is the single biggest mistake learners make with this pattern. If a situation **started in the past and continues now**, you cannot use the simple present.

| ❌ Wrong (simple present) | ✅ Right (present perfect) |
|---|---|
| How long ~~are~~ you married? | How long **have** you **been** married? |
| ~~I know~~ her for a long time. | I **have known** her for a long time. |
| How long ~~do you know~~ Lisa? | How long **have you known** Lisa? |
| ~~I'm learning~~ German for two years. | I **have been learning** German for two years. |
| How long ~~is it raining~~? | How long **has it been raining**? |
| ~~Vicky lives~~ in London all her life. | Vicky **has lived** in London all her life. |

<Callout type="warning" title="A quick test">
  Whenever the sentence answers the question **"how long?"** about something that's still going, your verb almost always needs **have / has** — never **am / is / are / do / does** alone.
</Callout>

## Two flavours of the pattern

English splits the present perfect into two close cousins, depending on what kind of verb you have.

### Flavour A — **state verbs** → present perfect simple

Verbs like **be, know, have, live, like, want** describe a state, not an action. With these, just use **have / has + past participle**:

| present simple (just now) | present perfect simple (since/for) |
|---|---|
| Dan and Kate **are** married. | They **have been** married **for five years**. |
| **Are** you married? | **How long have** you **been** married? |
| **Do** you **know** Lisa? | **How long have** you **known** her? |
| I **know** Lisa. | I'**ve known** her **for a long time**. |
| Vicky **lives** in London. | She **has lived** there **all her life**. |
| I **have** a car. | I'**ve had** it **since April**. |

<SayIt text="They have been married for five years." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I've known her for a long time." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="I've had it since April." />

### Flavour B — **action verbs** → present perfect **continuous**

Verbs like **learn, work, wait, watch, rain, read** describe an activity that goes on. With these, English prefers **have / has been + -ing** to stress that the activity is **still happening**:

| present continuous (right now) | present perfect continuous (since/for) |
|---|---|
| I'**m learning** German. | I'**ve been learning** German **for two years**. |
| David **is watching** TV. | He'**s been watching** TV **since 5 o'clock**. |
| It'**s raining**. | It'**s been raining all day**. |
| She'**s waiting** for the bus. | She'**s been waiting for ten minutes**. |

<SayIt text="I've been learning German for two years." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's been raining all day." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="She's been waiting for ten minutes." />

<Callout type="info" title="Same 's, different word">
  **He's** can mean *he is* **or** *he has* — context tells you which. Whenever **'s** is followed by **been** or a past participle (**known, lived, had…**), it's **has**. <br />
  • He **'s** tired. → he **is** tired. <br />
  • He **'s been** tired all week. → he **has been** tired all week.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge">
  Pick **one** of your sentences above and stretch it into a tiny three-sentence story.
