Lessons 44–47 taught you how to **ask** questions: flip the helper, summon *do/does/did*, send the wh-word to the front. Today we do the opposite. When a question gets **tucked inside another sentence** — *Do you know where Paula is?* — all that flipping goes away. The wh-word stays, but the **subject + verb** order goes back to normal. *Do/does/did* disappears too. And for **yes/no** questions, English has one more trick: glue **if** or **whether** in front.

  Look at Tom's first line. The direct question would be **What time *does* the post office open?** Inside *Do you know …*, three things happened: the helper *does* **disappeared**, the verb got its **-s** back (*opens*), and the word order went back to **subject + verb** (*the post office opens*, not *does the post office open*). That's the whole lesson.
</Callout>

## The big idea — questions inside lose their flip

A direct question has the helper **in front** of the subject (lesson 44). The moment that question slides inside another sentence — after *Do you know, I don't know, Can you tell me, I don't remember* — the helper goes back **behind** the subject. Statement order, statement feel.

  Inside *Do you know …*, the verb sits **after** the subject, just like in a normal statement. Don't keep the question-flip.
</Callout>

## Part B — *do / does / did* disappears

Direct present-simple and past-simple questions need *do/does/did* to ask the question (lesson 44). When the question moves inside another sentence, that helper **vanishes** — and the main verb takes back its tense:

- *does* → drop it, put **-s** back on the verb
- *did* → drop it, put the verb back into its **past** form

    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">Why <strong>did she go</strong> home?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">Where <strong>did I put</strong> the key?</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">embedded (do/does/did gone, tense back on verb)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">Do you know where <strong>he lives</strong>?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Do you know how <strong>airplanes fly</strong>?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I don't know what <strong>Jane wants</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I don't remember why <strong>she went</strong> home.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">I know where <strong>I put</strong> the key.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="The tense moves home">
  In *Where **does** he live?*, **does** was carrying the present tense — that's why the verb stayed plain (*live*). Once *does* is gone, the tense moves back onto the verb: *he **lives***. Same trick with *did*: the past was on *did*, so the verb was plain (*go*); when *did* leaves, the past goes back: *she **went***.
</Callout>

## Part C — yes/no questions need *if* or *whether*

Direct questions starting with *Is …? / Have …? / Can …? / Do …? / Did …?* don't have a wh-word to lead with. Inside another sentence, glue **if** (more common) or **whether** (a touch more formal) in front. Both mean the same thing.

    <div className="bg-white p-4">
      <p className="text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">embedded with if / whether</p>
      <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">Do you know <strong>if Jack is</strong> at home?</p>
      <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-700">Do you know <strong>if they've got</strong> a car?</p>
      <p className="mt-1 text-sm text-slate-700">Do you know <strong>if Ben can</strong> swim?</p>

    </div>
  </div>
</div>

- Do you know **if** they've got a car? &nbsp;*or*&nbsp; Do you know **whether** they've got a car?
- I don't know **if** anybody saw me. &nbsp;*or*&nbsp; I don't know **whether** anybody saw me.

## Now you try: a polite traveller

You're a polite traveller in a new country. Tuck each direct question inside *Excuse me, do you know …?* or *Can you tell me …?* — the polite shape softens any question.

1. Where is the train station? → *Excuse me, do you know …?*
2. What time does the next bus leave? → *Can you tell me …?*
3. Is the post office open today? → *Do you know …?*
4. How long does it take to walk to the centre? → *Do you know …?*
5. Did the morning train leave on time? → *Do you know …?*
6. Where can I buy a SIM card? → *Could you tell me …?*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — six things you don't know">
  Write **six** sentences starting with *I don't know …* or *I don't remember …* about your own life. Mix wh-questions and yes/no (with **if**). <br /><br />
  *Example:* "I don't know what time my brother is coming home. I don't remember where I put my passport. I don't know how the new washing machine works. I don't remember if I locked the door. I don't know whether it will rain tomorrow. I don't remember why I downloaded this app." <br /><br />
  Six humble admissions — every one of them swallowed a question without a single helper-flip. Now you sound like a native.
</Callout>
