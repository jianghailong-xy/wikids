Some sentences point at something you can already see — *the book is on the table*. But what if you want to **announce that something exists** — like a man on the roof, or seven days in a week? English has a special little phrase for that: **there is** and **there are**. They don't talk about a *place* — they say *something is here*.

## The big idea: announcing what's there

**There is** and **there are** introduce something new — they put it on the map. Think of them as a friendly *"Look! Here's something:"*

    <p className="my-3 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">There's a man on the roof.</p>

<SayIt text="There's a man on the roof." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="There's a train at 10.30." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="There are seven days in a week." />

<Callout type="info" title="Why two forms?">
  Just like **am / is / are**, the verb has to match what comes next. **One** thing → **is**. **More than one** → **are**. The word **there** never changes — only the **be** verb does.
</Callout>

## All four shapes — singular and plural

Every form follows the same little family. Here's the full set:

| | singular (one) | plural (more than one) |
|---|---|---|
| **+ statement** | There **is** a big tree in the garden. | There **are** some big trees in the garden. |
| **− negative** | There **isn't** any snow. | There **aren't** many people here. |
| **? question** | **Is there** a hotel near here? | **Are there** any restaurants near here? |
| **short answer** | Yes, there **is**. / No, there **isn't**. | Yes, there **are**. / No, there **aren't**. |

<Callout type="tip" title="Two ways to say no">
  **There is not** can shrink **two** ways: **there isn't** *or* **there's not**. Both are correct — pick the one that feels easy to say. <br />
  For plural, only one form: **there aren't**.
</Callout>

## Counting things — *How many ... are there?*

When you want to **count** something, ask with **how many** + plural noun + **are there**:

- **How many** players **are there** in a football team? — There **are** 11. <SayIt text="How many players are there in a football team?" iconOnly />
- **How many** people **are there** in your family? — There **are** five. <SayIt text="How many people are there in your family?" iconOnly />
- **How many** students **are there** in the class? — There **are** twenty. <SayIt text="How many students are there in the class?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="Word order trap">
  In the question, **are there** stays together — don't split it. <br />
  ✅ How many people **are there** in your family? &nbsp; ❌ How many people *there are* in your family?
</Callout>

## *there is* vs *it is* — the trickiest pair

This is where students slip up the most. **There is** and **it is** sound similar, but they do completely different jobs.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-emerald-50 to-teal-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">there is — first time</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">There's a book on the table.</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">= I'm telling you a book <em>exists</em> there. New information.</p>
    <hr className="my-3 border-emerald-200" />
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">Use **there is/are** to <strong>introduce</strong> something — to say it exists.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">it is — talking about it</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-amber-900">I like this book. It's interesting.</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">= I'm describing the book <em>we already know about</em>. (It = this book)</p>
    <hr className="my-3 border-amber-200" />
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">Use **it is** to <strong>describe</strong> something already in the conversation.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="The classic mistake">
  ✅ **There's** a book on the table. &nbsp; ❌ *It's a book on the table.* <br />
  When you're **first introducing** the book — its existence is the news — you must use **there's**, not **it's**.
</Callout>

## Side-by-side: introduce, then describe

Watch how a sentence often uses **there's** to put something on stage, and then **it's** to talk about it:

| First — *announce it exists* | Then — *say what it's like* |
|---|---|
| **There's** a train at 10.30. | **It's** a fast train. *(it = the 10.30 train)* |
| **There's** a lot of salt in this soup. | I don't like this soup. **It's** too salty. *(it = this soup)* |
| **There's** a new café on the corner. | **It's** very busy at lunchtime. *(it = the café)* |

<SayIt text="There's a train at 10.30. It's a fast train." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="There's a lot of salt in this soup. It's too salty." />

And one tiny one for *noises and weather* — these almost always start with **it's**, because they're describing, not introducing:

- *'What's that noise?'* — **It's** a train. <SayIt text="It's a train." iconOnly />
- **It's** raining. <SayIt text="It's raining." iconOnly />
- **It's** 9 o'clock. <SayIt text="It's nine o'clock." iconOnly />

## Now you try: describe your room

Look around the room you're in right now. Out loud, say **five sentences** about what's in it — three with **there is / there's** and two with **there are**. Then say **one** with **there isn't** or **there aren't**.

*Example:* "**There's** a window. **There's** a lamp on my desk. **There's** a cup of tea next to the laptop. **There are** two pictures on the wall. **There are** lots of books on the shelf. **There isn't** a TV in here."
