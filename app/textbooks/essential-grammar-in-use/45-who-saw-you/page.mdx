In lesson 44 every question used the **flip move**: helper to the front, subject right behind it. Today's twist: when the question starts with **who** or **what**, sometimes you flip — and sometimes you **don't**. The whole lesson hangs on one tiny test: is the *who/what* the **doer** of the action, or the **receiver** of it?

## Two questions, one street

Silvia spotted Paul in the street. From that one little fact, English can ask **two completely different questions** — and they sound similar, but they mean opposite things.

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">subject question</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-lg font-bold text-emerald-900">Who saw Paul?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600">— Silvia. <em>(Silvia saw him.)</em></p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-500">asking <strong>who did the seeing</strong></p>
  </div>
  <div className="text-center text-2xl font-bold text-slate-400 sm:my-0">vs</div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">object question</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-lg font-bold text-violet-900">Who did Silvia see?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-600">— Paul. <em>(She saw Paul.)</em></p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-500">asking <strong>who was seen</strong></p>
  </div>

<Callout type="tip" title="Same words, opposite meanings">
  *Who saw Paul?* asks about the **doer** (the person seeing). The answer is *Silvia*.<br />
  *Who did Silvia see?* asks about the **receiver** (the person being seen). The answer is *Paul*.<br /><br />
  Both questions came from one little sentence — *Silvia saw Paul* — but they zoom in on opposite ends of it.
</Callout>

## The big rule — flip only if who/what is the *object*

Here's the whole lesson in one sentence: **if the *who/what* is doing the action, no flip — the question already starts with the doer.** If the *who/what* is the receiver of the action, flip the helper as usual (lesson 44).

<Callout type="warning" title="The classic trap">
  ❌ <span className="line-through">Who does live</span> in this house? &nbsp;✅ **Who lives** in this house?<br />
  ❌ <span className="line-through">What did happen?</span> &nbsp;✅ **What happened?**<br /><br />
  When *who* or *what* is the subject (the doer), there's already a noun-thing in the front of the sentence — it's the *who/what* itself. There's nothing to flip the helper around.
</Callout>

## The somebody / something test

When you're not sure which kind of question it is, do this. Replace **who** with **somebody** and **what** with **something**. Now read the answer-sentence out loud.

- Where does *somebody / something* land? **In the subject seat (front)?** It's a subject question — **no helper, no flip.**
- Lands **in the object seat (after the verb)?** It's an object question — **flip with do / does / did**.

## Compare: same fact, two questions

Each fact below can be turned into a *who* question **and** a *what* question — one about the doer, one about the thing.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-slate-200 bg-white p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-slate-500">George likes oranges.</p>
    <p className="text-base text-slate-800"><span className="rounded bg-emerald-100 px-2">subject:</span> <strong>Who likes</strong> oranges? — George.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-slate-800"><span className="rounded bg-violet-100 px-2">object:</span> <strong>What does</strong> George <strong>like</strong>? — Oranges.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-slate-200 bg-white p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-3 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-slate-500">Jane won a new car.</p>
    <p className="text-base text-slate-800"><span className="rounded bg-emerald-100 px-2">subject:</span> <strong>Who won</strong> a new car? — Jane.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-slate-800"><span className="rounded bg-violet-100 px-2">object:</span> <strong>What did</strong> Jane <strong>win</strong>? — A new car.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="Listen to the verb">
  In the **subject** version, the verb keeps its full shape: *likes* (with -s for *who* = he/she-like), *won* (full past). <br />
  In the **object** version, *do/does/did* takes the tense and the verb goes plain: *like* (no -s), *win* (no -ed).
</Callout>

## Two contractions to know

When *who* is the subject and the next word is *is* or *has*, English squashes them together. Don't be fooled — they're still subject questions.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">What's / Who's = What/Who is</p>
    <p className="text-base text-sky-900"><strong>What's happening?</strong> <span className="text-sm text-slate-500">(= What is happening?)</span></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-sky-900"><strong>Who's coming</strong> tonight?</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">Who's = Who has (with got/done)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900"><strong>Who's got</strong> my key? <span className="text-sm text-slate-500">(= Who has got my key?)</span></p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900"><strong>Who's done</strong> the washing-up?</p>
  </div>
</div>

## who vs what — people or things

One final tiny rule: **who** asks about people (somebody), **what** asks about things, ideas, animals (something).

- **Who** is your favourite singer? <SayIt text="Who is your favourite singer?" iconOnly />
- **What** is your favourite song? <SayIt text="What is your favourite song?" iconOnly />

## Now you try: the detective game

Imagine something happened and you missed it. For each clue your friend gives, ask **two** questions — one **subject** question (about the doer) and one **object** question (about what was done / received). Read each pair out loud.

1. *"Somebody made a cake yesterday."* → **Who made…?** / **What did…?**
2. *"Somebody bought a present."* → **Who bought…?** / **What did…?**
3. *"Somebody invited Lisa."* → **Who invited…?** / **Who did…?**
4. *"Somebody took the photo."* → **Who took…?** / **What did…?**
5. *"Somebody gave Sam a card."* → **Who gave…?** / **What did…?**

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — the journalist's notebook">
  Pick any short news headline (real or invented). Then write **four** questions a journalist might ask about it: two for the doers (*who*-subject), two for the things (*what*-object). <br /><br />
  *Headline:* "A young scientist won a prize for her discovery."<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;1. **Who won** a prize? *(subject)*<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;2. **What did** she **win**? *(object)*<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;3. **Who gave** her the prize? *(subject)*<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;4. **What did** she **discover**? *(object)*<br /><br />
  Mix the two patterns until choosing between them feels automatic — that's the moment this lesson clicks for good.
</Callout>
