## The big idea: two ways, same meaning

To talk about the things you own, your family, parts of your body, illnesses — anything you "have" — English gives you a choice:

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-rose-50 to-pink-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">Way 1</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-3xl font-bold text-rose-900">have</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">I <strong>have</strong> a sister.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-sky-50 to-indigo-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">Way 2</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-3xl font-bold text-sky-900">have got</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">I <strong>have got</strong> a sister.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="They mean exactly the same thing">
  **I have a sister.** = **I've got a sister.** &nbsp;&nbsp; Pick whichever feels easier in the moment. **Have got** is a little more common in spoken British English; **have** is everywhere.
</Callout>

## Who uses what?

Just like **am / is / are**, the form changes with the subject. Watch how **have** turns into **has** for he / she / it:

| Subject | Way 1 | Way 2 | Short form |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I / We / You / They** | have | have got | **'ve got** |
| **He / She / It** | **has** | **has got** | **'s got** |

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't forget the s">
  With **he**, **she**, and **it**, **have** becomes **has** — in **both** patterns. <br />
  ✅ She **has** a car. &nbsp; ✅ She **has got** a car. &nbsp; ❌ She **have** a car. &nbsp; ❌ She **have got** a car.
</Callout>

## Asking questions — two flips

Each pattern has its own question form, too.

### Way 1 — add **do / does** at the front

This works just like every other normal verb. Put **do** (or **does** for he/she/it) at the start, leave **have** alone:

- **Do you have** a camera? <SayIt text="Do you have a camera?" iconOnly />
- **Does Helen have** a car? <SayIt text="Does Helen have a car?" iconOnly />
- **What kind of car does she have**? <SayIt text="What kind of car does she have?" iconOnly />
- **How many children do they have**? <SayIt text="How many children do they have?" iconOnly />

### Way 2 — just flip **have / has** to the front

Exactly the same trick as **am / is / are** in Lesson 2: grab **have** (or **has**) and lift it to the front.

- **Have you got** a camera? <SayIt text="Have you got a camera?" iconOnly />
- **Has Helen got** a car? <SayIt text="Has Helen got a car?" iconOnly />
- **What kind of car has she got**? <SayIt text="What kind of car has she got?" iconOnly />
- **How many children have they got**? <SayIt text="How many children have they got?" iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Pick a lane and stay in it">
  Decide **before you start the question** which pattern you'll use, then stick with it. Don't mix **do** with **got**. <br />
  ✅ **Do** you **have** a pen? &nbsp;&nbsp; ✅ **Have** you **got** a pen? <br />
  ❌ Do you have got a pen? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ Have you a pen?
</Callout>

## Saying "yes" and "no" the short way

Just like Lesson 2, the polite answer mirrors the verb of the question.

| Question | Yes | No |
|---|---|---|
| **Do** you have a camera? | Yes, I **do**. | No, I **don't**. |
| **Does** Helen have a car? | Yes, she **does**. | No, she **doesn't**. |
| **Have** you **got** a camera? | Yes, I **have**. | No, I **haven't**. |
| **Has** Helen **got** a car? | Yes, she **has**. | No, she **hasn't**. |

<Callout type="tip" title="Mirror the helper">
  The trick is to listen for the first word of the question. **Do/Does** → answer with **do/does** or **don't/doesn't**. **Have/Has** → answer with **have/has** or **haven't/hasn't**.
</Callout>
