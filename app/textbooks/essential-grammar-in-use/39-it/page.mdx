Some sentences have no real *doer*. *It's raining* — who is raining? Nobody. The rain is just falling. But English doesn't let a sentence start with a naked verb, so it slips a tiny placeholder into the empty subject seat: **it**. The same little word covers the **time**, the **day**, the **weather**, the **distance to somewhere**, and even *whole actions* (*it's nice to see you*). Today we learn when to reach for it — and the trap of forgetting it.

## The big idea: English needs a subject

Some languages are happy to say *Is raining* or *Is cold today*. English is **not**. Every English sentence needs a real subject sitting in front of the verb. When there's no person or thing to put there, English drops in a placeholder: **it**.

Use **it** for the clock, the calendar, and even "it's time to ...".

- What day **is it**? &nbsp; <SayIt text="What day is it?" iconOnly />
- **It's** Thursday. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's Thursday." iconOnly />

### Distance

Use **it** to talk about how far one place is from another.

- **It's** three kilometres from our house to the city centre. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's three kilometres from our house to the city centre." iconOnly />
- How far **is it** from New York to Los Angeles? &nbsp; <SayIt text="How far is it from New York to Los Angeles?" iconOnly />
- **It's** a long way from here to the station. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's a long way from here to the station." iconOnly />
- We can walk home. **It isn't** far. &nbsp; <SayIt text="We can walk home. It isn't far." iconOnly />

<Callout type="warning" title="far vs a long way">
  English is fussy here:<br />
  ✅ Questions and negatives → **far** &nbsp; (*Is it far? · It isn't far.*)<br />
  ✅ Positive sentences → **a long way** &nbsp; (*It's a long way.*)<br />
  ❌ It's far from here. *(say "It's a long way from here.")*
</Callout>

### Weather

This is where **it** shows up the most. **It** + a weather word = the sky right now.

- **It's** raining. &nbsp;&nbsp; **It isn't** raining. &nbsp;&nbsp; **Is it** snowing?
- **It** rains a lot here. &nbsp;&nbsp; **It didn't** rain yesterday. &nbsp;&nbsp; **Does it** snow very often?
- **It's** warm / hot / cold / fine / cloudy / windy / sunny / foggy / dark.
- **It's** a nice day today.

<SayIt text="It's raining." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It rains a lot here." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's a nice day today." />

## "It" vs "there"

Beginners often mix these up — they both look like little empty subjects. The trick is to peek at the **next word**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">it + verb / adjective</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-sky-900"><strong>It</strong> rains a lot in winter.</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-sky-900"><strong>It</strong> was very windy.</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">→ describing the weather <em>directly</em>.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-violet-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">there + is / was + a noun</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-violet-900"><strong>There is</strong> a lot of rain in winter.</p>
    <p className="my-2 text-base font-semibold text-violet-900"><strong>There was</strong> a strong wind yesterday.</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-600">→ introducing a <em>thing</em> (rain, wind, storm).</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="The one-second test">
  Look at what comes right after the gap.<br />
  → A **noun** (a storm, a lot of rain, a strong wind)? &nbsp; **there is / there was**.<br />
  → A **verb or adjective** (rains, is windy, was cold)? &nbsp; **it**.
</Callout>

## "It's nice to ..." — when "it" stands in for a whole action

Watch this clever trick. Instead of saying *To see you again is nice* — which sounds heavy and old-fashioned — English flips it around and uses **it** as a tiny stand-in for the action that comes later:

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-indigo-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
  <div className="grid items-center gap-3 text-center sm:grid-cols-[auto_1fr_auto] sm:text-left">
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-2 text-base font-semibold text-indigo-900 shadow-sm">It's</span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-2 text-sm text-slate-700 shadow-sm">
      easy / difficult / impossible / dangerous / safe /<br />
      expensive / interesting / nice / wonderful / terrible …
    </span>
    <span className="rounded-lg bg-white px-3 py-2 text-base font-semibold text-indigo-900 shadow-sm">to …</span>
  </div>
</div>

- **It's nice to** see you again. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's nice to see you again." iconOnly />
- **It's impossible to** understand her. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's impossible to understand her." iconOnly />
- **It wasn't easy to** find your house. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It wasn't easy to find your house." iconOnly />
- **It's dangerous to** go out alone at night. &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's dangerous to go out alone at night." iconOnly />

<Callout type="tip" title="Why does English do this?">
  English likes the **subject seat to be small and fast**, not heavy. Putting the long *to-action* up front feels clunky:<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;❓ *To get up early in the morning is difficult.*<br />
  So we drop **it** in front, push the action to the back, and the sentence breathes:<br />
  &nbsp;&nbsp;✅ *It's difficult to get up early in the morning.*
</Callout>

## Don't forget it!

This is the biggest trap in this lesson. Some languages let you start a sentence with the verb. English never does. **Always** put **it** in front.
