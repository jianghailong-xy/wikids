A comparative on its own says *more*; add **than** and you get the second half of the comparison: *older **than** Rome*, *more expensive **than** the Grand*, *taller **than** him*. Two extras you'll meet here: pronouns after *than* are usually **object** form (*than me, than him*), and you can pile **a bit** or **much** in front of the comparative for size — *a bit older* (small gap), *much bigger* (big gap).

  comparative + **than** + the second thing/person. *older than Rome, more expensive than I expected, taller than him*.

## Part A — *than* after a comparative

We use **than** to introduce the second half:

- Athens is **older than** Rome.
- Are oranges **more expensive than** bananas?
- It's **easier** to take a taxi **than** to take the bus.
- 'How are you today?' 'Not bad. **Better than** yesterday.'
- The restaurant is **more crowded than** usual.

## Part B — pronouns after *than*

In everyday English, **object pronouns** come after *than*: *than me / him / her / us / them*. The longer 'subject + verb' form (*than I am / than he can*) is also correct, but a bit more formal:

## Part C — *more / less than* …

You can use **more / less than** with numbers and amounts:

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">more than 50 ↑</p>
    <p className="text-base text-amber-900">A: How much did your shoes cost? £60? B: No, <strong>more than that</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">They have <strong>more money than</strong> they need.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">You go out <strong>more than</strong> me.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">less than 50 ↓</p>
    <p className="text-base text-sky-900">The film was very short — <strong>less than</strong> an hour.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-sky-900">He earns <strong>less than</strong> £1,000 a month.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## Part D — *a bit / much* + comparative

Want to say *how much* bigger / older / more expensive? Stick **a bit** (small gap) or **much** (big gap) in front:

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50">
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-sky-200 sm:grid-cols-2">
    <div className="bg-white p-3"><p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">a bit / much</p><p className="mt-1 text-sm text-sky-900">+ bigger / older / better / more difficult / more expensive + than …</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3"><p className="text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">small or big gap</p><p className="mt-1 text-sm text-sky-900">a bit = small gap; much = big gap</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- Box A is **a bit bigger** than Box B.
- Box C is **much bigger** than Box D.
- Canada is **much bigger** than France.
- Sue is **a bit older** than Joe — she's 25 and he's 24.
- The hotel was **much more expensive** than I expected.
- You go out **much more** than me.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the size">
  *a bit* and *much* go **before** the comparative, never after. ❌ *bigger a bit / bigger much.* &nbsp; ✅ *a bit bigger / much bigger*.
</Callout>
