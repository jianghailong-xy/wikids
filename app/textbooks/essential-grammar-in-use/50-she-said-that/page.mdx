In lesson 49 you learned to **tuck a question inside another sentence**. Today we do the same thing for **statements**. When you tell someone what another person said, English doesn't just copy the words — it shifts the verb back one tense (am → was, will → would, can → could). That's **reported speech**, and once you know the shifts, you can re-tell anything you've heard. Plus today: the **say vs tell** trap that catches everyone — *He said me* is wrong; *He told me* is right.

## Part A — the present-to-past shift

When you report what someone said, today's verb gets pushed back into yesterday's verb. Here's the full shift table.

<Callout type="info" title="And the pronouns shift too">
  When Clare said *"my new job,"* she meant **her** job — so when you report it, *my* becomes *her*. And when Steve said *"I'll phone you,"* the **you** was *me* — so the report becomes *he would phone me*. The verb moves a step back; the pronouns swing to the new perspective.
</Callout>

### The *have / has → had* shift, in detail

This one trips people up. *I have lost / he has gone* (present perfect) becomes *I had lost / he had gone* (past perfect) when reported.

- "I'**ve** lost my watch." → He said he **had lost** his watch.
- "My sister **has** gone to Australia." → He said his sister **had gone** to Australia.

## Part B — *say* vs *tell*

Both verbs report words — but they take different little partners. **Tell** always wants a person right after it. **Say** never does (or it uses *to + person*).

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">say (→ said)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-rose-900">He <strong>said</strong> that he was tired.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-500">(not He said me)</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-rose-900">What did she <strong>say to</strong> you?</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-500">(say <strong>to</strong> + person, never say + person)</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">tell (→ told)</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">He <strong>told me</strong> that he was tired.</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-500">(not He told that)</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">What did she <strong>tell you</strong>?</p>
    <p className="text-sm italic text-slate-500">(tell + person, never tell to + person)</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Two trapdoors, side by side">
  ❌ He <span className="line-through">said me</span> that he was tired. &nbsp;✅ He **told me** / He **said to me** that he was tired.<br />
  ❌ He <span className="line-through">told that</span> he was tired. &nbsp;✅ He **said that** / He **told me that** he was tired.<br /><br />
  Easy memory trick: **tell wants a person; say wants a fact** (or *to + person* if it must mention someone).
</Callout>

## Part C — *that* is optional

You can drop **that** without changing anything. Both versions are good English.

- He said **that** he was tired. &nbsp;*or*&nbsp; He said he was tired.
- Kate told me **that** she couldn't find a job. &nbsp;*or*&nbsp; Kate told me she couldn't find a job.

In quick speech, most people drop *that*. In writing or careful speech, keep it.
