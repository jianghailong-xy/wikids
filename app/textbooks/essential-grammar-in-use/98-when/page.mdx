**when** (and its time-cousins **before, while, after, until**) glue two halves of a sentence together — *one half says **when**, the other says **what happens***. Today's lesson has two parts:

1. **Position & comma** — *when* can sit at the **start** or in the **middle**. A comma marks the start version.
2. **No *will* after *when*** — even when you're talking about the future, the *when*-half stays in the **present**: *When I **get** home …* (not *when I will get*).

That second rule is the big one. Once it clicks, half a dozen "future" mistakes vanish.

## Part A — *when* at the start or in the middle

A *when*-sentence has two halves: a **time-half** (*when …*) and a **main-half** (*the rest*). You can put the time-half **first** or **second**:

  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">when at the start</p>
    <p className="text-base text-amber-900">**When I went out**, it was raining.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">**When you're tired**, don't drive.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900">**When Helen got married**, she was 25.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-600">→ Use a **comma** between the two halves.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">when in the middle</p>
    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">It was raining **when I went out**.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Don't drive **when you're tired**.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-emerald-900">Helen was 25 **when she got married**.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-xs italic text-slate-600">→ **No comma**.</p>
  </div>
</div>

The same trick works with **before / while / after**:

- Always look both ways **before** you cross the road. **=** **Before** you cross the road, always look both ways.
- **While** I was waiting for the bus, it began to rain. **=** It began to rain **while** I was waiting for the bus.
- He never played football again **after** he broke his leg. **=** **After** he broke his leg, he never played football again.

## Part B — *when* + future = *when* + **present**

The biggest *when*-trap: even when the action is clearly **in the future**, English uses the **present** in the *when*-half. The future tense (*will / 'll*) goes in the **other** half only.

> **When I get home this evening**, I'm going to have a shower. <br />
> *(not When I **will get** home)*

More examples:

- I can't talk to you now. I'll talk to you later **when I have** more time. *(not when I will have)*
- **When you come back**, we'll go shopping. *(not When you will come back)*

The same rule applies to **before, while, after, until**:

- Please close the window **before you go out**. *(not before you will go)*
- Rachel is going to stay in our flat **while we are away**. *(not while we will be)*
- I'll wait here **until you come back**. *(not until you will come back)*
- I'll do the dishes **after we have** dinner. *(not after we will have)*

<Callout type="warning" title="The locked rule">
  After **when / before / while / after / until**, never use **will**. Use the **present** — even if the meaning is clearly future.<br />
  ✅ *When I **get** home …* &nbsp; ❌ *When I **will get** home …*
</Callout>

### Why? A picture

The *when*-half is the **anchor in time** — it doesn't predict, it just points to *that moment*. The other half does the future-talking.

## Now you try — six time-sentences

Say each one out loud about your life.

1. *When I ___ tomorrow, I'll ___ .* — a future plan with *when*.
2. *I'll ___ before I ___ .* — something to do first.
3. *While I ___ , my friend ___ .* — two things at the same moment yesterday.
4. *I'll wait until ___ .* — what you're waiting for.
5. *When I was a child, I ___ .* — a true past memory.
6. *Don't ___ when you're tired.* — friendly advice.

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — three futures, no *will* in the time-half">
  Picture tomorrow morning. Say three sentences linked by **when / before / after**: <br />
  *When I **wake up**, I'll have coffee. Before I **leave** the house, I'll check the weather. After I **get** to work, I'll answer my emails.* <br />
  Three futures — but every *when / before / after* half stays in the present. That's the trick.
