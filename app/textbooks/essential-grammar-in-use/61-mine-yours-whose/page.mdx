In lesson 60 you met **my, our, your, his, her, their, its** — the possessives that **always travel with a noun** (*my book, her car, their parents*). Today's set is their stand-alone twin: **mine, ours, yours, his, hers, theirs**. They mean exactly the same thing, but they leave the noun behind. *That book is **mine*** = *That book is **my** book*. One pronoun does the whole job.

## Whose money is whose?

Joe and Sue are sorting the cash on the table. Listen for the noun-less possessives — *mine, ours, yours, his, hers, theirs*.

- It's **my** money. → It's **mine**.
- It's **our** money. → It's **ours**.
- It's **your** money. → It's **yours**.
- It's **his** money. → It's **his**. *(same shape!)*
- It's **her** money. → It's **hers**.
- It's **their** money. → It's **theirs**.

<Callout type="warning" title="The big trap — no apostrophe">
  ❌ *your's, her's, their's, our's* — never write any of these. The s just attaches: **yours, hers, theirs, ours**. Only **his** is the same in both columns.
</Callout>

## Part B — *my/your* + *noun* vs *mine/yours* (no noun)

### *his* — the special twin

*His* is the only one that does **both** jobs with the same shape.

- 'Is this **his** camera or **hers**?' 'It's **his**.'

## Part C — *a friend of mine / of his / of yours*

When you want to say *one of my friends*, English uses a special shape: ***a friend of + mine / yours / hers …***. Watch the trap — never *a friend of me*.

    <p className="text-base text-emerald-900">I went out to meet a friend <strong>of mine</strong>.</p>

## Part D — *Whose ... ?*

To ask **whose**, use *Whose* — with or without a noun.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">Whose + noun</p>
    <p className="text-base text-amber-900"><strong>Whose phone</strong> is this?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900"><strong>Whose money</strong> is this?</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-amber-900"><strong>Whose shoes</strong> are these?</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-sky-200 bg-sky-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-sky-700">Whose alone</p>
    <p className="text-base text-sky-900"><strong>Whose</strong> is this? — It's mine.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base text-sky-900"><strong>Whose</strong> are these? — They're John's.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="info" title="is or are?">
  Use *is* with a singular noun: *Whose phone **is** this?* Use *are* with a plural: *Whose shoes **are** these?* Match the verb to the thing being asked about.
</Callout>
