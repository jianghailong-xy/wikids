You've already met **will** for predictions about tomorrow ("It will rain"). Today you'll meet two friendlier jobs that **will** does every single day in real conversation:

- **I'll ...** — the moment you decide something or offer to help.
- **Shall I ...? / Shall we ...?** — when you want to ask *"Is this a good idea?"*

Get these two right and your English suddenly sounds like a real chat — not a textbook.

## A. I'll ... = I decide right now

When you decide something **at the moment of speaking** — an offer, a promise, a sudden idea — English uses **I'll** (= **I will**). The decision is fresh; it didn't exist five seconds ago.

## I think I'll ... / I don't think I'll ...

When the decision is fresh **and** you're sort of thinking out loud, English wraps **I'll** in a soft little phrase: **I think I'll ...** or **I don't think I'll ...**

- I'm tired. **I think I'll go** to bed early tonight.
- It's a nice day. **I think I'll sit** outside.
- It's raining. **I don't think I'll go** out.

<SayIt text="I'm tired. I think I'll go to bed early." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="It's raining. I don't think I'll go out." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't say 'I phone you tomorrow'">
  Watch out for the present-simple trap. The present simple is for habits and things that are *always* true — not for a decision you just made.<br />
  ✅ I**'ll phone** you tomorrow, OK? &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I phone you tomorrow.<br />
  ✅ I think I**'ll go** to bed early. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I think I go to bed early.<br />
  ✅ I don't think I**'ll go** out. &nbsp;&nbsp; ❌ I don't think I go out.
</Callout>

## B. The big trap: I'll vs I'm doing / I'm going to

This is where most learners slip. **I'll** only fits decisions made **right now**. If you arranged something **before this conversation** — yesterday, last week, this morning — the plan already exists, so we use **I'm doing** (present continuous) or **I'm going to** instead.

| ✅ already arranged → 'm doing / 'm going to | ❌ I'll ... |
|---|---|
| **I'm working** tomorrow. *(the rota was set days ago)* | I'll work tomorrow. |
| **What are you doing** at the weekend? | What will you do at the weekend? |
| I don't want my car any more. **I'm going to sell** it. *(decided last week)* | I'll sell it. |

<Callout type="info" title="One tiny test">
  Before you choose, ask yourself: *"Did I decide this **before** this conversation, or am I deciding it **right now**?"* <br />
  → **Before** → **I'm doing** or **I'm going to** <br />
  → **Right now** → **I'll**
</Callout>

## C. Shall I ...? Shall we ...?

When you want to ask **"Do you think this is a good idea?"** or **"What do you think I should do?"**, English uses **Shall I ...?** or **Shall we ...?** — never *Will I...?*

<div className="not-prose my-6 rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 text-center shadow-sm">
  <p className="text-sm uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">the formula</p>
  <p className="mt-2 text-xl font-bold text-rose-900">
    <span className="rounded-md bg-white px-2 py-0.5">Shall</span>
    <span className="mx-2">+</span>
    <span className="rounded-md bg-white px-2 py-0.5">I / we</span>
    <span className="mx-2">+</span>
    <span className="rounded-md bg-white px-2 py-0.5">verb</span>
    <span className="mx-2">+</span>
    <span className="rounded-md bg-white px-2 py-0.5">... ?</span>
  </p>
  <p className="mt-3 text-sm italic text-rose-800">= Is this a good idea? What do you think?</p>
</div>

### Shall I ...? — checking *me*

You're offering or asking permission, and you want the other person's opinion before you act:

- It's very warm in this room. **Shall I open** the window?
- "**Shall I phone** you this evening?" "Yes, please."
- I'm going to a party tonight. What **shall I wear**?

<SayIt text="It's very warm. Shall I open the window?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Shall I phone you this evening?" />

### Shall we ...? — checking *us*

You're suggesting something the two of you could do together:

- It's a nice day. **Shall we go** for a walk?
- Where **shall we go** for our holidays this year?
- "Let's go out this evening." "OK, what time **shall we meet**?"

<SayIt text="It's a nice day. Shall we go for a walk?" /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="What time shall we meet?" />

<Callout type="tip" title="Shall + question word">
  Just like other questions, you can put a **wh-word** in front: **What** shall I wear? **Where** shall we go? **What time** shall we meet? **Who** shall we invite? The pattern stays the same — *wh-word + shall + I/we + verb*.
</Callout>

## I think I'll / I don't think I'll

For each situation, say one fresh decision out loud — start with **I think I'll** or **I don't think I'll**.

1. **It's cold today.** → *e.g.* "**I don't think I'll go** out."
2. **I'm hungry.** → *e.g.* "**I think I'll have** something to eat."
3. **I feel very tired.** → "**I don't think I'll play** tennis."
4. **I like this hat.** → "**I think I'll buy** it."
5. **This camera is too expensive.** → "**I don't think I'll buy** it."

<Callout type="tip" title="Listen to the contraction">
  In real speech, English almost never says the long *I will*. It squashes into **I'll** — one syllable. Practice: "I'll go", "I'll buy", "I'll phone", "I'll carry". The smaller the word feels, the more natural it sounds.
</Callout>
