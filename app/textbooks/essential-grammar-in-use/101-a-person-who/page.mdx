Two short sentences, joined into one — that's a **relative clause**.

> I met a woman. **She** can speak six languages.<br />
> → I met **a woman who** can speak six languages.

The trick is swapping the second subject (*she / it*) for a special joining word — **who**, **that**, or **which**. Today's mission: pick the right one.

## Two scenes

A woman who can do something amazing, and a hat that does the wrong thing. Same recipe — two sentences turning into one.

<Callout type="tip" title="The swap rule">
  Take the second sentence. Find its subject (*she, he, it, they*) — the word that **points back** to a noun in the first sentence. Swap it for the right joining word and stick the two sentences together. <br />
  *He saw a film. **It** was funny.* &nbsp;→&nbsp; *He saw a film **which** was funny.*
</Callout>

## Part A — *who* is for **people**

When the noun is a person, use **who**. (You can also use *that* — but *who* is the everyday choice.)

| First sentence + second sentence | Joined |
|---|---|
| A thief is a person. **He/she** steals things. | A thief is **a person who** steals things. |
| Do you know anybody? **He/she** can play the piano. | Do you know **anybody who** can play the piano? |
| The man phoned. **He** didn't give his name. | **The man who** phoned didn't give his name. |
| The people work in the office. **They** are very friendly. | **The people who** work in the office are very friendly. |

<SayIt text="A thief is a person who steals things." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="The man who phoned didn't give his name." />

## Part B — *which* is for **things**

When the noun is a thing, use **which**. (You can also use *that* — both are correct.)

- An airplane is **a machine which flies**.
- Emma lives in **a house which is 400 years old**.
- Where is the cheese **which was in the fridge**?

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't use *which* for people">
  ❌ *Do you remember the woman **which** was playing the piano?* <br />
  ✅ *Do you remember the woman **who** was playing the piano?* <br />
  *Which* is for things only. People take **who**.
</Callout>

## Part C — *that* works for both

**that** is a friendly all-rounder — it works for **people** *or* **things**.

| With *who/which* | With *that* |
|---|---|
| The people **who** work in the office are friendly. | The people **that** work in the office are friendly. |
| An airplane is a machine **which** flies. | An airplane is a machine **that** flies. |
| I know somebody **who** can help. | I know somebody **that** can help. |

For people, *who* is more usual — but *that* isn't wrong.

## Now you try

Finish each sentence about your own life. Use **who** or **which / that**.

1. *I have a friend **who** ___ .*
2. *I don't like people **who** ___ .*
3. *My phone is something **that** ___ .*
4. *A teacher is a person **who** ___ .*

<Callout type="tip" title="One-sentence summary">
  **People → who.** &nbsp; **Things → which.** &nbsp; **Either → that.** <br />
  Find the second subject (*he / she / it / they*), swap it for the right joining word, and the two sentences click together as one.
</Callout>
