When two people talk, English does something neat: the second speaker doesn't repeat the whole sentence. Instead, they keep just the **little helping verb** — *am*, *is*, *do*, *can*, *will*, *has* — and let it stand for everything else. It's the **echo trick**, and once you hear it you'll spot it everywhere.

## The big idea: keep the helper, drop the rest

When you reply, you don't have to repeat the verb. Just keep the **helper** — and English fills in the blank for free.

    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">the long way</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">She isn't tired, but <span className="line-through text-slate-400">he is tired</span>.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-700">He likes tea, but <span className="line-through text-slate-400">she doesn't like tea</span>.</p>

    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">the short way</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">She isn't tired, but <strong>he is</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-3 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">He likes tea, but <strong>she doesn't</strong>.</p>
  </div>

<Callout type="info" title="Why the echo works">
  English only needs the helping verb to carry the meaning. *He is* already means *he is tired* in this context. Repeating *tired* would feel clunky — like saying the same word twice in a row.
</Callout>

Whatever helper the first sentence used, the reply uses the **same family**. Here are the eight you'll meet over and over:

## Rule 2 — full form on its own (no `'m`, `'s`, `'ve`!)

When the helper sits **alone** at the end, you can't squash it into a contraction. Say the **full word**.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-emerald-200 bg-emerald-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-emerald-700">✅ full form</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">She isn't tired, but he <strong>is</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Yes, I <strong>am</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-emerald-900">Yes, I <strong>have</strong>.</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-rose-200 bg-rose-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-rose-700">❌ short form</p>
    <p className="text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">She isn't tired, but <span className="line-through">he's</span>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">Yes, <span className="line-through">I'm</span>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-base font-semibold text-rose-900">Yes, <span className="line-through">I've</span>.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="warning" title="Why the squash breaks">
  A short form like *he's* needs to **lean on the next word**. Take the next word away, and the contraction has nothing to lean on — it falls over. Say the full *is*, *am*, *have* and it sounds natural.
</Callout>

## Rule 3 — but negative contractions are fine

Here's the twist: **negative** short forms (*isn't, haven't, won't, didn't…*) **are OK** on their own. They're sturdy enough to stand by themselves.

- My sister has got a car, but I **haven't**. <SayIt text="My sister has got a car, but I haven't." iconOnly />
- 'Are you and Jane working tomorrow?' &nbsp; 'I am, but Jane **isn't**.' <SayIt text="I am, but Jane isn't." iconOnly />
- 'Will you be at the party?' &nbsp; 'No, I **won't**.' <SayIt text="No, I won't." iconOnly />

<Callout type="info" title="Quick rule of thumb">
  Positive on its own → **full form** (*am, is, have, will…*). <br />
  Negative on its own → **contraction is fine** (*isn't, haven't, won't, didn't…*). <br />
  Just don't end a reply on *'m*, *'s*, *'re*, *'ve*, *'ll*.
</Callout>

## After Yes and No

The same trick lives behind every short answer. After **Yes** or **No**, drop the helper that matches the question.

| question | yes | no |
|---|---|---|
| Are you tired? | Yes, I **am**. | No, I'**m not**. |
| Will Alan be here tomorrow? | Yes, he **will**. | No, he **won't**. |
| Is there a bus to the airport? | Yes, there **is**. | No, there **isn't**. |
| Have you got a minute? | Yes, I **have**. | No, I **haven't**. |
| Can you swim? | Yes, I **can**. | No, I **can't**. |

<SayIt text="Yes, I am." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="No, I'm not." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Yes, he will." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="No, he won't." />

<Callout type="tip" title="Mirror the question">
  Look at the **first word** of the question. *Are…?* → reply with *am / are*. *Will…?* → reply with *will / won't*. *Have…?* → reply with *have / haven't*. The helper just bounces back.
</Callout>

## do / does / did — the "invisible" helper

Present simple and past simple don't show their helper in the *first* sentence. But the moment you reply, **do / does / did** pops up to do the echo.

<div className="not-prose my-6 grid gap-3 sm:grid-cols-2">
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-amber-50 to-orange-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-700">present simple → do / does</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">I don't like hot weather, but Sue <strong>does</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">Sue works hard, but I <strong>don't</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">'Do you enjoy your work?' 'Yes, I <strong>do</strong>.'</p>
  </div>
  <div className="rounded-2xl border-2 border-violet-200 bg-gradient-to-br from-violet-50 to-purple-50 p-5 shadow-sm">
    <p className="mb-2 text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-violet-700">past simple → did</p>
    <p className="text-sm text-slate-700">A: Did you and Chris enjoy the film? <br/> B: I <strong>did</strong>, but Chris <strong>didn't</strong>.</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">'I had a good time.' 'I <strong>did</strong> too.'</p>
    <p className="mt-2 text-sm text-slate-700">'Did it rain yesterday?' 'No, it <strong>didn't</strong>.'</p>
  </div>
</div>

<SayIt text="I don't like hot weather, but Sue does." /> &nbsp; <SayIt text="Did it rain yesterday? No, it didn't." />

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't repeat the verb">
  ✅ Sue likes hot weather, but I **don't**. <br />
  ❌ Sue likes hot weather, but I don't like.<br /><br />
  Once *do/does/did* shows up, the original verb (*like, work, enjoy*) is already understood. Stop there.
</Callout>
