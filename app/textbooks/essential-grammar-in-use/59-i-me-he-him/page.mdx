Pronouns travel in pairs: *I/me, he/him, she/her, we/us, they/them*. The first one of each pair is the **subject** — the doer in front of the verb. The second is the **object** — the receiver after the verb, and after every preposition. Pick the wrong one and the sentence collapses (*Tom knows I*?). Pick the right one and nobody even notices you said it.

## A door full of pronouns

Mia and Lukas keep mixing up their pronouns. Watch the **first word** (the doer) versus what comes **after the verb or preposition** (the receiver).

<Callout type="tip" title="Two columns, one job each">
  *I, we, you, he, she, they, it* sit **before the verb** — they do the action. *me, us, you, him, her, them, it* sit **after the verb** or **after a preposition** — they receive it. Get the column right and the sentence sounds native.
</Callout>

### Subject side — *I / we / you / he / she / they* + know Tom

- **I** know Tom.
- **We** know Tom.
- **You** know Tom.
- **He** knows Tom. *(don't forget the -s)*
- **She** knows Tom.
- **They** know Tom.

### Object side — Tom knows *me / us / you / him / her / them*

- Tom knows **me**.
- Tom knows **us**.
- Tom knows **you**.
- Tom knows **him**.
- Tom knows **her**.
- Tom knows **them**.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch the trap">
  ❌ *Tom knows I.*&nbsp;&nbsp;❌ *Me know Tom.* <br />
  ✅ Tom knows **me**.&nbsp;&nbsp;✅ **I** know Tom. <br />
  The verb (*knows*) tells you what side of itself the pronoun is on.
</Callout>

## Part B — Things: *it* and *they/them*

## Part C — After a preposition → object form

Here's the rule that catches everyone: **after every preposition** (*for, to, with, at, on, about*…), use the **object** pronoun. Always.

<div className="not-prose my-6 overflow-hidden rounded-2xl border-2 border-amber-200 bg-amber-50">
  <div className="bg-amber-200 px-4 py-2 text-center text-xs font-bold uppercase tracking-wider text-amber-900">preposition + object</div>
  <div className="grid gap-px bg-amber-200 sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-4">
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">for me</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">at her</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">with us</p></div>
    <div className="bg-white p-3 text-center"><p className="font-semibold text-amber-900">with them</p></div>
  </div>
</div>

- This letter isn't **for me**. It's **for you**.
- Who is that woman? Why are you looking **at her**?
- We're going to the cinema. Do you want to come **with us**?
- Sue and Kevin are going to the cinema. Do you want to go **with them**?
- 'Where's the newspaper?' 'You're sitting **on it**.'

### Special pattern — *give it / them to ...*

When the *thing* is a pronoun (*it / them*), it goes **first**, then *to + person*.

## Now you try

Write **six** true sentences about people you know, using a different pronoun each time. Mix subject and object.

1. *My best friend is … . **He / She** …*
2. *I see … every week. I really like **him / her**.*
3. *My parents … . I always talk to **them** about …*
4. *We sometimes … . **It** is fun.*
5. *That bag is heavy. Can you carry **it** for **me**?*
6. *My neighbours have a dog. **They** love **it**.*

<Callout type="tip" title="Star challenge — never *I* after a preposition">
  *with my friend and I* sounds polite — but it's wrong. After **with / for / to / about / between** etc., always use **me**: *with my friend and **me***. Read your six sentences and check every preposition: is the pronoun in the right column?
</Callout>
